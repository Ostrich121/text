Digital sovereignty, geopolitical imaginaries, and the reproduction of European identity Linda Monsees a and Daniel Lambach b

ABSTRACT “Digital sovereignty” has emerged as a hot topic in European politics. But although true European digital sovereignty seems unattainable, analysing the digital sovereignty discourse is still useful since it tells us much about European politics. We examine three “projects” which are part of the broader digital sovereignty initiative: 5G, Gaia-X, and the semiconductor industry. This empirical perspective allows for a better understanding of how imaginaries about digital sovereignty play out in these speciﬁc tech projects and how these then help to aﬃrm a particular European identity. Methodologically, we focus on how particular geopolitical imaginaries appear in these digital sovereignty projects.

Our empirical analysis reveals that Europe’s comparatively weak digital industries are considered a security issue. China and, to a lesser degree, the United States are not only seen as economic rivals but also security threats when it comes to issues such as espionage and data protection. Based on this, we argue that digital sovereignty projects, despite being full of contradictions and tensions, contribute to a distinct EU identity of an agile, future-oriented global player in the digitised economy. This, while not entirely new, is a powerful imaginary even if the proposed idea of “sovereignty” might never be enacted.

Digital sovereignty is a geopolitical issue. As the rivalry between the United States (US) and China escalates, digital technologies and the capability to compete in relevant industries are becoming an ever more important marker of international status and security. The claims about digital sovereignty (DS) are not merely cheap talk but guide concrete policies in the EU. Indeed, DS is used to legitimise speciﬁc infrastructure and economic projects. Through the analysis of three key DS projects, this paper argues that DS is used to construct a European identity around the idea that the EU is (or will become) a core player in the global geostrategic competition and the digital economy. By contrasting European ideals and practices with those of China and the US, the EU portrays itself as a responsible actor that is serving its citizens and protecting their data.1

In their introduction to this special issue, the editors argue that “digital sovereignty is presented as the way forward to developing the EU as a secure and resilient society, to achieving a leadership position within the international system, and reducing its dependence on other parts of the world” (see Bellanova et al., 2022). Digital sovereignty as a concept highlights the need for technological capacity, broadly understood, not only for economic but also security purposes. For example, 5G technology is considered to be central for the future digital society due to the expected improvements to communication, health, and the economy. However, it is also perceived as a highly vulnerable technology allowing for new kinds of cyberattacks. The term DS as such has a normative dimension where the focal point lies on “self-determination and independent decisionmaking in the digital space” (Sahin and Barker 2021, p. 11). Previous work on digital sovereignty was mainly interested in policy-oriented (Christakis 2020, Kar and Thapa 2020) or more theoretical clariﬁcations of the term itself (Pohle and Thiel 2019, Floridi 2020). Other works have analysed the reasons for the emergence of DS on the political scene (Lambach and Oppermann 2022), identifying “a widespread perception that Europe had been disadvantaged by large US tech companies” (Burwell and Propp 2020, p. 4).2 We expand this literature empirically by analysing three “projects”, i.e. initiatives mentioned in DS documents and speeches that are legitimised by referencing DS explicitly: Gaia-X, 5G, and semiconductors. Even though these projects are all central to the digital sovereignty agenda, they diﬀer in their aims, origins, and actor constellations. Gaia-X was initiated by the German and French governments and is now a European project to develop an independent cloud computing system aimed at providing better privacy and security. 5G is considered a key technology for social and economic development, especially through the Internet of Things. The development of 5G and its many applications is directly promoted by the EU but relies heavily on public-private partnerships even as it has become contested over the role of Huawei and China. The semiconductor industry is a crucial backbone of the digital economy. During the Covid19 pandemic, the reliance on semiconductor “chips” has become even more visible. Together, examining these three projects allows us to see how geopolitical imaginations play out in speciﬁc economic policies initiated by the EU.

Conceptually, we expand on previous work by focusing on the prevailing geopolitical imaginaries that inform DS strategies and how these impact processes of identity formation. That is not to say that prior research has nothing to say on geopolitics – to the contrary. Many publications agree that “much of the digital sovereignty rationale is driven by the current geopolitical climate” (Komaitis 2021, p. 13, also Christakis 2020, Sahin and Barker 2021). While several countries play a role in speciﬁc debates, the US and China are the main actors against which Europe formulates its agenda of digital sovereignty and thereby its identity. Thus, we follow a similar approach to Barrinha and Christou (2022) who highlight the performative eﬀects of claiming sovereignty. In terms of European geopolitics and identity, it is these countries that are the main players in the development and implementation of digital technology, but they are also seen as economic rivals as well as potential threats to European data protection standards. By examining the geopolitical imaginaries informing the various strands of the DS discourse, we can reconstruct how diﬀerent assumptions, patterns of justiﬁcation and threat-images contribute to a distinct idea of the EU as a digital global player. Crucially,

this happens despite (or maybe because of?) the many contradictions, ambivalences, and resistances within DS practices.

To situate our study better, we discuss in the next section the role of the EU within global tech politics and present our analytical framework that focuses on geopolitical imaginaries and how these imaginaries contribute to the construction of a distinct EU identity. In the main section, we present our three cases and their dominant geopolitical imaginaries. This will pave the way to understand “digital sovereignty” not only as EU policy but to construct an identity of the EU as a global digital player.

Global tech politics and the EU Over the past decades, digital technologies have become an integral part of economy and society, which has been described with terms such as “platform economy” or the “internet-industrial complex” (Flyverbom et al. 2017). This feeds into the notion that digital sovereignty is not primarily about military security but about economic strength and digital security, including critical infrastructure and data protection. American tech companies such as Google, Apple, Amazon, or Intel as well as their Chinese counterparts (Alibaba, Tencent, Huawei, etc.) are often quasi-monopolistic providers of high-tech products and digital services (Beaumier et al. 2020, Hathaway 2020). There are only a few companies worldwide that can provide 5G equipment, produce high-end semiconductors, or oﬀer cloud computing capacities. Network eﬀects and economies of scale mean that markets tend to be dominated by very small numbers of companies. Since these companies oﬀer technologies that customers around the world use everyday, they “enjoy a direct, indeed virtually unmediated, link to their users, most of whom connect to these ﬁrms through devices they carry in their pockets every day” (Culpepper and Thelen 2020, p. 294, emph. removed). As a result, global tech companies are not only core for European economies but also at the centre of many societal debates concerning, for instance, privacy, data protection, media freedom and thus democratic stability. Attempts to control and regulate the big platform companies increased in the wake of the Snowden revelations and ultimately led to the idea of European technological sovereignty (Komaitis 2021, p. 12). The publicisation of the collusion between big tech and intelligence services led users to lose trust in tech companies and their commitment to the protection of user data (Steiger et al. 2017). “Digital sovereignty” builds in part on this sentiment of suspicion towards foreign big tech companies. As we will show below, the relationship between the EU and foreign big tech companies is ambiguous and characterised by multiple levels of dependency (see also Farrand and Carrapico, 2022). In short, global tech companies are not only crucial for economic politics but also embedded in contemporary debates around values such as privacy or human rights. Furthermore, as this article demonstrates, these socio-economic issues are fed by and sustain existing geopolitical rivalries. The notion that the EU can achieve “strategic autonomy” through technological capacity concerns economic, security and value dimensions, which are entangled with each other.3 However, we do not wish to imply that this discussion is somehow caused by technological breakthroughs. Rather, we agree that “ostensibly unprecedented concerns are in fact reheated versions of narratives that date back decades if not centuries” (Eberle and Daniel 2022, p. 8). Geopolitical debates that seemingly have a strong technological focus are usually less about the novel

The EU as a technology and innovation project is, however, not novel at all. In many aspects, current DS debates echo earlier discussions going back to the founding of the European Union. Historians of technology (see Misa and Schot 2005 and the other contributions to the special issue) point out how technological infrastructures were instrumental in European integration before the European Communities, and the capability to develop high technology in Europe has always been a prominent justiﬁcation of European cooperation, going as far back as EURATOM, one of the founding treaties of the European Communities (Barry and Walters 2003). Technology, research, and innovation policies were seen as important for economic competitiveness and European integration (Banchoﬀ2002, Stajano 2009, pp. 159–188). There were always perceptions of a “technology gap”, ﬁrst in comparison to the United States in the 1950s and 1960s, and then towards Japan and the US in the 1980s and into the 1990s (Peterson and Sharp 1998, pp. 4–9). These “gap” discussions were frequently portrayed in explicitly political terms (e.g. Servan-Schreiber 1967) although, at least to us, they were only rarely put in such starkly geopolitical terms as the one that surrounds current DS debates. This debate on technology needs to be understood in a broader context of the “geopoliticisation” of the European Union, which has begun to acknowledge and openly discuss its geopolitics over the past two decades (e.g. Kuus 2007, Bialasiewicz 2011). This is also an identity issue: Over the 1990s, European identity formation shifted from othering Europe’s past to geographic and cultural otherings (Diez 2004). Guzzini (2012) and the various contributions to this edited volume argue that the return of geopolitics can be explained as a response to a crisis of foreign policy identities which were left unmoored in the post-Cold War period and latched onto geopolitical imaginaries to make sense of the new European security landscape. These developments come together in recent discussions about “strategic autonomy” and “technological sovereignty”, among others, which have also informed DS discourses (Crespi et al. 2021). This paper examines speciﬁc DS tech projects in this wider context of EU identity and geopolitics. We argue that the link between technology and geopolitics is drawn more explicitly than before. In the past, technological capability was more clearly framed as a question of economic competitiveness, whereas it is now also a factor in global power politics. In this sense, portrayals of a digitally sovereign European Union do not represent a break with, but rather a continuation of the past, with a few aspects that are more strongly highlighted than they used to be.

To get at the question of identity in a geopolitical context, we use the concept of the “imaginary” as the lens through which we explore the “collectively held and performed visions of desirable futures” (Jasanoﬀ2015, p. 19) of European digital policy. Here we focus speciﬁcally on how these imaginaries are embedded in geopolitics and examine how they play out in diﬀerent DS projects. Crucially, geopolitical imaginaries provide images, visions and roles through which the Self and Others are constituted (Mattern 2005). Identities, while enacted in practical relations, are informed by imaginaries but they are also actively constructed, both through their everyday enactment but also

Page 6

through elite cues. Our focus in this paper is on how European institutions and elites use DS discourses and geopolitical imaginaries to construct an identity of the European Union. This follows Kaina and Karolewski who discuss “identity technologies” (2013, pp. 33–39) which the EU deploys towards citizens in order to construct a collective European identity. Among the technologies mentioned, the manipulation of cultural symbols, the use of quasi-national rituals, the creation of foundational myths, and the promotion of positive self-images all rely on a shared imaginary of what it means to be European in the world. This is comparable to concepts of “nation branding”, whereby states engage in public diplomacy to create an image that they wish to transmit internationally (Manor and Segev 2015).4 In our empirical analysis, we examine how DS is used to reproduce this European identity. Analytically, we look at self-images and representations of threats in the geopolitical imaginaries surrounding DS discourse. For example, we do not ask whether China “really” is a threat but how a threatening image of China is perpetuated in debates on digital sovereignty. This aligns with similar approaches in European security studies, such as Christou et al.’s plea to “explore how EU security practices across a broad array of issue areas and through variegated processes and contexts are constructed, contested, and operationalised in terms of governance/governmentality, change and outcome” (Christou et al. 2010, p. 342). We posit that our approach is a valuable addition to existing approaches on European security since it understands geopolitical threat imaginaries as not only the cause but also the result of politics and ties them to debates about European identity. How we imagine the geopolitical world shapes how we relate to it and act within it, and we enact our imaginations through our practices and discourses. Our cases also demonstrate, on the one hand, how imaginaries shape DS discourses and projects. They show that even when projects are unlikely to achieve their stated substantive aims, they still “work” by aﬃrming a distinct EU identity as a geopolitical and geoeconomic actor.

Analysis Methodologically, we conducted a qualitative analysis of all three cases. The cases we chose were selected for several reasons. First, all three are suﬃciently concrete and advanced initiatives. Second, the EU has a diﬀerent role in each case. While the EU is leading the Gaia-X project, in 5G, it is mainly a facilitator among member states’ governments. In the semiconductor industry, the EU is mainly providing subsidies and institutional support through private sector partnerships. Third, patterns of competition and cooperation vary between cases. In the Gaia-X case, the European project is mainly positioned as an alternative to commercial oﬀers by major US corporations, while for 5G, the main competitors (or threats) are from China. Semiconductors are a curious blend of competition and collaboration due to the highly integrated supply chains of this technology. Hence, the three cases oﬀer substantial variation in terms of publicprivate cooperation and EU relations to other actors, promising a somewhat representative view across the spectrum of DS activities.

The material includes diﬀerent perspectives, especially from EU bodies, national governments, think tanks, and industry groups. Think tanks played a special role in concretising DS discourse. While EU documents used cautious diplomatic language, think tanks

oﬀered more assertive interpretations of the rationales and intentions behind digital sovereignty. We are aware that each case could be discussed in greater depth, but our aim here was distinctly comparative – by looking at multiple projects, we gain a better sense of the commonalities and diﬀerences across cases (Jackson 2011, ch. 5). As already mentioned in the introduction we conducted a textual analysis but focus on the practices of DS as they materialise in these diﬀerent projects (Bueger and Gadinger 2015). This is important since previous works mainly focused on DS discourse without paying attention to how DS is enacted in practice. Naturally, the many contradictions within the cases and across actors cannot be summarised to their full extent in this paper. We focus on the main dynamics and illustrate how the debates around digital sovereignty create a distinct European identity. Our analysis was conducted in the spirit of interpretive research and involved a close reading followed by an analysis of the following guiding questions: What is the threat and who or what needs protection? How are other actors portrayed? What roles are prescribed for the EU? Findings from these discussions were then used for the inductive derivation of the results we summarise below.

Gaia-X Gaia-X is a project to create a European cloud computing infrastructure. Cloud computing refers to distributed data storage and on-demand computing power drawing on “the cloud”, a decentralised network of computers, and is seen as a key enabling technology for growth in an ever more digitised economy. The Fourth Industrial Revolution (“Industry 4.0”) is characterised by the introduction of smart technology into manufacturing and industrial processes, which will generate huge amounts of proprietary, industrial data: “It is estimated that, with the ongoing digital transformation, the global volume of data will be multiplied by ﬁve by 2025” (Pannier 2021, p. 1). Making the most of this opportunity means that companies and countries need reliable and secure cloud storage to protect their data and their trade secrets from theft and industrial espionage. The cloud market is currently dominated by three American companies, who have a joint market share of 66% in Europe: Amazon Web Services (AWS), Microsoft Azure, and Google Cloud (Pannier 2021, p. 1). The market dominance of these “hyperscalers” is a source of concern for European policymakers and businesses. Legal instruments like the 2018 CLOUD Act and the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act give US intelligence agencies widespread powers to monitor cloud-stored data and other internet traﬃc, and the Snowden revelations have demonstrated to Europeans the scale at which such competencies are used, underscoring the need for “European sovereignty” (Autolitano and Pawlowska 2021, pp. 4–5). With the failure of the International Safe Harbor Privacy Principles and the subsequent EU-US Privacy Shield framework – both were overturned by the European Court of Justice in 2015 and 2020, respectively – transatlantic data relationships have become a deeply contentious issue and one that hits close to the self-construction of a supposedly more ethical Europe that values the protection of citizen data.

In 2019–2020, Gaia-X was pitched as a cloud computing solution that adheres to “European values” and safeguards data protection standards. The aim was not to create a new cloud service to compete with the hyperscalers but to set up a federated infrastructure

Page 8

that creates interoperability among existing services. Based on a core of open-source software, Gaia-X was supposed to build a decentralised ecosystem of companies, with participation based on adherence to a set of common rules, e.g. in terms of cybersecurity standards, terms of service, and principles of data sovereignty.5 It has established national hubs as points of contact for interested parties. Germany and France have been the primary drivers of the Gaia-X project since its inception. Other European countries have joined the initiative and operations were transferred to a non-proﬁt corporation set up under Belgian law (Gaia-X AISBL) in September 2020. As of September 2021, over 300 companies and institutions have applied to join the association and provide use cases and services. Most of these are European but the big players of the cloud market – Amazon, Microsoft, Google as well as Chinese companies like Alibaba – have also signed on, most of them through their European subsidiaries. However, the participation of controversial non-European companies like Palantir has raised considerable doubt whether Gaia-X’s certiﬁcation scheme will be demanding enough (Pannier 2021, p. 3). In 2021–2022, reports emerged of discontent and disillusionment among participants, with some companies pursuing parallel projects or even leaving the consortium. The realities of the market currently seem to make Gaia-X a commercially unviable product. In addition, continued funding from the German government for the next project stages is in doubt.

Nevertheless, Gaia-X has been described by think tanks “as a ﬁrst step towards the concretisation of what the Union deﬁnes as ‘digital sovereignty’” (Autolitano and Pawlowska 2021, p. 3, see also Jorge-Ricart 2020). Its most obvious purpose is to protect European companies’ data, facilitate data integration along supply chains, and open new opportunities for value creation (BMWi 2019, Kagermann and Wilhelm 2020). In addition, Gaia-X aims at the diversiﬁcation of the cloud computing market and at expanding European regulatory power (European Union 2020, p. 3, Grüll and Stolton 2020). These economic aims are tightly bound up with geopolitical concerns, with many commentators highlighting Gaia-X’s strategic importance (Bolognini and Pelino 2020, Jorge-Ricart 2020, Autolitano and Pawlowska 2021). As a report by the Atlantic Council’s Scowcroft Center for Strategy and Security puts it: “Cloud computing is embedded in contemporary geopolitics; […] cloud computing is the canvas on which states conduct signiﬁcant political, security, and economic activity” (Herr 2020, p. 1). The major theme is one of European dependence “on the US and Chinese IT giants” (Autolitano and Pawlowska 2021, p. 14) and “on infrastructures made available by international providers” (Bolognini and Pelino 2020, p. 3, emph. added). This raises concerns about cybersecurity and is also viewed “as a serious threat to a state’s sovereignty” (Pannier 2021, p. 2), with some French commentators going so far as to call for the “digital decolonization” (Gomart et al. 2018, p. 35) of Europe.

The collective imaginary here is one of European dependence. This is further fuelled by real fears about being drawn into the conﬂict between the two “digital ﬁefs” (Jorge-Ricart 2020), China and the United States. In particular, the main threat to be fended oﬀare USbased companies whose Silicon Valley ethos is seen as incompatible with European regulatory standards. In addition, there is a real worry that Europe is missing out on the industrial data revolution. Thierry Breton, the European Commissioner for Internal Market and Services, “declared that the war for industrial data begins now and Europe will be the main battleﬁeld” (Braud et al. 2021, p. 4). It might be tempting to put these concerns

European actors consistently argue for Gaia-X in terms like those expressed by French Finance Minister Bruno Le Maire: “We are not China. We are not the United States. We are European countries with our own values” (Grüll and Stolton 2020). But which values are these? They may include high-minded ideals like openness, diversity, and pluralism (BMWi 2019, p. 7), political principles like sovereignty and transparency (BMWi 2019,

Despite their fuzziness, the emphasis on values is itself noteworthy because it signals an attempt to create a “European brand” of cloud computing.6 This might be an economic proposition, implying that US providers are less secure and less trustworthy (Herr 2020, p. 18) but it is also indicative of a wider loss of trust. In a technical sense, trust refers to data integrity, but the political overtones of the repeated use of this term are impossible to overlook. To some degree, the entire Gaia-X project is driven by a loss of trust in the United States. Furthermore, US platform companies are widely seen as unethical hyper-capitalists whose lack of respect for European rules and regulations are often interpreted through a cultural lens. All this plays into a narrative that a “European way of life” – whatever that may be – is under threat if Europe continues to be dependent on US cloud services.

5G 5G stands for the ﬁfth-generation technology standard for mobile telecommunications, which will improve upon existing 4G (LTE) technology in several ways: faster download speeds (by a factor of 10), greatly reduced latency, and a higher network density allowing for a larger number of users. Whereas 4G networks consist of networks of towers, 5G will use a broader range of cellular technologies, including smaller cells that transmit on very high, shorter-range frequencies. So even though 5G and LTE standards are partly compatible, the use of these higher frequencies requires the installation of completely new infrastructure, especially in urban areas with the densest clusters of users. Given the expected growth of industrial and private data transmission (see Gaia-X section), many actors and commentators frame 5G as a key technology enabling social and economic connectivity and the widespread deployment of “smart” devices in homes, factories, and the workplace. The EU’s NIS Cooperation Group argues that “5G networks will play a central role in achieving the digital transformation of the EU’s economy and society” (NIS Cooperation Group 2019, p. 3). Hence, the security of 5G networks will be “crucial for national security, economic security and other national interests and global stability” (Government of the Czech Republic 2019).

The political stakes involved became especially visible during the Huawei controversy. As European countries began plans to upgrade cell networks, the question emerged of

Page 10

who should supply the necessary technology. The market for 5G technology is currently dominated by ﬁve companies: Huawei, ZTE (both Chinese), Nokia (Finnish), Ericsson (Swedish), and Samsung (South Korean), of which Huawei would be the cheapest and most technically eﬃcient supplier. Several countries raised concerns over the inclusion of Chinese-made technology into European mobile data networks over fears of surveillance and espionage. On the surface, this is not dissimilar to European worries about US dominance in cloud computing and the resulting data protection issues. However, fears about Chinese 5G hardware went beyond “mere” surveillance to also include sabotage scenarios (e.g. Skierka 2020, p. 2). The controversy was further stoked by the US government lobbying its European counterparts “to follow its lead in excluding Huawei from participating in 5G upgrades” (Burwell and Propp 2020, p. 7, see also Shapiro 2020, Steiner and Grzymek 2020). However, not all European countries agreed with the Trump Administration’s hard line against China. The German government and others “were reluctant to move decisively against companies that had been important partners for years and were a key part of their 3G and 4G systems” (Oertel 2020, p. 24).

This politicisation of vendor choice is noteworthy since the technical or economic merits of the various companies’ oﬀers are rarely mentioned. Instead, the debates have been consistently about geopolitical questions. While 5G also has military applications, most concerns addressed risks of industrial espionage and sabotage by Chinese intelligence agencies. For the EU and European countries, 5G has become a linchpin issue for the development of their strategic autonomy (European Commission 2019). For instance, the Commission adopted a recommendation for a common EU approach to the security of 5G networks in March 2019 and published an EU toolbox on 5G cybersecurity in January 2020. The extent to which EU Member States succeed in developing a common approach to the 5G security question is seen as an important test for the strategic autonomy of the EU in the digital environment. (Madiega 2020, p. 4) The toolbox refers to “the technological sovereignty of the Union” (NIS Cooperation Group 2020, p. 3), while ENISA highlights “the importance of 5G to the sovereignty of nation-states” (ENISA 2019, p. 72). Many European states have hardened their stance against involving Huawei – or “non-EU state or state-backed actors” (NIS Cooperation Group 2019, p. 32) in EU parlance.7 What we can see here is how security concerns reinforce existing stances against China.

Through this process of intense politicisation, 5G has become an issue of geopolitics and “geoeconomics” (Cartwright 2020, p. 2). The EU sees itself as being “caught in the crossﬁre” (Oertel 2020, p. 24). In contrast to Gaia-X, however, China is clearly presented as the main rival and threat when it comes to future security and privacy concerns. A notable theme running through this discussion is the complete identiﬁcation of private companies with the countries they represent, like the way the “hyperscalers” are associated with the US in the Gaia-X case. The argument supporting this quasi-mercantilism is that Chinese companies are governed by laws requiring them to cooperate with state security organs, including for acts of espionage and sabotage of critical infrastructures abroad (Skierka 2020, p. 2). Notably, this argument is only being deployed against Chinese companies, not the South Korean technology giant Samsung, while Nokia and Ericsson are consistently portrayed as safe “European” options. The 5G debate is two-faced. On the one hand, 5G is presented as crucial for a futureoriented Europe that provides the best infrastructure for its citizens, can compete in the digital economy, and provides citizens with better services in health, mobility, or safety. On the other, 5G also represents a vulnerability to external threats, whether in military terms or as economic espionage and privacy invasions. The entire 5G debate is thus less about 5G as a technology but a negotiation of EU–China (and partly EU–US) relations in the context of a tense geopolitical environment. 5G also serves as a tool to sharpen a European identity through contrasting it with an image of China as the Other. It thus feeds into and reproduces a particular geopolitical imaginary in which the main threat comes from China targeting infrastructure, the economy and European citizens.

Semiconductors Semiconductors are materials that can be used to amplify electronic signals. At current levels of technology, semiconductors are as small as 2–10 nm and billions of them are used on one single “chip” (Ezell 2020, p. 5). Semiconductors are essential in the production of everyday electric goods such as cars and phones but also computers and all kinds of digital devices. The production of semiconductors is highly complex and involves a specialised global value chain. It is said that a single semiconductor might cross international borders around 70 times before making it to its end product (Alam et al. 2020,

p. 6). The economy’s reliance on semiconductors was exempliﬁed during the Covid-19 crisis when the production of semiconductors was hampered by disruptions to global production chains. This had a huge impact on manufacturers around the world leading to production delays especially in the car industry (Miller et al. 2021). We are thus talking about a highly globalised, interconnected but also strategically important industry. It is thus no wonder that semiconductors are a central battleground when it comes to US–China (economic) competition (Ezell 2020, Goodkind 2021, Hongpei 2021). It is widely acknowledged that having a strong semiconductor industry is a precondition for economic competitiveness in the digital economy. Currently, all major players heavily subsidise their semiconductor industry. However, the EU’s market share has dropped over the past two decades and currently lies below 10% (Kleinhans 2021, p. 6). It is in this context that the EU has identiﬁed semiconductors as an essential element of its digital sovereignty strategy (Kleinhans 2021, p. 6). To support this crucial industry, the EU Commission launched its “alliance for semiconductors and industrial cloud technologies” in July 2021, aimed at “[joining] forces to maintain and boost the competitiveness in the sector in the EU” (European Commission 2021a). Here, we can see how the EU presents itself as a core economic actor able to innovate and compete on a global level by drawing on existing imaginaries of a European identity.

Whereas European 5G eﬀorts aim at technological autonomy and the Gaia-X project is an attempt to impose European regulatory standards onto a chunk of the cloud computing market, self-reliance of any form is practically impossible in semiconductors. The EU aims at focusing on 2 nm chips even though its current market share in this highly advanced segment is zero. The EU produces only chips at the lower end of the quality

Page 12

spectrum, which leads experts to doubt the feasibility of these plans (Poitiers and Weil 2021). Even for the US and China, which have greater market shares and can produce more advanced semiconductors, gaining self-reliance is nearly impossible. Semiconductor production relies on a global and highly specialised value chain. Despite these widely acknowledged technological facts, claims about the need for selfreliance still abound. This drive for autonomy – despite being rather unrealistic – also aﬀects the depiction of China. China is presented as the core competitor and in part also a threat, even though it continues to import chips from the US. China is not even close to being autonomous and heavily subsidises its own semiconductor industry. Thus, while China is certainly seen as a main competitor in the production of semiconductors, its role is depicted in less stark security terminology than in the debate around 5G. The dominant theme is economic rivalry between China and the US. It is clearly framed as an economic competition and the idea of economic rivalry dominates over security frames. However, geopolitical concerns still matter. The EU Commission writes in the context of its Digital Compass that Global trade and investment policies are increasingly deﬁned by the US–China rivalry, particularly as relations between the two superpowers have become more transactional and confrontational. Economic interdependencies are now more often leveraged for geopolitical interests than used to be the case. (European Commission 2021b) The Commission here speaks openly about “geopolitical interests” of the “superpowers” in the context of economic policy. The role of China is depicted in even starker terms in this piece by a Brussels-based think tank: More recently, Chinese industrial strategy in the sector has been decidedly aggressive. A taskforce working with high-ranking government oﬃcials and industry leaders was given a budget of 1 trillion renminbi ($170 billion) to spend between 2014 and 2024 to create semiconductor national champions. (Poitiers and Weil 2021, p. 10) Here, Chinese behaviour is described as “aggressive”, aiming at the creation of “national champions”. But while the European debate about the semiconductor business continually refers to geopolitical tensions between the US and China, two aspects lead to a stronger emphasis on the economy and not security. First, semiconductors are essential products for all other digital technologies. In contrast to 5G they are not directly associated with (in-)security themselves but exist one step removed from such concerns. “Securitising” semiconductors is thus more complicated. Second, the simultaneous idea of Chinese domination but also a possibility of European self-reliance is a diﬃcult ﬁction to uphold. Even if plans by the EU to increase its market share in semiconductors worked out, this would still not lead to meaningful autonomy due to the transnationally integrated character of production chains. Consequently, the theme of interdependence is characteristic for this debate (Alam et al. 2020) and “autonomy” is less of an aim than “market dominance”.

Much like the 5G debate, an industrial policy for semiconductors is justiﬁed through narratives of the EU being caught in the US-China rivalry (Goodkind 2021, Poitiers and Weil 2021). From that, it follows that the EU must catch up and at least aim for a higher market share. For example, Commissioner Breton said in the context of starting the alliances for semiconductors and industrial cloud technologies:

… the alliance on semiconductors will rebalance global semiconductor supply chains by ensuring that we have the capacity to design and produce, in Europe, the most advanced chips towards 2 nm and below. (European Commission 2021c) To conclude, the debate around semiconductors is a highly technical one and mostly driven by economic concerns. Since semiconductors are a crucial but hidden component of digital technologies the debate is less concerned with public values such as privacy or data protection than, for example, the Gaia-X project. End-users and citizens are barely a concern. We can ﬁnd scattered remarks about climate change and the role semiconductors might play in a climate-friendly economy (Strategic Forum 2019, p. 4) but overall, the debate is driven by the (ﬁctional) idea of self-reliance and the (more realistic) goals of higher market shares. DS serves as a tool to cover up the inherent tensions within the semiconductor discourse. While, on a practical level, autonomy is unrealistic, the EU still justiﬁes its policies with the aim of self-reliance (see also Carrapico and Barrinha 2017). Despite the existing tensions, the discourse on semiconductors is able to reinforce the existing EU identity of an innovative economy that is forward-looking and trying to outrun, or at least catch up to its geopolitical rivals.

In sum, this empirical section has highlighted the similarities and diﬀerences between the three DS projects. We showed how the US and China are framed diﬀerently in relation to the EU in all projects. In the context of Gaia-X the US is a threat to European values and economic competitiveness but they do not appear as a central agent in the debate around 5G, where China is positioned as the main threat. In the debate on semiconductors, the US is partly a rival but also a possible partner in the ﬁght against Chinese competition. These relations must be considered in a broader context. The recent inauguration of the EU-US Trade and Technology Council may also herald a closer transatlantic relationship under the Biden Administration (Bendiek and Stürzer 2022). Hence, while the EU digital sovereignty project is trying to achieve “strategic autonomy”, that autonomy is primarily articulated in security terms vis-à-vis China. Even though these visions might not be technologically feasible, they continue to work and have an eﬀect on EU politics and the imagination of the EU as an agile, future-oriented tech hub.

Conclusion Digital sovereignty is not only a catch phrase for EU policies but is also put into practice through diﬀerent projects. This article has examined three of those projects highlighting how they rely on and solidify diﬀerent geopolitical imaginaries. Rising geopolitical tensions and the need to develop a strong digital economy are used to justify the idea of digital sovereignty. Economically, China and the US are both presented as rivals to some degree, with the EU being caught in the middle. Companies are identiﬁed with their home countries, evoking the prevailing spirit of “economic nationalism” (Helleiner 2021).

However, the representations are far from symmetrical. China is portrayed as a straightforward threat or at least as a very dangerous competitor in 5G and semiconductors. In the asymmetric portrayal of the US and China, EU elites refer to established geopolitical imaginaries that cast China as more of a security threat than the United States, which is

Page 14

presented more as an economic rival. Of course, this is a gross simpliﬁcation – some EU members, e.g. Germany or the members of the “16 + 1” (a Chinese cooperation format with Central and Eastern European countries), value their close economic relations with China and are more likely to use a rhetoric of partnership. In addition, the rivalry with the US was particularly acute during the Trump Administration, when transatlantic relations were at their worst. These events and perceptions, of an EU abandoned by America while being confronted with a more assertive China, clearly inﬂuence how digital sovereignty was framed in our case studies, much more than the speciﬁc role of the EU within them. Whether the EU was leading the eﬀort (GAIA-X), coordinating among member states (5G) or engaging in public-private cooperation (semiconductors) had little impact on how these projects were framed in DS terms. Instead, the unifying strand connecting the case studies is geopolitical.

In addition, we argued that digital sovereignty is used as a cipher for a European identity that is future-oriented, economically strong and acting in the interest of its citizens. Especially this idea of protecting its own citizens is used to distinguish a European identity from its Chinese (5G) and American (Gaia-X) foils. This is especially remarkable considering the contradictions and conﬂicts that shape the three diﬀerent DS projects. For one, technological factors prevent the achievement of digital sovereignty in a straightforward fashion. We saw this in the case study on the semiconductor industry, where autonomy is not feasible since no state has the technical capabilities to produce high-end semiconductors alone. Similarly, Gaia-X as an autonomous European cloud service is unrealistic considering the global infrastructure of data sharing. While initially planned as competing with US-based market leaders such as AWS, those companies are now active participants in the project. True “sovereignty” thus seems an unrealistic goal and this tension is not resolved by DS. This is, however, obscured by the projects feeding into an imaginary of a digitally sovereign EU. That means that DS works as an overarching symbol towards which EU policies might be oriented but which, most of all, represents a promise. While we showed how DS can contribute to the construction of a particular EU identity, we do not claim that this is entirely novel. Discourses about technological innovation and the need for stronger tech policies have existed since the 1950s. However, we do think that the DS projects which we analysed are more heavily politicised in geopolitical terms than previous iterations.

We can only oﬀer some conjectures on the practical impact of DS. It seems reasonable to expect that improving technological capacities in EU digital industries will be good for the economy. More competition and higher regulatory standards might also improve cloud computing services beyond the EU. But we are sceptical that the projects discussed here will be able to achieve these or other goals, as GAIA-X (in particular) and the semiconductor strategy (in part) seem to go against market realities. Will these projects, or the overall ambition of the EU to become digitally sovereign, change anything about the technology race or the EU’s position in it? The race is the product of self-fulﬁlling perceptions – if suﬃcient countries consider themselves to be in a race, then they are in a race. And if a race exists, it makes sense (a) to improve the EU’s capacities while (b) trying to defuse tensions and overcome the competitive logic of the race. The DS strategy is an attempt at the former, while the latter currently seems unachievable, given the limited leverage of the EU over China and the US.
