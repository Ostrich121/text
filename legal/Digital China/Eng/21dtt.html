Dancing to the same tune? EU and US approaches to standards setting in the global digital sector Jamal Shahina,b,c aAmsterdam School for Regional, Transnation and European Studies, Faculty of Humanities, University of Amsterdam, Amsterdam, The Netherlands; bBrussels School of Governance, Vrije Universiteit Brussel (VUB), Ixelles, Belgium; cUnited Nations University, CRIS, Bruges, Belgium

ABSTRACT As both the European Union (EU) and the United States (US) con­ struct their approaches to governing the digital economy in a global context, the role and influence of international organisa­ tions are being questioned. Specifically, the EU, traditionally a champion of multilateral regimes, has embarked on a strategy that tends toward a ‘digital sovereignty’ approach. However, the EU’s sovereigntist approach relies upon the power of multilateral regimes to ensure ‘managed interdependence’. This article focuses on the differences between the EU and the US regarding global standardisation processes and bodies. It argues that across the Atlantic, there is political agreement on how to manage standards development, but that policies to achieve this often diverge. This article focuses on some of the efforts of the EU-US Trade and Technology Council working group on standardization to show how this agreement on politics, but not policies, has emerged.

KEYWORDS Digital governance; European Union; digital sovereignty; standardization; United States; EU-US Trade and Technology Council

Introduction As other contributions to this special issue indicate, the digital economy has emerged as a paramount policy concern for transatlantic negotiators. Europe, with its market of 450 million potential consumers and its stated desire to be at the forefront of digital innovation, and the United States, with its dominance in the sector, have an interest in ensuring the smooth functioning of the global digital economy. To this end, the EU and the US revitalised the dialogue on Trade and Technology between the two blocs in 2020, resulting in the inauguration of the Trade and Technology Council (TTC) by US President Biden and European Commission President von der Leyen in June 2021 (see also Birchfield 2024). Transatlantic cooperation did exist prior to the launch of the TTC, but interactions took place at the operational level and rarely received such significant political endorsement.

For instance, standards ensure common security protocols are used for safe online payments and trustworthy sharing of digital identities. They also enable a common routing system for internet traffic, so that people can visit websites, such as taylorfrancis. com or unu.edu. Standards have become one of the battlegrounds of global digital politics, and all participants in the digital realm are taking an increased interest in the governance of these technical standards that were once considered outside of the realm of politics. I will argue that the EU and the US have made high-level efforts to streamline and increase coherence in the field of standardisation, to counter perceived threats from actors outside of these two blocs. Although American and European principles on the issue of standards development are different, stronger external pressures have led to concerted efforts to increase cooperation. I will show that this increase in bilateral action reveals a transatlantic push to maintain leadership over standards development, directly impacting global digital cooperation.

As with other pieces in this special issue, this article contributes to the ongoing debates in academic literature on the reverberation in political (regulatory) regimes across the Atlantic and their influence on global policy discussions. This contribution builds on literature that considers standards and rules in the digital sector, adding reflections on how securitisation and digital sovereignty have influenced these debates, often situated in public policy analysis literature. The article also examines EU and US cooperation in their approach to standards development in the digital economy. The paper supports this argument by looking at collaboration within the TTC, and their concerted efforts to shape global negotiations in Standards Development Organisations. Through this, I reveal whilst both actors tend to dance differently to the same tune, they do so in ways that are – in some respects – converging.

This paper focuses on explaining EU and US collaboration in standards building. I will draw upon inspiration from a securitisation approach (Christou 2019; Claessen 2020; Bellanova, Carrapico, and Duez 2022; see Brown Scott 2024) to try to help explain how the EU identifies standards as a crucial issue. The European Commission has securitised discussions of standards to justify joint transatlantic action in negotiating the digital economy. This allows for standards to be developed within the transatlantic partnership, instead of at the global level. Perarnaud and Rossi (2023) show how the EU’s approach to standards has been inconsistent, and yet also reveals attempts ‘to strategically use standards to leverage influence at the global level’ (p.4). Those who build the standards have a clear advantage over those who then must follow them. I build on this work with an excursion into explaining the EU-US relationship and how standardisation has become one of the tools to help these actors govern the digital economy. I also combine literature on international standards and public policy with literature on the concept of digital sovereignty, which has become a key area of focus in many policy and scholarly discus­ sions. Digital sovereignty is an ecumenical concept, which broadly describes the changing role of state-based actors – and particularly the EU – in the digital sector. I use ‘digital sovereignty’ to illustrate the EU’s strategy. The EU has instrumentalised this term to carve out a role for itself as a sovereign actor in the digital sector, with ambitions to shape rules

and standards at European and global levels. In doing so, it has created the space to deal with the US as an equal partner, thereby fomenting its role at the international level.

In line with the aims of this special issue, the core research question in this article focuses on questions of sovereignty and leadership in governing the digital economy. I ask the question: Why do the US and the EU collaborate in the digital sector, and what does this mean for global digital cooperation? To provide analytical depth to the response to this question, I choose to draw on examples relating to standardisation, which allow us to unpack how both the European Union and the United States have addressed their work­ ing relationships with each other and other actors. American and European attitudes towards international standards bodies provide a useful source for explaining how they have been active in negotiating the digital economy: for a long time, their approaches to standards development have not been in tune. The framework of the article focuses on the emergence of ‘digital sovereignty’ discourses. These have emerged particularly from the EU side; the US talks more about leadership, as they have already established sovereignty in this space. I then show that the securitization of standards development has enabled the EU to take on a role as an equal partner with the US in bilateral discussions, to some extent bypassing global forums for discussion. This focus on digital sovereignty and security allows me to show that transatlantic collaboration is present and growing: the EU and the US have started to play the same tune and are now starting to coordinate their dances.

Standardisation: an increasingly political discussion Standardisation in the digital sector has appeared on the radar screens of European and American policymakers and has been used as a tool by other parts of the world such as China (Kokas 2022, Nanni 2021). Standards are the topic of many digital policy discussions due to the need for interoperability and connectivity across networks (which are not networks if they are not connected), and increasingly – as I show below – forums for standards development are recognised as spaces where geopolitical tensions are emerging.

Standardisation is a key instrument for international cooperation, as it provides space for technical bodies to establish norms, principles, and rules that determine the technol­ ogies that underlie the digital economy. A plethora of different institutions exist to ensure this takes place at multiple levels of governance. At the global level, they are generally determined by private organisations such as the International Standards Organisation (ISO), the International Electrotechnical Commission (IEC), and other similar organisations (Fransen and Kolk 2007; Harcourt, George, and Seamus 2020; Mattli 2001; Mattli and Büthe 2003; Postnikov 2019; Russell 2014; Teleanu 2021). Regional and national standards bodies also exist, and these work to contribute to the development of standards at the global level, and also to develop standards for areas that are not specifically determined anywhere else (Shahin et al. 2024).

Although binding agreements on the implementation of technical standards are often made in multilateral organisations such as the International Telecommunications Union (ITU), much of the work takes place in private member­ ship organisations. These Standards Development Organisations (SDOs) are gener­ ally nongovernmental organisations with private membership, where states act as

This multistakeholder institutional setup leads to a complex arrange­ ment where states work in tandem with private-sector organisations to establish rules that are subsequently applied in the relevant sectors (Fransen and Kolk 2007). It is worthwhile noting that the increasing importance of the Internet for global communication encouraged a shift from old-style telecommunications (‘bellheads’) technologies to internet-style (‘netheads’) networked technologies (Updegrove 2007). This, in turn, resulted in several challenges to existing international stan­ dards-setting processes, as standards for Internet-related technologies are deter­ mined by organisations that were governed in completely different ways (Brousseau, Marzouki, and Méadel 2012; Harcourt, George, and Seamus 2020). This has added to the complexity of the standards ecosystem for the digital economy, thereby contributing to what some have described as ‘disarray’ (Simcoe 2012).

The leadership of standards development is sometimes claimed by multiple orga­ nisations that have different standards development processes, leading to forum shopping by some actors (Girard 2019). This story of conflicting fora and processes for standards development is typical to the digital sector, where bellheads and net­ heads still compete for dominance in emerging technology fields (David and Shurmer 1996). Standards are built in a myriad of different ways. Essentially, two emerging dynamics compete in the field of standards development: a top-down and a bottomup one (Ameil 1995; Blind, Gauch, and Hawkins 2010; Kerwer 2005; Overbeek, van Apeldoorn, and Nölke 2007). The US has traditionally considered that the rules around standards are bottom-up, whereas the EU has taken the opposite approach (Shahin 2006a).

As several scholars have noted, standards in the digital space – and particularly in the field of the internet – have emerged as points of contention in international politics (Cabral and Tobias 2006; Flonk, Jachtenfuchs, and Obendiek 2020; Nanni 2021; Rühlig 2023). Drawing on literature from fields such as Science and Technology Studies (STS), we also learn about the importance of standards and their institutions in perpetuating preexisting structures, as well as the tendency to treat standards as silent ‘sites of contesta­ tion’ (Dijstelbloem 2021; Milan and ten Oever 2017). In the sections below, I outline the increased political salience of standards and their governance in recent years, particularly as the dynamics of the world shift towards a rethink of the liberal order as a key driver of international politics.

I have chosen to look at discussions on ‘technical’ standards, which also relate – to a large degree – to the turn to infrastructure governance (Musiani 2013, 2022). I discuss specifically the area of standards-building in the EU-US TTC (see also Papers 6 and 5). Standards are crucial for both European and American policymakers, who have different interests and ambitions when it comes to standards development. Cooperation on standards setting exists at the bilateral level as well as in global forums. Thus standards provide a relevant field of investigation of the collaboration between the EU and the US in negotiating the global economy. The salience of standards development in the technical sphere must not be underestimated, as political institutions work toward developing common understandings of how to ensure that economies and societies are not desta­ bilised by advances in these fields, which address our critical infrastructures and potential threats to democratic societies.

Framework: digital sovereignty and the securitisation of standards The following framework outlines how digital sovereignty in the EU provides a useful lens to approach the EU-US relationship in negotiating the digital economy. I show how this concept allows the EU to claim legitimacy in bilateral discussions with the US. I then draw upon securitisation debates to explain the increasingly political nature of standardisation.

Digital sovereignty: claiming legitimacy Digital sovereignty has become a leitmotif for many states and even non-state actors, who are exploring ways of recapturing ‘control’ of the governance of the digital sector (Young 2015). It has a variety of different meanings and an equal variety of synonyms, such as network sovereignty, technological sovereignty, and cyber sovereignty (Adams and Albakajai 2016; Benhamou and Sorbier 2006; Shen 2016). It was originally used by states such as China to react to the increasing threats of globalisation (Thumfart 2022). In recent years, European actors, particularly at the EU level have started to adopt the language of digital sovereignty or variants of the term, such as ‘open strategic autonomy’ (Broeders, Cristiano, and Kaminska 2023; Monsees and Lambach 2022; Poli 2023; Thieulin 2019). This curiosity in the use of digital sovereignty as a justification for policy development, particularly by the EU, whose ‘sovereignty’ is contested by many, has piqued interest in the term. In tune with this broader debate over the nature of ‘Digital Sovereignty’ in Europe, Hoeffler and Mérand refer to the concept as a ‘repertoire of ideas’ within the von der Leyen Commission, designed to ‘rein in Big Tech’(2023, 17). Standardisation is a key element of this digital sovereignty approach and was identified as one of the key pillars of the European Commission’s Strategic Foresight Report. This emphasised the need for strengthened standards processes to help the EU develop its concept of open strategic autonomy (European Commission 2022b).

Accordingly, many have tried to explain how the digital economy transforms global institutions, or rather, how these actors – in the case of this literature, the EU – try to shape their role in these global spaces (Barrinha and Christou 2022). Others, such as Couture and Toupin (2019), have looked at how ‘sovereignty in relation to the digital’ also incorporates different communities (such as indigenous communities). The emergence of new tech­ nological challenges and powerful (corporate) actors acting globally in the digital domain contributes to what Gammeltoft-Hansen and Adler-Nissen have termed as a process of ‘expansion of the playing field related to sovereignty’ (Gammeltoft-Hansen and Adler- Nissen 2008).

To understand how digital sovereignty is used as a guiding framework for policy in this area, Gammeltoft-Hansen and Adler-Nissen offer insights from a critical studies perspective (2008). To understand the functioning of sovereignty claims, who performs them, and under which conditions, the authors put forward three key features – players, rules, and moves. Where the players enact sovereignty claims, moves would be the strategic use of these claims through political and legal practices to achieve different outcomes. As I show in the analysis below, both the US and the EU’s engagement in the TTC show that they are making moves to ensure that standards agreed by these two partners maintain their dominance. Rules represent the legal content of sovereignty and also ‘reflect nonexplicit rules that structure the way we

think and act in the name of sovereignty’ (Gammeltoft-Hansen and Adler-Nissen 2008, 6). Moreover, this framework understands (digital) sovereignty not as a claim to supreme authority in a given territory or policy field, but as a form of legitimation for a new sort of politics – the sovereignty playing field – where the EU’s claim to (digital) sovereignty become understood as meaningful by other players, allowing it to be accepted as a global player, and authority, on digital issues. This framing of sovereignty reflects ongoing discussions about the use of the concept as an act of legitimation (Werner and De Wilde 2001). In this sense, digital sovereignty becomes a form of stating the aspirational, or claiming legitimisation through saying and doing.

Indeed, Emmanuel Macron in his speech on ‘The Quest for European Sovereignty’ (Macron 2017), described this desire to seek international acknowledgement as a sovereign player: ’European sovereignty requires constructing, and we must do it. Why? Because what constructs and forges our profound identity, this balance of values, this relation with freedom, human rights and justice cannot be found anywhere on the planet. This attachment to a market economy, but also social justice’. This sentiment was echoed by Commission President von der Leyen (2020): ’“Digital sovereignty” is not just an economic concept. We are a Union of values. One of the great questions is: How can we preserve and promote our values in a digitised world?’. In the analysis below, I identify the use of digital sovereignty as a tool to enable the European Commission to position itself as an equal partner to other international powers, with the specific (domestic) goal of protecting European values in the digital realm (Heidebrecht 2024; Roberts et al. 2021; Seidl and Schmitz 2023).

Standards and securitisation: building and setting standards I have shown that digital sovereignty provides legitimation of the EU’s role in shaping global policy in the digital sector. In part, this legitimation has emerged due to the securitization of the digital realm. Beyond technical organisations and trade negotiations, the digital realm has become ‘weaponised’ (Hansen and Nissenbaum 2009; Christou 2019; Brown Scott 2024). Technical standards development, initially non-political, became politicised and then securitised. Geopolitical tensions around the world have sparked reactions that have not left the digital sector unscathed. Security issues have become part of digital sovereignty discourse and practice in the EU (see Lambach and Oppermann 2022 for the German narratives around digital sovereignty; von Brockdorff 2022; Thieulin 2019). However, the EU tries to promote its variant of the securitised ‘digital sovereignty’ discourse. Adler-Nissen and Eggeling see EU digital sovereignty as a ‘“third-way” alter­ native to US surveillance capitalism and Chinese and Russian techno-authoritarianism’ (Adler-Nissen and Anabel Eggeling 2024, 993), focusing on how this term reflects the increased geopoliticisation of the EU. They see digital sovereignty as a performative discourse, with security as being central to their understanding of how this is executed. In a similar vein, Bellanova, Carrapico, and Duez (2022) reveal that the ‘digital sovereignty as security’ approach can be coined as the EU’s attempt to ‘control digital security infrastructures’ (p.338), which impacts the nature of European Security integration. Farrand and Carrapico (2022) build on this argument, arguing that digital sovereignty is about ‘regulatory mercantilism’. They show how the European Commission moves towards oversight and control of (generally US) private sector actors. Regulatory

Page 8

mercantilism in cybersecurity policy, or geo-dirigiste turn in the EU’s industrial policy (Seidl and Schmitz 2023) are markers that reveal a more proactive attempt from the EU (and particularly the European Commission) to position itself as a key player in the global digital economy.

This paper contributes to these debates on the increasing importance of security in contemporary policy in the digital economy. As the literature above argues, the European Commission has used (variants of) digital sovereignty as a tool to legitimise a turn to the securitisation of cyberspace within its borders. The EU, through various legislative acts and agencies (Cyber Resilience Act, NIS 2 directive, the EU’s Cybersecurity Agency ENISA, and the establishment of the Cybersecurity Competence Centre) has developed a competence inside its borders (see Fahey 2024). Standards are central to cybersecurity policy, with the EU establishing Certification frameworks for Cybersecurity and the Cloud. Both of these standards impose requirements on third-country service providers in the EU. The EU uses this security turn to allow it to exercise its domestic competence on a global level. I argue that the EU’s use of the language of ‘digital sovereignty as security’ enhances its capacity to work with other sovereigns (such as the US) in combating common threats. In this way, digital sovereignty is used as a particular European-Commission-led manifestation of the securitisation of cyberspace (see also Brown Scott 2024). However, the use of the ‘digital sovereignty as security’ discourse has important consequences, as Perarnaud and Rossi note: ‘the most important outcome of these developments relates to one of its unintended consequences – the legitimation of a discursive push towards a state-centred vision of Internet standardisation’ (2023, 17).

The subsequent parts of the paper address how sovereignty and securitisation have become important drivers for increased cooperation between the EU and the US, and describe how this has played out in the field of standards development. I carry this out through an examination of the EU-US TTC. The analysis is based upon my reading of policy documents, speeches, and formal and informal statements made concerning transatlantic cooperation on technical standards, as well as several interviews held in the period March to October 2023 with participants in the TTC and the EU-sponsored Trade and Technology Dialogue. Drawing on the framework developed by Gammeltoft-Hansen and Adler- Nissen, I discuss the players, the rules, and the moves that have been made in terms of developing a common language – and action – on standards development in the TTC.

The players in international standards-setting Economic interests – and security concerns – have become more dominant in policy discussions around international standards in the digital sector. The destabilisation of the security environment due to Russia’s invasion of Ukraine and increased tensions between China and Taiwan have contributed to this dominance. The digital sector has been singled out for specific attention, due to the use of digital platforms to threaten the stability of Western democracies. As partners on both sides of the Atlantic see the importance of collaboration and coordination across the digital sector, they emphasise the mechanisms they use to work together. This section of the paper focuses on how the EU has shaped its role as a player in this field. In the US context, discussions around digital sovereignty are minimal in the sense that the US already claims sovereignty in this space. US strategies for

The von der Leyen Commission took office in 2019 and established a digitally sovereign Europe as a critical priority for their first term. However, the vagueness of this concept and the lack of a concrete policy line left the operational aspects of what this means for policy development to a later stage. As I show below, standards development was a crucial part of the European Commission’s sovereign aspirations. The EU instrumentalises digital sovereignty to position itself as a key player in setting international standards. Interestingly, the EU’s digital policy was attributed to two Commissioners who, at first sight, did not seem to share the same conceptual under­ standing of digital sovereignty and how it should be achieved (Hoeffler and Mérand 2023). Therefore, to analyse the Commission’s pursuit of digital sovereignty, it is important to understand the institutional dynamics at play within the European Commission. These dynamics play into the shaping of the EU’s external action in the area and provide partners in other parts of the world with insights into the EU’s ambitions.

The mission letters addressed by the President of the Commission both to von der Leyen (2019b, 2019c), are insightful in this respect. In these letters, overlapping areas of responsibility are described. For example, Executive VP Vestager is tasked with co-leading the EU’s industrial strategy with Thierry Breton, but is also responsible for leading the work on the European Data strategy, devising a European approach to Artificial Intelligence, and upgrading liability rules for digital platforms and services (DSA). Furthermore, the mission letter states that the Executive VP is tasked to steer the EU as it navigates the Digital Age, strengthening its industry and innovation, which is seen as a critical tool to enhance the EU’s technological leadership and digital sovereignty.

The relationship between these two members of the College who are nominally responsible for digital issues has been the subject of several news pieces that highlight their divergent positions, perhaps from an over-simplistic point of view (see, for example, Larger et al. 2020). Although Vestager was hierarchically superior, the overlapping com­ petencies of the Commissioners and the broad nature of their mandates may also lead to this perception in European policymaking circles.

Taking up her role as the EU’s top competition policy chief, with the added responsi­ bilities of the Executive Vice Presidency, Vestager would acquire a reputation as the enforcer of EU rules, especially in the competition domain. This fact has led many actors to consider Vestager in the free market values (neo-liberal) camp of the debate (The Economist 2022). For her, digital sovereignty would be about the ability to enforce the EU’s own rules on companies, individuals, and even third states when they operate in the EU. This would follow a market-based approach, that took European values into account. Standards building was seen as key to this strategy. As Vestager said in a speech to the European Parliament in May 2022 ‘We need to influence global standards so that they reflect democratic values’ (Vestager 2022).

The protection of the internal market and the global competitiveness of the EU digital sector, so the logic states, should be addressed through a set of rules fit for the digital world. This should emerge in collaboration with like-minded partners

(such as the US) and international organisations that help develop standards and norms for the digital economy. The EU would achieve digital sovereignty by ensuring a level playing field that is defined at a global level, where EU companies can thrive. However, prior to her period of unpaid leave as Executive Vice President, Vestager received criticism as to the effect of her political efforts, which have failed yet to reduce the power of US tech giants in the digital world (Stolton 2023).

On the other hand, Thierry Breton, a former French minister with an industrial back­ ground rooted in the telecommunications sector, is a proponent of a more interventionist approach to practising digital sovereignty. This comes through clearly in his approach to European standardisation. Breton has argued that the European Commission’s Standardisation Strategy takes ‘action to preserve the integrity of the European standar­ disation process, putting European SMEs and the European interest at the centre’ (Breton 2022). This approach is represented by high investments in the ‘national’ or ‘European’ industry as a tool to increase global competitiveness and secure the security of supply. Moreover, it may include restrictive policies towards third countries and foreign compa­ nies – which by their market power or by benefiting from state aid from their country of establishment – are seen as distorting competition. This anti-competitive behaviour penalises the EU’s industrial base. When non-European technologies (and standards) dominate, the European Commission argues that security threats may appear.

Speeches and press releases from these Commissioners reveal competing approaches to digital sovereignty. Generally, both Vestager and Breton chose to highlight different features of European digital sovereignty – with Breton usually placing the stress on how that policy contributes to building digital sovereignty from an economic point of view, and how it contributes to ending dependencies on other parts of the world, by placing Europe in a ‘technological race’ or a ‘Big Data race’ with other regions. This is blatant in his speeches, for example, ‘in the face of growing tensions between the United States and China, Europe will not be a mere bystander, let alone a battleground. It is time to take our destiny into our own hands. This also means identifying and investing in the digital technologies that will underpin our sovereignty and our industrial future,’ (Breton 2022) delivered in Hannover in July 2020. When Breton talks about standards, he talks about increasing the power of the EU’s Internal Market and its reach beyond its borders.

Vestager, on the other hand, seems to present a view that promotes the EU’s role in helping set rules ‘externally’. In other words, she appears to reflect on how the regulation of certain technologies will contribute to an individual’s self-determination, to the protec­ tion (and projection) of democracy and other European values, and their contribution to overarching societal goals at the international level. In May 2021, Vestager gave a speech where she said: ‘our ability to promise Europeans a positive digital future doesn’t just depend on what happens in Europe – but on how we manage to shape the world around us’ (Vestager 2022). As shown below, this shaping of the world around the EU happens through the building of standards.

Through this unpacking of the political approaches to digital sovereignty inside the European Commission, I show that there is a willingness to work together and to work apart from the US. It appears that rather than contradicting each other, the Commissioners complement each other’s perspectives and enable discussions to take place with different audiences. The increasing role of private actors (‘new sovereigns’) as players in the digital sector cannot be ignored. The role of these new sovereigns ensures that digitalisation is a politically salient issue, with contestation on both sides (Alford 2022; Smith 2023). The transatlantic relationship is multilayered and engages public and private sector actors on both sides. US-based service providers dominate the scene, which adds a layer of ‘player’ to the discussions between the EU and the US. To protect its citizens, the EU uses its regulatory role and self-declared sovereign status to ‘bring back control’ to European soil, through the raft of digital policy legislation that has accompanied the von der Leyen Commission (Bradford 2020).

The rules of international standards setting This section of the paper examines the rules of standards setting. Following Gammeltoft-Hansen and Adler-Nissen, this refers to how the EU and the US think and act as (digital) sovereigns in standards development. I have shown in the preced­ ing sections that the international standards development ecosystem is complex and that the EU and the US have different histories of governing standards. These differ­ ences influence their approaches to collaboration. The EU and the US recognise that if they wish to keep global leadership, their approaches to the ‘rules’ around standards development need to change. The securitisation of the digital sector facilitates this: for example, the partners have worked together on guidelines for Artificial Intelligence systems.

The work carried out in the TTC aims to bring some semblance of ‘sovereign control’ over discussions on standardisation, recognising that they are part of the increasingly securitised debates on governing the digital sector. Despite the private sector-driven nature of the field, the EU and the US have worked together – and apart – to encourage widespread acceptance of standards for the digital economy. Indeed, both actors have stressed the need for a common approach to standards building, where it has been stated that ‘[h]aving better aligned and interoperable technology standards would facilitate trading activities between companies considerably’ (US EU Trade and Technology Council 2022). This joint move to recognise the importance of the rules of standards building – in a bilateral setting – is an indicator of how the EU and the US (re)enforce their leadership in the ‘rules of the sovereignty game’ in the digital sector.

This transatlantic collaboration on standards in the digital sector comes at a time when the interest in promoting global cooperation in the digital sector is increasingly apparent. Whilst the EU and the US are making rules through the TTC, the UN attempts to reinforce global rules for the digital sector. Reacting to the increasing politicisation and ‘nationa­ lisation’ of the digital sector, the UN Secretary-General launched a process aimed at stimulating digital cooperation. This culminated in the report ‘The Age of Digital Interdependence’ in 2019 (United Nations Secretary-General’s High-Level Panel on Digital Cooperation 2019). That report has been supplemented with a series of events and publications focused on the elaboration of a ‘Global Digital Compact’ (United Nations

2023). Likewise, the annual UN-organised Internet Governance Forum (IGF), in existence since 2006, has also emphasised the application of global rules and standards, to ensure that the global communications network functions on principles of openness and inter­ operability. In many ways, this emphasis on global cooperation sits in contrast to the increased development of action at the bilateral level, epitomised by the TTC and championed by the EU.

From technical to (geo)political moves? Sovereignty and leadership in standards development As mentioned in (Young 2015), at the beginning of the century the trend was to consider the global establishment of ‘non’-rules as the basis for technological and economic systems. However, recent years have shown a reverse trend toward sovereignty-driven policymaking in the digital sector. Economic and political impulses have driven recent developments in the digital sector. These concern the need to regulate the sector which is dominated by a handful of large US-based corporations. One of the ways this has become visible is through the establishment of local rules with global consequences, such as the EU’s Digital Markets Act. This section of the paper explains the moves that public authorities have made to support the emergence of standards leadership in the US and the EU. The US approach to standards relies on the private sector, with bottom-up innovations determining standards. This contrasts with the EU’s approach, which is reliant on top-down regulation from public authorities. I then go on to describe how work on standards development in the TTC is bridging these two approaches.

The US approach: leading the digital sector In the United States, the American National Standards Institute (ANSI) is responsible for standards management at the national level, but they traditionally have a weaker position in decision-making in this space, due to the nature of the American innovation system which relies on the private sector. Alongside ANSI, the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST), which is part of the US Department of Commerce, also plays a role in developing standards for technology. The approach to standards development in the United States is bottom-up, with a focus on the establishment of standards through market dynamics, as opposed to the top-down approach favoured in Europe. Mattli and Büthe have gone so far as to call the American national standards-setting process ‘anarchic’ (2003). Literature in the field of standards-setting processes tends to represent the European processes as far stronger and more advanced than those within the US (David and Shurmer 1996; Laurent 2022; Mattli and Büthe 2003). Whilst US approaches to standardisation appear to be far more ‘laissez-faire’ than in the EU, it should be noted that this should not be confused with a lack of government support for innovation in the US (Fred and Keller 2009).

The EU approach: shaping the digital sector The European Union has taken a proactive approach to standardisation in the digital sector, in its capacity as a global regulator (Young 2015). It has continued

to push for a digital economy built upon rules that enable the Single European Market to function in a global system (Damro 2012). The EU’s new standardisation strategy echoes this ambition to create European standards with global reach. In this strategy, the European Commission states: ‘While European standardisation has been a success story for the establishment of the EU’s single market, the strategic importance of standards has not been adequately recognised at the cost of EU leadership in standards-setting. This must change.’ (European Commission 2022a, 1).

The EU is an organisation that is quintessentially a standards builder (European Commission 1988; Laurent 2022). It has concerned itself with creating standards for the digital single market since the eEurope initiatives at the turn of the century, when it highlighted data protection for European citizens (consumer protection), and the creation of a level playing field to enable innovation (Shahin 2006a, 2006b). Ursula von der Leyen has argued that to give Europe ‘a fair chance of shaping the digital world [, we] have to develop European solutions and standards’ (von der Leyen 2019a). More recently, the EU has pushed for a Declaration on Rights and Principles for the use of the Internet, along with a host of other initiatives designed to help shape the digital sector (Shahin 2022).

European standards bodies also play a key role in determining not only European standards, but global ones, as the market power (Damro 2012) of Europe is so conse­ quential. The key organisations in this field are CEN (European Committee for Standardisation), CENELEC (European Electrotechnical Committee for Standardisation), and ETSI (the European Telecommunications Standards Institute). They have been tasked through European legislation to act as the body that sets standards of importance to the European market: they define and develop standards at the EU level.

Relating to the language of digital sovereignty, Europe claims a ‘functional’ sovereignty in the digital sector through its self-appointed role as a rule-setter. This is not the case in the United States, where discussions on sovereignty in the digital sector are rarely raised. The dominance of the American technology sector (see introduction to this special issue) reveals that those who hold power rarely need to make claims toward sovereignty.

The European Commission even attempted to reassess the underlying infrastructure of the internet through its initial ‘DNS4EU’ initiative, which attempted to set European stan­ dards for the internet’s core functions. The (un)intended consequences of such a policy, however, have been highlighted (see Perarnaud and Rossi 2023; Radu 2023). The literature describing the evolution of GAIA-X reveals this. GAIA-X was originally intended to be an alternative cloud provision service under EU rules but turned into a European attempt at standard setting (Autolitano and Pawlowska 2021; Obendiek and Seidl 2023). At the time of writing, at least two of the ‘Big Tech’ corporations based in the US (Microsoft and Amazon) have developed ‘sovereign clouds’ that align with EU rules on standards for cloud services. This has ‘thwarted’ attempts to use standardisation processes to support EU-based services.

When considering digital sovereignty as security, these examples show how security, stability, and ‘ownership’ of the digital economy’s standards also emerged as key motivations for many of the policy initiatives launched in the period 2019–2024. The values of security, stability, and ownership are reflected in the combined messages of both Vestager and Breton, shown above. In hindsight, some of these initiatives seem to have been a little too enthusiastic and have seen strong resistance from the technical community and civil society alike.

Complementary or conflicting responses? The EU-US Trade and Technology Council as the vehicle for negotiations Ongoing transatlantic discussions on the digital sector are not new. Although it has recently seen a revival through the establishment of the TTC, a previous incarnation of the partnership was established in 1994. At that time, the European Commission set up a dialogue between Commission officials and their counterparts in the US administration. This was established for three main purposes: to improve information exchange, to facilitate early warning for potential conflicting issues, and to gain a greater understand­ ing of the differences and similarities between American and European regulatory frame­ works. It was called, by the European Commission, the EU-US Information Society Dialogue. Its American partners preferred to refer to these meetings simply as ‘Bilaterals with the Commission’ on the specific topic under discussion (although the Commissiongiven name was recognised by the US participants to the meetings). This was not the high-level ministerial meetings that we see in the TTC. Representatives from different services were in attendance on both sides; the personnel involved were normally depen­ dent upon the topic under discussion. Representatives from the European Commission from different Directorate Generals (DGs) would attend: notably DGs Internal Market, External Relations, and Trade. They would participate alongside the coordinating service on the European Commission side, DG Information Society (now called DG CONNECT). On the US government side, participants feature officials from the National Telecommunications and Information Administration (NTIA), Department of Justice (DoJ), Federal Communications Commission (FCC), and Federal Trade Commission (FTC). These gatherings tended to be quite informal with no official secretariat; the European Commission would coordinate meetings in Brussels and its counterpart would do the same for the Washington discussions. This was relatively active, with annual meetings held until around 2005.

After this time, as political priorities changed within the institutions, these meetings became less effective. The European Commission shifted its focus to global frameworks for information sharing. In 2019, however, with the change in political direction at the European Commission level, and a desire to become more active in supporting European interests with its strategic partners, the idea of a transatlantic Trade and Technology Council emerged. The TTC between the EU and the US is the first active instance of a European-managed TTC. It has since established one with India, and has intentions to develop a similar relationship with the Japanese, albeit labelled the EU-Japan Digital Partnership (European Council 2022).

The establishment of the TTC came at a time when stakes in the digital sector were high. The European Union, riding high on the perceived positive benefits of the General Data Protection Regulation, was actively pursuing a global leadership role in the digital space, and was intent on developing new rules for Digital Services and Digital Markets. Geopolitical and economic considerations were also part of this move to push for more ‘digital sovereignty’, which were driven by concerns about the ongoing dominance of several key US-based companies (Google, Apple, Facebook, Amazon, and Microsoft: GAFAM) and increasing concerns about the motives and roles of the other dominant actor in this space, China (see Brown Scott 2024). At the same time, Russia’s concerted actions on digital platforms, aiming to destabilise democratic institutions in the West, led

to action across democratic nations to try to curb the misuse of social networks and other core infrastructures in the digital space (Fahey 2024). This securitsation of the digital sector increased the need for like-minded partners to talk.

To date, the TTC has held several ministerial meetings. Dialogues at the opera­ tional level are continuous. Ten working groups have been established in the TTC, one focusing on standardisation. Within the EU, the European Commission is managing the process, and work is carried out by two Directorate Generals: DG GROW and DG CONNECT (Communications, Networks and Technologies). On the US side, exchanges are also cross-governmental, with different departments enga­ ging in the discussions. Substantive work takes place within the working groups. Similar to the Information Society Dialogue, work consists mainly of sharing knowl­ edge, understanding approaches, and ensuring that contradictory policies do not appear.

One example of how the TTC has worked relates to the recent implementation of the US’s Inflation Reduction Act, which was seen as contradictory to earlier discussions on managing global supply chains – more specifically on the transfer of innovation from Europe to the US. The TTC was (post-factum) the vehicle to exchange knowledge and to counter potential problems between the two partners on the issues for European markets that could arise from the US government’s venture into green technology.

Participants and observers of discussions at the working group level claim that the TTC is a positive step. It provides a privileged and institutionalised channel for discussion on issues pertinent to the digital economy (Burwell 2024 and Interview 2 involved in the working group on standardisation, March 2023). However, these same respondents also reveal that much of the ‘dialogue’ is about getting different stakeholders into the room to allow them to represent their interests to regulators (Interview 1). A review of the list of submitted documents available on the European Commission’s website for the TTC working groups reveals that many proposals are made by trade associations. Similarly, minutes of meetings – some of which are available for public viewing (Macron, n.d.) – also show that the participants in many of these meetings are often high-level executives at large ICT companies. This is remarkably similar to the previous transatlantic foray into negotiating the digital economy.

In terms of the work around standards, it has been clear that the efforts to develop common approaches have had to overcome fundamentally different approaches to establishing standards. The working group on technical standards has been estab­ lished to ‘develop approaches for coordination and cooperation in critical and emer­ ging technology standards including AI and other emerging technologies’. It does so with the ambition to develop standards that are ‘in line with the EU’s and US’ core values’, recognising ‘the importance of international standardisation activities’ (European Commission n.d.). The conceptual clash between the bottom-up and topdown approaches that characterise the US and EU approaches has generated – according to some interviewees who have been engaged in the discussions as obser­ vers – a lowest-common-denominator approach to some of the discussions (Interviews 1, 2 and 3). However, dialogue through the TTC has enabled common understandings to emerge. The Working Group on Standardisation meets regularly and works on common roadmaps (in the field of artificial intelligence, for example). Engagement has been broad: the working parties and their corresponding workstreams are

Page 16

comprised of experts from different parts of the American and European public administration systems, or their research representatives. ‘Politics’ have not always turned into concrete policies, despite the activity in the TTC. The TTC has remained symbolic for the most part, but this symbolism is important for both partners: it shows that they are willing to discuss global policy issues in bilateral settings. It has the added value of giving the EU recognition on the international scene. It may be the case that these joint political moves to frame the global digital economy are sufficient to ensure US and EU leadership.

Additionally, a representative from the parallel Trade and Technology Dialogue (TTD) project is present. The TTD is sponsored by the European Commission. It was created to enable civil society and other interests to be represented in the TTC, attempting to build a multistakeholder process that brings together different interests to shape a common approach to standardisation in the digital sector (Interview 2). The establishment of the TTD reveals an attempt to bring the opposed approaches to standards development together. By creating a multistakeholder framework for discussion, common understand­ ings on standards development may appear.

Conclusion There is an evolving convergence between the discourses delivered by both the EU and the US. The ‘trusted partner’ status between the two is a reaction to the increasing geopolitical tensions in the world. Standardisation is one of the key areas where the US and the EU see the need to cooperate.

Whether this reverberation also plays out in concrete policy outcomes is another matter. Discussions on certification standards continue in the EU, with the Nordic coun­ tries strongly supporting a removal of the sovereignty requirement on cloud services, to continue strong ties with US companies. On the other hand, private actors such as Microsoft and Amazon are actively working with the EU institutions to ensure full compliance with such requirements with their Sovereign Cloud initiatives. Therefore, reverberations are present in the actual implementation of negotiations in the digital economy.

As can be seen from the above, the US and the EU see each other as functional partners when it comes to discussions on governance of the digital sector, and they do so in a context that requires international collaboration. The language used in policy docu­ ments and strategies is converging. There is a recognition that the US and the EU need to work together to balance the power exercised by other actors, notably China, in this space (Teleanu 2021). However, the interplay between the US and the EU does little to support the current multilateral system, where debates on standardisation are also taking place. The joint transatlantic effort toward a Declaration for the Future of the Internet, now signed by 70 states around the world, operates outside of the current multilateral system. Similarly, the TTC creates a space for bilateral consultation on global governance. Both partners are also active in creating similar negotiating contexts with other parties, such as Japan.

There is a slow but steady turn, however, in the US approach to multilateral organisa­ tions in this space. In a return to the politics of the Obama tenure, the US is moving forward on language around interconnectedness and interdependence, whilst ensuring

American leadership in key digital sectors such as cybersecurity and artificial intelligence. The ITU now has an American Secretary General, and the US has rejoined UNESCO as a member. Both organisations also work on standardisation issues in the digital sector. It may be that the shaping role that the EU has nurtured over the years is also to be adopted by the US.

The EU believes that the success of its ambition to achieve digital sovereignty has been legitimised by the political buy-in of the TTC. It has used the TTC to legitimise its role in standards development in the digital sector, a space traditionally domi­ nated by the US private sector. It has done so, in part, by drawing attention to the security threats to the digital sector, which has encouraged joint action with the US. Together, these partners are shaping policies that have a global impact and are consciously doing so in a bilateral forum. At the same time, multilateral and global spaces for standards development have not been ignored. Are the US and the EU dancing to the same tune? The future is unclear, but the music is becoming the same.
