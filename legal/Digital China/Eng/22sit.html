https://doi.org/10.1515/ngs-2021-0015 Received May 1, 2021; accepted September 6, 2021

Abstract: In the states of the European Union (EU), the question currently being asked is to what extent dependence on technologies from the USA and China will have a lasting impact on state sovereignty. The term digital sovereignty stands for the EU’s desire to compensate for the deficits of the past decades, which were caused by an insufficient development of the location for software and hardware development. Autocratic states use the path of digital autarky, the USA a path of liberalization and high degrees of openness. In the EU, on the other hand, regulation, data protection, and liberal values developed over centuries play a major role in the less pronounced IT development. The path of European states to more digital sovereignty has been addressed politically as an “action plan,” but there is still no common understanding or definition of what digital sovereignty exactly means. There is a lack of a target and a measurable index as well as evaluated measures derived from it. Based on a historical derivation, this article proposes a definition for European digital sovereignty and the formation of an index. The index needs to be further scientifically elaborated for applicability.

Keywords: cyber sovereignty, data sovereignty, technological sovereignty, European Union, governance, digital sovereignty

1 Introduction

The European Union addresses the need of action to increase digital sovereignty. This is necessary because the IT software- and hardware-systems used are predominantly sourced from non-European countries and dependence is increasing. The relevance of the EU’s IT industry is largely negligible, whereas it is becoming increasingly important for keeping a state’s ability to act and thus state sovereignty.

2 Historical Classification of State Sovereignty and Digitization

The question of state sovereignty and the implementation of a citizen’s right to determine for themselves the disclosure and use of their personal data became relevant with the invention of information technologies arguably dates all the back to the use of signal fires in ancient Greece (Aschoff 1989, 19; Standage 2000). In our time, the introduction of ARPANET, from which the Internet evolved, was fundamental to what we now understand as the network of networks as the basis for the commercialization of technology (Abbate 2001, 25).

2.1 State Sovereignty

Since the Middle Ages, the establishment and preservation of state sovereignty has been the focus of political debate in Europe. The concept of sovereignty emerged in late medieval Europe with the discussion of philosophers on the internal and external independence of states and on the effective exercise of state power. Bodin is thus regarded as the founder of the concept of sovereignty (Bodin 1994). In the following centuries, this also led to monarchical absolutism through the statetheoretical explanations of Hobbes, who discussed foundation of states as a social contract of citizens in favor of a sovereign monarch.

The teachings of Bodin and Hobbes marked the transition from the Middle Ages to modern Europe1 and are still inﬂuential today. The territorial boundaries of states were deﬁned by the Westphalian model2: state borders delineated the boundaries of a political, legislative, and executive space of the sovereign (Zimmer 2008, 55). Even if the principles have essentially remained the same until today, the model of the one and only sovereign was a counter-model to today’s understanding of the state, which is characterized by the separation of powers. Michael Foucault’s “governmentality” approach is particularly suitable for addressing sovereignty in the context of digitization because of the following parallel: the role of the internet and its governance, i.e. the influence of the internet on (political) decisions as well as the regulation of the internet by superordinate bodies (e.g. ICANN3) plays an essential role in the (political) discussion. The deﬁnition of internet governance is itself a political question (Abbate 2017). What is regulated centrally, what is permitted de-centrally, which decisions are influenced by the internet and how, and which stakeholders play which role in this are some of the core questions being discussed (DeNardis et al. 2020). Foucault’s approaches to “governmentality” in the context of the further development of state sovereignty were inﬂuenced by similar questions. He rejects the principle of one state sovereignty. As a result, he clariﬁes that the state governs the population and directs its actions to inﬂuence independent, free individuals in such a way that the optimum results for the people of a state. He opposes a clear attribution of power and formulates a sovereign administrative action characterized by multiple, different inﬂuences of power, such as the government and the citizens (Bublitz 2014, 273). In his reﬂections on “governmentality” in the 1970s and his terms, “Analytics of Power” and “Microphysics of Power,” Foucault

2.2 Relevance of Digitization for State Sovereignty

Digitization raises new challenges to the concept of state sovereignty, namely through the necessary protection of secret or relevant data and through a high level of technology dependency on manufacturers from other states, which can call state sovereignty into question. It is impossible to imagine today’s society and governance, or economic and political discussion without digitization. Digitization can be seen as an evolutionary stage of the industrial revolution. In Germanlanguage literature in particular, the current stage is referred to as the fourth, “4.0,” stage (Kagermann, Lukas, and Wahlster 2011).

Worldwide, England was the pioneer 400 years ago with the first stage (Tomory 2016). Already at the beginning of the eighteenth century, its economy was more advanced than that of the rest of Europe (Mohajan 2019, 383). Further

revolutionary stages followed with the introduction of assembly line production (second stage) and the introduction of modern computers in binary technology (third stage) from 1935 onwards (Ford 1923, 36; Mokyr 1998, 10; Strawn 2019). With the fourth revolutionary stage, the introduction of the internet, the spread of PCs and mobile devices among the population, a new epoch has begun since the turn of the millennium at the latest. A new, much faster type of innovation phase created mechanisms that enabled “digitization everywhere” (Schwab 2016). Almost all areas of economic and public life are connected: Users’ mobile devices, sensors in many electronic devices and the digitization of production or logistics chains enable a high degree of digital connectivity, which is the basis for decentralized self-organization and decreases the relevance of higher-level control. In the economy, buyers can record their requirements digitally, track the progress of their order even in the complexly organized and distributed production processes in “smart factories,” and track logistics up to delivery. This is a key driver of so-called cyber-physical systems (Bauernhansl, ten Hompel, and Vogel-Heuser 2014, 14–30; Wagner, Herrmann, and Thiede 2017).

Customers are meanwhile used to interacting digitally with providers. They develop the same expectations in the interaction between citizens and the state. This is how the need arises to carry out administrative services in governments, ministries, and authorities digitally. In the EU, 82%4 of the population use the internet, 56%5 of citizens search for information about public authorities online. However, administrative processes are still characterized by central control, concentration of knowledge and power, and the appropriation of information relevant to decision-making in order to guarantee reliable administrative action (Rölle 2020). This leads to slower processes than industry and civil society require in the fourth stage. On the one hand, civil society is gaining more and more access to information and can contact governments directly through platforms or organize itself through them; on the other hand, technological progress is giving the state more and more access to citizens’ data. Here, too, centralized control is taking a back seat due to the de-centralization of knowledge and power. In this respect, the “State 4.0” will be even more relevant in the future for establishing connectivity to the fourth industrial revolution and the associated challenges to functionality, sovereignty and IT security (Deckert 2020; Flatt et al. n.d.).

2.3 Concept of Digital Sovereignty – More than Just the Sum of its Parts

With the historical derivation of the terms sovereignty and digitization, the question arises whether “digital sovereignty” is a mere combination of the two individual terms and how this is to be understood. The frequency of use of the term in articles and books available online shows a growing consideration (Couture and Toupin 2019, 2). The literature research on the deﬁnition yields a heterogenic picture, which follows in three main categories.

Firstly, digital sovereignty in the sense of sovereignty in cyberspace. In the first growth phase of the internet, the idea of a sovereign space independent of states arose, which was to be created without territorial borders and with its own rules for the users, i.e. the people. This was to create an opposition to the traditional claim to the Westphalian model. With the encroachment of the state on the internet, activists sought a dedicated space, cyberspace, that would be free of state regulations and state sovereignty (Turner 2008, 13). This approach risked upsetting the nation-state, territorially limited and sovereign state system that had grown over 250 years. The discourse of the approach is well reﬂected in a manifesto by John Perry Barlow entitled “A Declaration of the Independence of Cyberspace,” in which he sees the colonial and, in his view, freedom-restricting rules of traditional states in cyberspace overcome and physical intervention possibilities for regulation reduced (Barlow 1996). Although Barlow’s approaches did not catch on, ideas related to the sovereignty of cyberspace are still present. Even if a separate cyber sovereignty space has not been established, a certain degree of self-organizing legal freedom is evident on the Internet (Dienstbühl and Weber 2014). Secondly, the concept of data sovereignty in the sense of data security is also important. The question “How secure is my data?” is just as much a concern for citizens as it is for companies or state institutions. In this context, companies focus on aspects of digital law and data protection, while the state discussion aims more at maintaining national security. In addition, the state is given a role in protecting the data of its citizens, since in a nation state, citizens demand the right to have the state protect their rights. Absolute data sovereignty means being able to decide anew at any time whether or how data is stored.

The German Ethics Council distinguishes data sovereignty from data protection and defines it in the context of the use of big data in health care as informational self-determination, which is ethically as well as constitutionally founded (Deutscher Ethikrat 2017, 40). Despite the focus of the observation, general principles for data sovereignty can be derived from it. Digital data sovereignty on the internet is also compared to territorial, “supreme control” and in the original

deﬁnition of sovereignty “independent of other sovereigns,” so that state sovereignty and data sovereignty converge conceptually to some extent in the literature (Bodin 1994; Goldsmith 1998, 475ff; Woods 2018).

In this respect, the question arises whether and how a state can sovereignly store and make available data on its territory. This applies equally to data of citizens, companies as well as state institutions. The rights of citizens are defined in the Charter of Fundamental Rights of the European Union and thus bring the “character of the Union as a community of values” to the fore expressed (Schmitz 2004, 692; Charter of Fundamental Rights of the European Union of European Union 2012, Article 8). In this context, the aspect of surveillance plays an essential role, as it guarantees the data sovereignty of citizens and institutions, i.e. ensures informational self-determination. Approaches to safeguard national security correspond to “strong data sovereignty,” whereas “weak data sovereignty” describes the privately motivated implementation of digital rights aspects (Polatin- Reuben and Wright 2014).

Thirdly, technological sovereignty is relevant. The question of sovereignty in the use of specific, key enabling technologies (KET), is a matter of political and technological debate. It arises from a lack of “free technology” and contradicts a profit-maximizing, (neo-) liberal attitude and calls for alternatives to commercial hardware and software with a focus on the use of open source technologies that form the basis of technological sovereignty (Haché 2014, 164ff). Beltrán builds on this concept and deﬁnes technological sovereignty in a political context as “an anti-establishment practice in the ﬁeld of IT, in order to integrate it in other antiimperialist struggles” (Beltrán 2016, 18). In doing so, she proposes to fundamentally question the approaches of large IT companies and to consider alternatives in order to re-appropriate technological sovereignty.

Other considerations focus on an exclusively technological, i.e. hardware and software-oriented definition. The construction of dedicated submarine cables, territorially localized data routing, national, encrypted mail systems, territorial, local data storage also for cloud services or own encryption technologies are politically motivated and technologically feasible approaches to achieve greater autarky and thus sovereignty (Maurer et al. 2015). The VDE6 distinguishes digital from technological sovereignty by considering aspects of digital sovereignty such as “trust,” “IT security,” and “data security” as well as “access to raw materials,” “access to components” and “access to knowledge” as sub-areas of an overarching technological sovereignty (Illgner 2020). So what does it mean to be technologically sovereign? It is not about completely mastering all technologies and achieving maximum autarky. Rather, it is about understanding and

5 Status Quo of the EU in Digital Sovereignty

It becomes clear that not all European states occupy top positions in digital rankings, although their state sovereignty is very well developed. The fact that some states have fallen behind digitally carries the risk that this will have a negative effect on state sovereignty. The EU heads of state have recognized this and addressed the need for action at the political level. In the context of the EU Council Presidency in 2020, the strengthening of digital sovereignty was raised to the European level, whereby the term “technological sovereignty” introduced above is mostly used here (Bauer and Erixon 2020; Pohle 2020). The need for an action plan has also been recognized at the level of the EU Commission. However, the term is understood and discussed very individually. The different perspectives and interpretations show that digital or technological sovereignty is still not a clearly deﬁned term. According to the research of this paper, no clear deﬁnitions or scientiﬁc concepts can be found in the scientiﬁc literature (Mertz et al. 2016, 48). The concept of sovereignty can be derived historically for European states, based on Europe’s values and formulated and quantified state-specifically in the characteristics of governance. The concept of digitization can also be derived historically. Measurement systems for the digital level of states are basically available.

What is completely missing so far is a quantification of digital sovereignty based on a clear definition of the term and a common understanding in the EU. The measurement should include variables that influence state sovereignty, the degree of digitization of the state, and its ability to sufficiently control possible dependencies. Here it has been shown that there are fundamental dependencies and interdependencies of the EU states. Yet there is no clear national or European strategy for a future positioning of the European states for higher digital sovereignty. In this respect, the currently discussed measures of a European action plan can only have a limited effect.

6 Necessary Steps for the EU on the Way to Increasing Digital Sovereignty

Digital sovereignty of a state can undoubtedly be achieved by becoming autarkic and investing in its own technology development and data security so that it is independent of others. Examples of this are Russia, China, Brazil, and others (Belli 2021). In authoritarian states, a largely territorialized internet policy of isolation in the sense of the sovereign preservation of the state can thus be observed (Belli 2021; Pohle and Thiel 2019). These effects can be seen in Figure 1. The concept of sovereignty of autocratic states is also underpinned with digital sovereignty in order to justify autocratically motivated sovereignty internally and strong economic and regulatory policies externally (Arsène 2021; Budnitsky and Jia 2018; Creemers 2020).

Democratic states define digital sovereignty more in terms of data protection, protection of the interests of their own citizens, and, to some extent, economic aspects (Pohle and Thiel 2019). Technological sovereignty in the approach of national or European autarky, i.e. a policy of digital isolation, cannot be the goal, however, because sovereignty in digitization must go hand in hand in Europe with the sovereignty of the states in the context of Europe’s values. “[T]he ability that Europe must have to make its own decisions in accordance with its own values and rules …” is what Commission President von der Leyen formulates as the basis of digital sovereignty (von der Leyen 2020).

6.1 Definition of Digital Sovereignty

The following definition is therefore proposed for common understanding and further analyses and discussions. A state is digitally sovereign precisely when – its sovereignty is preserved in the sense of a Foucauldian approach of “good governance” based on Europe’s democratic and liberal values, i.e. administrative and governmental work must be carried out sovereignly, – technological digitization by means of key enabling technologies is deﬁned for the respective state, revised if necessary and generally accepted, and – under the aspect of strong data sovereignty, technology sovereignty is also established, so that the technologies used by state organizations are understood and can be controlled or replaced at any time if necessary.

In principle, all forms of autonomy and autarky are conceivable, depending on the technology and the relevance of the technology for “autonomous, good governance.” Characteristics may discussed in the context of the implementation of digital sovereignty. The ability to act involves the following sub-areas (Hoepner et al. 2016, 89): – Deﬁnition of KETs in close cooperation between politics, research and industry; – Development of KETs and corresponding cooperation from research and industry, national, European, and international; – Ability to evaluate technology and its integration into existing ecosystems with a focus on trustworthiness and security; – Risk assessment also of supply chains; – Transparency of system architectures, associated interfaces, the exchange and storage of data; and – Choice of technologies, architectures and suppliers.

An alternative path could therefore be for the EU to specify which KETs are relevant today and in the future, and which agree on a common definition of digital sovereignty. The evaluation should focus on how “good governance” is still possible in the sense of European values and in consideration of multi-stakeholders: exactly which KETs will be relevant will need to be assessed technologically, socially, and politically, and will depend on the interests of individual governmental, intergovernmental and industrial stakeholders. Borrowings from STS and multi-stakeholderism can be made here (DeNardis and Raymond 2013; DeNardis et al. 2020).

6.2 Measurement of Digital Sovereignty

In addition, the status of digital sovereignty of a state should be determined metrically, as this is the only way to set valid and common goals for the EU. A first step for a metric approach including possible input parameters is proposed here. From that, EU-wide or national action plans can be effectively developed. The action plan will influence almost every social, political, and economic area: Education policy, funding policy, investments in start-ups, the digital ecosystem of the EU, the role of politics in the economy and the handling of ICT in administrations, to name a few.

6.3 Five Steps to Strengthen Digital Sovereignty

In order to effectively implement the action plan called for at the political level, there needs to be a clear understanding and a normative model, i.e. measurability, as described above. In order to strengthen the EU’s path towards digital sovereignty, five steps should be initiated, as follows: First, establish a common understanding of what digital sovereignty means. A European understanding of what is meant by digital sovereignty is needed. This should be discussed politically at European and nation-state level and, if necessary, anchored in European legislation. This requires measurable goals. Second, indicators should be found that underpin the new understanding, i.e. quantify the degree of sovereignty and digitization. Indicators can be influencing factors in legislation and the basis for monitoring the European action plan. Third, a quantitative model should be developed to derive a digital sovereignty index. This index would enable the states to position themselves internationally and to set targets in the action plan or in European legislation. Fourth, by comparing measures and states on the basis of the digital sovereignty index, the effects of measures can be identified and possible deficits identified and needs for action developed.

Fifth, recommendations at European or national level can be included in the action plan to increase digital sovereignty.
