Digital sovereignty as control: the regulation of digital ﬁnance in the European Union Shawn Donnellya, Elena Ríos Camachob and Sebastian Heidebrechtc aUniversity of Twente and Leiden University, Enschede, Netherlands; bPolitical Science Munchen, Technische Universitat Munchen, Munich, Germany; cPolitical Science Wien, Universität Wien, Vienna, Austria

ABSTRACT Crypto-ﬁnance is becoming increasingly popular, but the area is still unregulated in many ways. Its innovations promise to bring beneﬁts by decreasing costs and increasing choices for intermediaries and customers. At the same time, and among other things, the breakdown of important exchanges and lawsuits against prominent ﬁgures illustrate potential challenges for investors, ﬁnancial stability, and monetary sovereignty. How does the EU navigate the crypto verse? In this article, we analyse three regulations of the EU’s Digital Finance Package through the lenses of discursive and historical institutionalism to understand when and where concerns about digital sovereignty motivate action. We argue that EU actors aim to regulate digital ﬁnance and retain sovereignty by employing diﬀerent narratives that justify their policy choices. EU regulators upgrade existing regulations to cover digital ﬁnance areas that resemble traditional ﬁnancial services and justify it by existing rationales on investor protection and ﬁnancial stability. Digital ﬁnance poses security challenges that are new to ﬁnancial regulation. EU actors employ a narrative of security threats to increase control in areas traditionally dealt with in cybersecurity.

To explain these policy changes, we apply historical and discursive institutionalist perspectives. We show that the EU justiﬁed its choices, on the one hand, with traditional narratives focused on ﬁnancial stability and investor protection where it could perform upgrades of existing regulations, and on the other hand with new rationales focused on preventing security threats like cyber-attacks in ﬁnancial services. MiCA upgrades existing ﬁnancial market regulation and discourses to continue traditional control of ﬁnancial market risks, from investor protection to ﬁnancial stability, while the DLT pilot fosters market innovation in crypto-asset market infrastructures under the supervision of EU regulators focused on risk. Reference to digital sovereignty is used more strongly to justify new regulatory logics, as in countering cybersecurity threats for digital ﬁnance. Overall, the digital sovereignty narrative helps expand and cement legislative support for new moves. Our results contribute to the growing literature on the EU’s rhetoric and action in terms of digital sovereignty and strategic autonomy (Falkner et al., 2023; Heidebrecht, 2023; Schmitz & Seidl, 2022), and literature on the evolution of European ﬁnancial regulation (Donnelly, 2018; Högenauer et al., 2023; Posner & Quaglia, 2023; Ríos Camacho, 2021).

Digital sovereignty, digital ﬁnance and cybersecurity Digital sovereignty has become a buzzword among EU policy-makers (Obendiek, 2021) and EU research scholars alike (Floridi, 2020; Pohle & Thiel, 2020). Scholarly work on digital sovereignty argued that although EU leaders such as Emmanuel Macron, Ursula von der Leyen or Angela Merkel have embraced the term, it ‘lacks a clear deﬁnition and is deployed inconsistently’ (Roberts et al., 2021, p. 2). Yet, as Lambach and Oppermann (2022) argue, the ambiguity of the concept of digital sovereignty, like that of strategic autonomy (Seidl & Schmitz, 2023), can serve as a ‘coalition magnet’ that enables heterogeneous actors to jointly promote a new policy agenda for diﬀerent reasons. In line with the introduction to this special issue, we identify claims that the EU needs to control digital activity and its diﬀerent layers (like infrastructure, standards, content, or data, see (Chander & Sun, 2021; Floridi, 2020)) as the core purpose around which various actors layer diﬀerent interpretations and policy initiatives. Control implies the ability to inﬂuence and restrict the manufacturing, design, use, and output of digital technologies with hard legal obligations and statutory regulation (Falkner et al., 2023). This article investigates the relative importance of public policy debates and actions motivated by digital sovereignty, versus control of digital ﬁnance based on standard ﬁnancial market regulation. An existing body of

Page 5

literature addresses the implications of digital ﬁnance generally for public policy (Bernards & Campbell-Verduyn, 2019; Langley & Leyshon, 2021). Brown and Piroska (2022) argue that ﬁntech pose risks to society and consumers by ‘proﬁling’ poor households into generators of ﬁnancial assets (Gabor & Brooks, 2017). Others see the entrance of tech-driven private companies as a threat to the crucial payment sector (Brandl & Dieterich, 2023) and point to the relevance of digital ﬁnance in potential ‘infrastructure war’ (de Goede, 2020, p. 351).

However, literature on EU ﬁnancial regulation (Donnelly, 2018; Högenauer et al., 2023; Ríos Camacho, 2021) has not yet dealt with crypto-based ﬁnance or cybersecurity aspects of ﬁnancial regulation. It has focused on explaining the creation and functioning of new institutions and policies such as Banking Union (Howarth & Quaglia, 2016), Capital Markets Union (Braun et al., 2018), the de facto ﬁnancial regulatory sovereignty of the EU when dealing with the United States (Posner & Quaglia, 2023) and the speciﬁcs of post-trade ﬁnancial services after Brexit (Donnelly, 2023; Quaglia & Spendzharova, 2022). Given the EU’s growing interest in identifying opportunities and threats of digital ﬁnance innovations, we need to understand how the EU expands regulatory and supervisory control over digital ﬁnance, which we theorise and empirically demonstrate below.

Digital sovereignty and digital control discourses: understanding regulatory motivations The introduction to this special issue presents a framework to measure discursive and policy change in EU digital policy. Signiﬁcant policy changes are deﬁned as those that change policy substantially through important adjustments of instruments (like the level of ﬁnes), introduce new policies or even provide for new compelling reasons for regulation in the ﬁrst place. Digital sovereignty can also be mapped by the discourse used to motivate these changes, which those institutions and policies embody (Pohle & Thiel, 2020, p. 9), and the way that discourse is used to create and adjust those policies and institutions over time. The authors deﬁne the bar for digital sovereignty as motivations that constitute a ‘new central positive reference point in the policy discourse’ (Falkner et al., 2023, pp. 5–7). These claims challenge the narrative of the ‘free’ internet (Mansell, 2011, p. 27), and ﬁnance outside of government-regulated institutions that crypto enthusiasts support.

We distinguish two major kinds of institutional change and associated shifts in policy narratives in digital ﬁnance with relevance to mapping and explaining whether and why actors strive for something that can be described as digital sovereignty. The ﬁrst kind of institutional change is the upgrade. Upgrades are extensions and adaptations of existing rules,

regulations, authorities, and the paradigms that underpin their mission and mechanisms, relying on discursive continuity and generating incremental institutional and policy change. Control will be central where it previously existed without naming digital sovereignty. We can measure the degree of continuity or change not only in discourse, but in the strength of institutions, legal obligations, and supervision.

Upgrades are consistent with historical institutionalist accounts of policy and institutional development over time. Policy specialists and stakeholders replicate continuity of inherited rules, norms, values and practices as they grapple with new but related challenges that ﬁt old templates, particularly if institutions have performed well in the past. While institutional failure would be expected to foster abrupt reforms (Hesse, 2021), upgrades are incremental changes to law and regulation responding to the evolution of already regulated activity (Capoccia & Kelemen, 2007; Pierson, 2000; Streeck & Thelen, 2005; Verdun, 2015).

The second kind of change is the innovation. Innovations are new institutions, legal rules, and motivations for introducing them. This can happen when old institutions break down, opening existing concepts to contestation and replacement, sometimes with advocacy coalitions pushing for particular choices over other alternatives. It can also happen when a new, exogenous sense of threat to another policy area is perceived as strong and action is urgent.

This sense of urgency for innovation is observable in cybersecurity. It carries over into legal innovations for cybersecurity in ﬁnancial services, both traditional and crypto-based as well. As policy-makers link cybersecurity to digital ﬁnance, new discourse, law, policy, and institutions are established as new expressions of sovereign control in the face of an unprecedented threat. They correspondingly rely more intensively on processes familiar to discursive institutionalists (Schmidt, 2011): policy initiatives motivated by understanding the needs of public policy in the current moment. Discursive institutionalism posits that actors exercise power through narratives that frame situations, justify responses, and persuade others to accept their views as a strategy (Carstensen & Schmidt, 2016, p. 323). EU actors regularly act as policy entrepreneurs, even on issues that are part of the intergovernmental pillar of EU competencies (Brandão & Camisão, 2021). They can use discursive devices strategically to promote their preferences, like invoking the concept of the market (Jabko, 2006), strategic autonomy (Schmitz & Seidl, 2022) and (digital) sovereignty (Bora, 2023, p. 1284) to build support. Discursive institutionalism analyses continuity as well as change but explains change better than historical institutionalism. When inherited institutions and norms do not speak to the risk or threat that lawmakers seek to control, references to new motivations are required to build and hold additional support. Vague concepts can also be used as coalition magnets to shore up political

Page 7

support for novel regulations from non-specialist parliamentarians and governments. We therefore expect more explicit references to combatting threats to digital sovereignty in the ﬁeld of cybersecurity than in ﬁnancial regulation, which has robust institutions to build on. Innovations would be more likely than upgrades to analogue ﬁnancial regulation. A useful distinctionwithin the respective discourses surrounding ﬁnancial services versus cybersecurity is that between risks and threats, and links to concepts of appropriate public policy response that are central to discursive institutionalism. Risks are inherentto all activities, but central to ﬁnance and any market innovation. Risk-based regulation typically seeks to calibrate the degree of legal obligations and supervision to the probability and impact of risk and failure from market activity. Threats, however, imply vulnerability rather than simply risk, requiring state regulators to insist on higher standards, legal obligations, and regulatory powers, even where these need to be built from scratch, and/ or intrude on other policy areas (Backman, 2023, p. 91). Another key diﬀerence is that threats imply persistent, malicious intent of adversaries that requires readiness and resilience, rather than risks of failure arising from accidents, market practice or regulatory design. While the EU’s tradition to deter cyber threats goes also back to the 1980s (Carrapico & Farrand, 2020; Liebetrau, 2023), extending that threat to digital ﬁnance is a regulatory innovation. The three regulations studied below diﬀer in terms of their legal tradition and the objectives they aim to achieve. The MiCA and the DLT Pilot focus on the mitigation of traditional risks arising from new crypto operations, including monetary sovereignty and ﬁnancial stability. By contrast, DORA is geared towards ensuring resilience of ﬁnance as critical infrastructure. We therefore expect that policy-makers build on traditional ﬁnancial regulation and supervision aiming to reduce risks of crypto-ﬁnance when dealing with MiCA at the DLT Pilot. The options range from traditional statutory supervision (MiCA) where risk perceptions are strong and parallels between digital and regular ﬁnance are clear, to supervised self-regulation where greater uncertainty prevails about regulatory feasibility (DLT Pilot). The Pilot permits market participants to prove that they can create private infrastructure to manage traditional market and operational risks taking place in the cryptoverse. We expect that references to digital sovereignty are more likely to be visible when creating a new link between cybersecurity as threat prevention and the resilience of digital ﬁnance. This will imply signiﬁcant policy changes to meet new perceived threats.

Empirical strategy We conduct a theory-guided congruence analysis. The approach can be described as the drawing of inferences from the (non-)congruence of concrete observations with speciﬁc theoretical expectations from abstract theories for understanding the cases under study (Blatter & Blume, 2008,

p. 325). We demonstrate policy change and trace the justiﬁcations that accompanied these changes by demonstrating the process of changes over time (Bennet & George, 2005). Thus, we ﬁrst describe the substantive content of the policy area addressed and the status quo ante (before the adoption of the EU’s digital ﬁnance package). Secondly, we examine important regulatory elements like the introduction of harmonised legal obligations and EU agencies with strong regulatory powers and vertical coordination over national competent authorities and the positions of important policy actors on these elements. Third, we elaborate on how diﬀerent actors promoted these changes by evoking diﬀerent discursive strategies that ultimately enabled policy changes. This allows us ﬁnally to test for congruence with our theoretically derived expectations and to situate our ﬁndings in the context of this special issues framework on the relationship of discursive and policy changes towards digital sovereignty. We selected inﬂuential cases of EU regulation of digital ﬁnance. While inﬂuential cases are typically not representative of a larger sample of EU digital ﬁnancial regulation we choose inﬂuential cases because we expect that they can show more signiﬁcant empirical eﬀects (Seawright & Gerring, 2008), which allows us to draw conclusions on the development of EU digital ﬁnance regulation more generally. We examine the three policy-sub-areas addressed by the regulations of the EU’s Digital Finance Package, issued on 24 September 2020 by the Commission. The package contains three following three regulations: MiCA (Regulation (EU) 2023/1114), DLT-Pilot (Regulation (EU) 2022/858), and DORA (Regulation (EU) 2022/858). All three regulations have the Single Market Article 114 as their legal basis, while they diﬀer substantially in terms of regulatory approach and content (see below). In order to maximise observation points (Geddes, 2003), we use a snowball approach starting with oﬃcial legislative and position papers to identify relevant additional data in the form of statements, speeches, position papers, press releases of important actors: the European Supervisory Authorities (ESAs), the European Central Bank (ECB), the European Commission, Council, Parliament and industry players. We also analysed the parliamentary debates about the three regulations (available on the EUR-Lex website) and statements of leading Commissioners, like Margrethe Vestager, responsible for making ‘Europe Fit for the Digital Age’. We further conducted six topic-centred interviews for triangulation purposes. We assured all interviewees strict conﬁdentiality so that we provide only anonymised information about the interviews in Appendix 1.

Upgrades in EU digital ﬁnance regulation: crypto-assets and market infrastructure EU regulation of crypto-assets and infrastructure is primarily contained in the MiCA Regulation adopted in May 2023. It eﬀectively upgrades previous

Page 9

regulations designed to ensure regulatory control of ﬁnancial markets with ﬁnancial stability and/or investor protection in mind and applies it to crypto-assets. MiCA’s most prominent predecessor is the Market in Financial Instruments Directive (MiFID) II of 2014, which upgrades and consolidates multiple directives governing actors and activity in securities markets with ﬁnancial stability and investor protection risks in mind. MiCA also addresses risk-oriented standards for post-trade ﬁnancial services that upgrade provisions of the European Markets Infrastructure Regulation (EMIR) for traditional ﬁnancial services companies. Parallel to MiCA is a pilot project on whether market participants can manage risks of trading and clearing of crypto-assets that EU law deﬁnes as securities, based on supervised self-regulation of market participants, rather than statutory regulation. MiCA largely reﬂects historical institutionalist expectations. EU actors’ narratives based on innovation coupled with risk reduction and ﬁnancial stability show discursive continuity in EU ﬁnancial market regulation. Occasional reference to monetary sovereignty, which goes beyond the traditional focus on ﬁnancial markets, is used additionally as a coalition magnet to build majorities rather than design regulation. MiCA represents a comprehensive, although incremental, change in EU ﬁnancial market regulation given its hard legal obligations and strong supervision, reﬂecting existing ﬁnancial market law. Thus, the EU maintains itself as a rule-maker and sovereign in ﬁnancial market regulation by implementing upgrades related to cryptobased ﬁnance without focusing on digital sovereignty explicitly.

Legislating the markets in crypto-assets regulation The 2018 Commission’s FinTech Action Plan contained its deﬁnition of the main risks and challenges of crypto-assets, paralleling assessments by the Financial Stability Board (FSB). It noted that existing regulations failed to cover crypto-assets suﬃciently. Crypto-assets were subject to high volatility, fraud, operational weaknesses, and crypto-asset exchange vulnerabilities (European Commission, 2018, p. 3) that might pose risks to ﬁnancial stability (Financial Stability Board, 2020, p. 1; 2022). The European Supervisory Authorities in ﬁnancial services (ESAs), especially the European Banking Authority (EBA), played a key role in supporting the Commission’s drive to standardise the deﬁnition of crypto-assets across EU Member States and the identiﬁcation of their risks and opportunities given their technological novelty. The ESAs started warning about the risks of buying or holding crypto-assets in 2014 (European Banking Authority, 2014, p. 46), and supported the drive to regulate in MiCA (European Supervisory Authorities, 2022).

Following the ESAs’ advice, the Commission proposal of 24 September 2020 (European Commission, 2020d) considered that most crypto-assets were not securities, and therefore would fall outside existing EU legislation

The Commission stressed that the EU needed to extend existing securities and macroprudential regulation to ‘instil appropriate levels of consumer and investor protection and market integrity […]’ and ‘safeguards to address potential risks to ﬁnancial stability and orderly monetary policy’ (European Commission, 2020d, pp. 2–3). Historical arguments of what to regulate and not prevailed over ideas about digital sovereignty.

MiCA also adopted inherited capital markets discourse that poor regulation and divergent national rules hurt innovation and competition, preventing the proper functioning of the Single Market. In other words, innovation and regulation go together. In her 2019 mission letter to Commissioner Dombrovskis, Commission President Ursula von der Leyen addressed not only risks cryptoassets may pose but called for ‘a common approach with Member States on cryptocurrencies […] to make the most of the opportunities they create … ’ (von der Leyen, 2019, p. 6). A 2019 joint declaration of the Council and the Commission concurred, stating that ‘technological innovation can produce great economic beneﬁts for the ﬁnancial sector’ (Council of the European Union, 2019). Market actors urged an innovation-friendly approach to EU digital ﬁnancial regulation that was technology-neutral, innovation-friendly, and reduced fragmentation of the Single Market for digital ﬁnancial services (Association for Financial Markets in Europe, 2020a; International Association for Trusted Blockchain Applications, 2021).

Meanwhile, the Council reiterated in 2021 that the lack of an EU-wide regulatory framework for crypto-assets could generate arbitrage and an unwelcome regulatory race to the bottom, creating risks of market failure. It therefore recommended that the EBA and the European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA) address the classiﬁcation of crypto-assets, while the ECB should provide the National Competent Authorities (NCAs) with an opinion on the risks for payment systems and monetary sovereignty (Council of the European Union, 2021b, p. 22).

In addition to these established arguments in the single market debate, MiCA represented an eﬀort to alleviate the consequences that the rise and widespread adoption of ‘global stablecoins’ may generate in the European market (Interview VI). Stablecoins are essentially crypto-assets that are pegged to a commodity or currency. In 2019, Facebook announced its plans to issue its own stablecoin through the Diem Association (formerly the Libra Association). Given Facebook’s large number of users, its stablecoin could lead to the global adoption of stablecoins. This caused concerns among EU legislators and supervisors about the EU’s monetary sovereignty. Both the Council and the Commission agreed that once a ‘stablecoin’ reached signiﬁcant scale, it could create ‘new potential risks to monetary sovereignty, monetary policy, the safety and eﬃciency of payment systems, ﬁnancial stability, and fair competition’ (Council of the European Union,

Page 11

2019). Both institutions underlined the necessity to safeguard monetary sovereignty (ensuring the euro remained the primary currency) if global stablecoins became increasingly accepted (European Commission, 2020a,

p. 9)(Council of the European Union, 2021b, p. 5). Within Parliament, debates on the Digital Finance Strategy referenced challenges to monetary sovereignty from stablecoins (European Parliament, 2020a), while its 2020 resolution on digital ﬁnance addressed issues of ﬁnancial stability and eﬀective monetary policy, without referring to (monetary) sovereignty (European Parliament, 2020b). Supervisors, like the European Insurance and Occupational Pensions Authority, underlined in a 2021 discussion paper that Libra could interfere with monetary policy and monetary sovereignty if it reached a signiﬁcant scale and a large volume of domestic payments (European Insurance and Occupational Pensions Authority, 2021).

The adopted MiCA was a clear move towards more regulatory control of digital ﬁnance based on well-known risks. It envisaged the ﬁrst EU-wide regulatory framework for crypto-assets, the issuers, and crypto-asset service providers (CASPs), thereby for the ﬁrst time ensuring regulatory coverage of crypto-assets based on traditional regulation. MiCA created strict requirements for issuers of crypto-assets in Europe and CASPs. CASPs comprise trading venues or crypto exchanges, and custodian wallet providers (MiCA- R., Art. 3 (16)). Examples include that all issuers of crypto-assets have to draft ‘crypto-asset white papers’, that include disclosure, conduct, and liability rules (e.g., MiCA-R, Rec. 24), and then beneﬁt from a European passport for their products (permission to solicit investment throughout the Single Market). All CASPs are subject to strict minimum capital requirements (MiCA-R, Art. 67), and must comply with governance and conduct requirements (MiCA-R, Art. 68). Paralleling provisions on central counterparties under EMIR, CASPs must cover operational risks by protecting users’ wallets assuming liability if they lose investors’ crypto-assets (MiCA-R, Art. 70). MiCA classiﬁed crypto-assets into three main categories: Crypto-assets and utility tokens, asset-referenced tokens (ARTs), and e-money tokens, and deﬁnes respective regulatory and supervisory obligations ‘depending on the risks they entail’ (MiCA-R, Rec. 18). According to MiCA, ARTs and emoney tokens can pose challenges for ﬁnancial stability, monetary policy transmission or monetary sovereignty (e.g., MiCA-R, Rec. 59). Such ‘stablecoins’ reference, other assets including currencies to maximise stability, whereas an e-money tokens reference one single ﬁat currency. As they can be used by many holders and replace the euro as means of payment, their use could raise speciﬁc challenges: ﬁnancial stability, monetary policy transmission or monetary sovereignty.

To meet these risks, MiCA enshrined stronger regulatory oversight for ARTs and e-money tokens from issuers outside the EU or referencing currencies than the euro (e.g., MiCA-R., Rec. 45, 104). Issuers of ARTs and CASPs are

further obliged to have a registered oﬃce in the EU to guarantee their proper supervision (MiCA-R, Rec. 42, 74). Issuers of e-money tokens must be authorised as a credit institution or as an e-money institution under the E-Money Directive (Directive 2009/110/EC). In other words, MiCA upgrades existing EU regulation to control the crypto-asset market without stiﬂing innovation already allowed. The Commission, Council and EP agreed on the need to ensure that stablecoins remain subject to monitoring and control for traditional purposes. They shared the concern that once ARTs became ‘signiﬁcant’ (Council of the European Union, 2021b; European Commission, 2020d; European Parliament, 2022), they could be used as a means of exchange and to make large volumes of payment transactions, which could threaten monetary sovereignty. As a response, the EBA will supervise all ‘signiﬁcant’ stablecoins (MiCA-R, Art. 43 (7); Art. 56 (6)) that fulﬁl a number of quantitative criteria, such as when 10 million or more entities hold the ART or its aggregate value (market capitalisation) is higher than EUR 5 million (MiCA-R, Art. 43). Conversely, MiCA treats e-money tokens as electronic money (MiCA-R, Art. 48 (2)), so that they become subject to national competent authority supervision under existing law (E-Money Directive), unless they are ‘signiﬁcant’ and thus supervised by the EBA. Because ARTs are not regulated by the E-Money Directive, and have broader applicability, as in the case of Libra, they are seen to pose greater potential risks to the smooth operation of payment systems using the euro, monetary policy transmission and monetary sovereignty. The Parliament and the Council accordingly supported the proposal that the ECB have authorisation powers over issuers of signiﬁcant ARTs (MiCA-R., Art. 21(4)) (Council of the European Union, 2021b, p. 117; European Parliament, 2022,

p. 51).

MiCA’s regulatory structure therefore resembles traditional ﬁnancial supervision, with EU oversight reserved for the riskiest entities and activities, and national authorities doing the rest. In this regard, national authorities will be responsible for carrying out the supervisory functions and duties provided for in MiCA for ensuring enforcement (MiCA-R, Art. 93). This parallels traditional ﬁnancial supervision in the Single Supervisory Mechanism (SSM), where supervisory functions for the largest and riskiest entities fall to the ECB (Högenauer et al., 2023). MiCA parallels the distribution of tasks between the member state and the supranational level based on size and risk. The EBA supervises the most ‘signiﬁcant’ stablecoins that pose risks to the market, the functioning of the euro, and its monetary sovereignty. CASPs that have more than 15 million active users are classiﬁed as ‘signiﬁcant crypto-assets service providers’ and will be supervised by the national authorities (MiCA-R, Art. 85). However, the European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA) will have an ‘intervention power’ to ban or limit the provision of crypto-asset services by the CASPs in case of threats to market integrity, investor protection or ﬁnancial stability (MiCA-R, Art. 103).

In line with our theoretical expectations, MiCA replicates the EU’s approach to balancing the potential beneﬁts and risks of ﬁnancial markets, upgraded to cover the use and industry activities of crypto-assets. EU actors’ narratives based on innovation coupled with risk reduction and ﬁnancial stability show discursive continuity with EU ﬁnancial market regulation. Its hard legal obligations build on existing regulations and powers. It also creates a supervisory framework that resembles that of post-2008 ﬁnancial regulation in Europe, dividing supervision between EU authorities (ECB and ESAs) for the most signiﬁcant entities, and national authorities for the less signiﬁcant ones.

The DLT Pilot in post-trade services In addition to promoting investor interests and ﬁnancial stability through transparency, oversight and authorisation, MiCA also attempts to address provisions provided by post-trade services designed to ensure ﬁnancial stability. The European Market Infrastructure Regulation (EMIR) of 2012 mandated central counterparties (CCPs), central security depositories (CSDs) and trade repositories for pre-crypto ﬁnancial market transactions. CCPs prevent market collapse by ensuring that parties to contracts get paid, CSDs provide deposit facilities, and trade repositories register data on ownership and transactions that large trading markets require to function. The description of CASPs in MiCA parallels these functions, indicating an upgrade from EMIR. The DLT Pilot allows private actors to construct rules and institutions for trading and post-trade services in crypto-assets under the guidance of EU supervisors, just as the 2012 EMIR provisions did. This continues a belief that market participants are best suited to designing and operating infrastructure within limits designed to contain the risk of system failure. CASPs, like clearing houses, central counterparties, and trade repositories under EMIR, are provisionally allowed to retain ownership and control of the process to promote innovation and expertise, while supervisors seek to ensure that procedures, standards, and mechanisms for addressing payment and insolvency problems reasonably contain ﬁnancial stability risks. The Commission’s proposal underscored the need for suitable market infrastructures for buying and selling crypto-assets in both primary ﬁnancial markets (where new instruments would be bought) and secondary markets that ensure investor, consumer and market safety. Without this, crypto-asset markets could not be expected to develop. At the same time, there were concerns that private activity could circumvent market infrastructure regulations (EMIR) mandating features to guarantee payments (central counterparties), deposits (CSD requirements) and reporting applicable to traditional ﬁnancial service providers (European Commission, 2020b). Safe innovation replicating traditional measures was the goal.

As the guardian of monetary and systemic ﬁnancial stability in the euro area, the ECB’s stance on the pilot project was of particular importance. It supported the DLT Pilot as a means of fostering innovation with potential eﬃciency beneﬁts in the ﬁnancial system, particularly for cross-border payments. However, it also supported limitations that would help contain the ﬁnancial stability risks of the experiment. The ECB encouraged new regulatory conditions on the original issuers of the securities to ensure that DLT-platforms would not serve as a magnet for poor-quality assets (junk) that could create bubbles and unleash crashes (European Central Bank, 2021, p. 6). It also foresaw potentially problematic compliance with the Capital Requirements Directive (Directive 2013/36/EU) on reserve holdings without a centralised party involved.

The ECB saw it as essential for ﬁnancial stability that EU licensing and capital requirements rules for credit institutions be updated to include DLT operators rather than relaxed. Financial market infrastructures would also need to ﬁnd a way to minimise and control credit and liquidity risks (European Central Bank, 2021, p. 7). The ECB also underscored that the pilot should address investor protection in case of bankruptcy and the removal of assets from trading (European Central Bank, 2021, p. 9). Finally, it urged analysis of potential risks to monetary policy arising from increased payment settlement using bank money and e-tokens without using central bank money (European Central Bank, 2021, p. 3). Overall, the ECB promoted the upgrading of existing rules based on established narratives around ﬁnancial regulation.

The pilot legislation passed through Parliament and Council in one reading. The Committee on Economic and Monetary Aﬀairs underlined that the pilot should not circumvent or undermine existing supervisory control and regulation of traditional market infrastructures, but that it should also allow a wider range of instruments (including sovereign bonds) and institutional investors (European Parliament, 2021a). The Council accepted the proposal with the Parliament’s observations without any need for changes. The DLT Pilot’s agreed rules extend pre-digital supervision to crypto by placing selected multilateral trading facilities (exchanges) and securities settlement systems under the supervision of ESMA and national counterparts (DLT-Pilot R, Art. 12). Operators need to be registered and authorised for the purposes of existing regulation, particularly the Market in Financial Instruments Directive (MiFID) covering transparency and investor protection; EMIR; and the Central Securities Depositories Regulation (CSDR, Regulation EU/909/2014), which sets out the minimum capital standards and operating requirements for central security depositories for traditional ﬁnancial instruments. Whereas central security depositories would normally be required to register the transfer of securities on a central platform, the pilot allows these authorised companies to allow the transfer of securities in a

Page 15

decentralised manner using DLT instead. This simpliﬁes procedures and contains transaction costs while still keeping track of who owns the securities. To limit the scope of the pilot, crypto-assets that are categorised as commodities or derived from them are not eligible for trade (DLT-Pilot R, Art. 3). This excludes physical commodity futures (tokens representing raw materials, for example) and non-fungible tokens. Instead, assets must be eligible securities, i.e., based on a company’s business activities, paralleling traditional securities law. However, the pilot is also limited to securities that are not very liquid, meaning there is not much trading in them. This contains potential risks if the pilot fails. Central securities depository operators will not have to register DLT transactions on their own books, relying on decentralised records instead. Part of the pilot is ﬁguring out how to ensure that decentralised ownership, trading, custody and reporting are as safe and secure as traditional trading and post-trade services regulated by EMIR. In addition to ﬁnancial stability, the DLT Pilot also mandates cybersecurity for digital infrastructure, as set out in DORA (below).

Another restriction on the pilot mirroring concerns of the ECB is a preference for settlement in central bank or bank money over payment in crypto-assets. Settlement in crypto-coins and e-money tokens as deﬁned and regulated in MiCA remains possible however, since settlement in traditional money as the ECB prefers collides with the ambition of DLT-based crypto transactions. To hedge against problems, traditional supervisors (ESMA and NCAs) have the authority to revoke authorisation, while market infrastructure companies have a duty of cooperation with competent authorities (DLT-Pilot R, Art. 11). Overall, the DLT pilot opens the door to meaningful experimentation with crypto-based market infrastructure. However, concerns of the Commission, of the ECB, of ESMA and of the European Parliament about traditional risks to the integrity and safety of ﬁnancial markets remain strong. MiCA replicates much of EMIR, and the Pilot mirrors, references and upgrades much of the EU’s recent advances in reclaiming regulatory control over ﬁnancial markets, together with market participants. The Pilot promotes innovation through supervised trials and takes existing supervisors and regulations as a baseline and subjects companies to these in the interest of ﬁnancial stability, as well as investor and consumer protection.

Thus, we observe a signiﬁcant policy upgrade that aims to control cryptoassets and market infrastructures rather than allowing them to escape public oversight and regulatory control for the pure sake of innovation. These arguments suﬃce, without referencing digital sovereignty directly. The substance is one of strengthened regulatory control, but digital sovereignty is not a central reference point in the discourse of the most relevant actors. It is just one of the reference points among others such as regulatory fragmentation, protection of consumers and investors, market integrity, and ﬁnancial stability.

Cybersecurity for ﬁnance: digital sovereignty in the operational resilience act Before the EU’s digital ﬁnance package, the consideration of cyber threats was not part of ﬁnancial regulation. By contrast, in DORA, EU policy-makers stressed that ‘increased digitalisation and interconnectedness also amplify ICT risk, making society as a whole, and the ﬁnancial system in particular, more vulnerable to cyber threats or ICT disruptions’ (DORA-R, Rec. 1). DORA brings recent developments from the area of cybersecurity to ﬁnancial regulation. The 2019 Cybersecurity Act (Regulation (EU) 2019/881), crystallized the EU’s regulation and governance of cybersecurity into a new area of robust legal requirements and institutional powers. It supplanted the loose coordination of national authorities and laws with a harmonised regime entailing both vertical and horizontal coordination between the EU Agency for Cybersecurity (ENISA), national counterparts, and actors like the more than 700 Computer Security Incident Response Teams. The Cybersecurity Act also provides the deﬁnition of cyber threats (Cybersecurity Act, Art. 2 (8)), namely as ‘any potential circumstance, event or action that could damage, disrupt or otherwise adversely impact network and information systems, the users of such systems and other person’. With DORA, concerns about such cyber threats enter the policy discourse of digital ﬁnancial regulation. DORA focuses on cybersecurity in the cryptoverse rather than ﬁnancial transactions and risks. While the EU’s post-2008 ﬁnancial reforms led to the creation of single rulebooks in banking and securities to regulate ﬁnancial risks linked to ﬁnancial services, they did not cover digital operational resilience and, therefore, did not address the risks that arise from digitalisation. Neither the EU’s ﬁrst cybersecurity law, the Network and Information Security (NIS) Directive of 2016 (Directive (EU) 2016/1148), nor the Cybersecurity Act addressed ﬁnancial services directly. However, the EU addressed this gap in part through the NIS 2 Directive (Directive (EU) 2022/2555) by including more detailed provisions and supervision for ﬁnancial services, and more speciﬁcally through DORA. DORA requires ﬁnancial entities to have a sound, comprehensive and well-documented ICT risk management framework as part of their overall risk management system (DORA-R, Art. 6 (1)) in response to potential threats to the EU’s critical infrastructure, which includes ﬁnancial services.

Some elements in the policy discourse about DORA resembled the wellknown logic in the context of Single Market legislation and have Article 114 as its legal basis. The Commission stressed the need for harmonisation, without which the Single Market freedoms for ﬁnancial services could be hampered (European Commission, 2020c, p. 2). Financial industry associations accepted that the Single Market could beneﬁt from harmonised ICT security governance arrangements to prevent threats like fraud, loss or

Page 17

theft (Association for Financial Markets in Europe, 2020b). The Council’s negotiating position concurred and stated that the harmonisation of an ICT risk framework through DORA would contribute to ‘the smooth functioning of the single market’ (Council of the European Union, 2021c, p. 7). The Parliament’s rapporteur, Billy Kelleher (Renew, Ireland), advocated that DORA’s provisions should not hamper the competitiveness and attractiveness of the EU with requirements not required elsewhere (European Parliament, 2021b,

p. 83). For him, the primary objective was to ensure that ‘the EU must become a key player on digital ﬁnance’ (European Parliament, 2021b). While references to established arguments in terms of innovation and ﬁnancial stability of the ﬁnancial services were present, we observed more security-related arguments in the DORA policy discourse. These stress elements like international challenges, systemic risks that arise because of dependencies on a few central counterparties (which is mostly referring to big technology companies that are based outside of the EU), and cyber threats (European Parliament, 2021b).

The desire to defend sovereignty against foreign actors is also addressed in concerns about systemic risks attached to big tech cloud services. In line with the Commission’s proposal, the Council and the EP argued that ‘the broader issue of counteracting systemic risk which may be triggered by the ﬁnancial sector’s exposure to a limited number of critical ICT third-party service providers is barely addressed by Union legislation’ (Council of the European Union, 2022b). Such critical ICT providers, a few important big technology companies, oﬀer services like cloud computing infrastructures on which many digital ﬁnancial services rely. Actors like the ECB, which support the development of digital ﬁnance in the EU (European Central Bank, 2020, p. 2), were also concerned about concentration. ‘[W]hen EU ﬁnancial service providers increasingly rely on (critical) services oﬀered to them by non-EU technology ﬁrms, EU sovereignty with regard to those services (e.g., the ability to regulate and supervise those non-EU service providers) decreases’ (European Central Bank, 2020, p. 28, italics by authors).

While systemic risks, though in a very diﬀerent fashion, were part of the traditional ﬁnancial regulatory discourse, the very prominent rhetoric relating to defence and threats around DORA is new to ﬁnancial regulation. For example, the Commission justiﬁed its proposal by highlighting the issue of cyber threats, and aimed to promote ‘ﬁnancial entities’ defensive capabilities and threat detection techniques (European Commission, 2020c, p. 11). The rhetoric of threats was widely shared, particularly by the Council, which justiﬁed its position to ensure that all ﬁrms ‘can withstand all types of ICTrelated disruptions and threats, in order to prevent and mitigate cyber threats’ (Council of the European Union, 2021a, 2022a). The ESAs were concerned early about the risks that stem from non-harmonisation of rules and ICT dependencies of EU ﬁrms, including those with

Page 18

2242

S. DONNELLY ET AL.

foreign technology ﬁrms. The EBA called for an EU-wide harmonised framework (European Banking Authority, 2018, p. 25) and argued that ‘cybersecurity measures need to be applied throughout the ﬁnancial system, in particular where multiple entities interconnect’ (European Banking Authority, 2018,

p. 23). The cross-cutting nature of ICT risks and the necessity for a harmonised EU legal framework was emphasised in a joint technical advice in 2019 in which all three ESAs argued that there was a lack of ‘explicit references to ICT and cybersecurity risk’ […] so that ‘every relevant entity should be subject to general requirements on governance of ICT, including cybersecurity, to ensure safe provision of regulated services’ (European Supervisory Authorities, 2019, p. 4).

DORA covers almost the entire ﬁnancial sector, including CASPs, and ﬁve important areas for digital operational resilience: ICT risk management, incident reporting, testing, third-party risk, and information sharing (DORA-R, Art. 2). DORA’s supervisory structure widely parallels the supervisory structures in traditional ﬁnancial regulation, so that the competent authorities that supervise entities in accordance with existing ﬁnancial regulations also become responsible for supervising the operational resilience provisions of DORA (DORA-R, Art. 46). For example, most credit institutions are supervised by the national competent authorities (NCAs) and only by the ECB where they are classiﬁed as signiﬁcantly important entities in accordance with the SSM Regulation (DORA, Art. 46 (a)).

The question of coordination and cooperation was crucial. The ESAs together with the EU Cybersecurity Agency ENISA develop common draft regulatory technical standards for further harmonisation of ICT risk management tools, methods, processes, and policies (DORA-R, Art. 15). In addition to the NCAs, the ESAs and ENISA cooperate with the ECB as the supervisor of large banks in the euro area. Because the system is widely decentralised, DORA tasks the ESAs with preparing, in consultation with the ECB and ENISA, a joint report exploring the feasibility of setting up a single EU Hub (DORA-R, Art. 21). By centralising information, such an EU Hub should improve cooperation against cyber incidents and threats. In addition to this, a key novelty of DORA is to bring critical ICT providers into ﬁnancial supervision. For this purpose, the ESA that is responsible for overseeing most of the ﬁnancial institutions that use the services of the critical ICT provider will be designated ‘lead overseer’ (DORA-R, Art. 31(1)). Lead overseers will assess whether critical providers have suﬃcient arrangements to manage ICT risks (DORA-R, Art. 33) and signiﬁcant powers to ensure supervision, including the conduct of general investigations and inspections in and outside of the EU (DORA-R, Art. 35-36). The lead overseer may impose a periodic penalty payment to coerce the critical providers (DORA-R, Art. 50-51). DORA is a case of innovation in ﬁnancial market regulation based on new responsibilities and driven by perception of threat, linked to concerns about

Page 19

ﬁnance as critical infrastructure. Innovations in DORA are reﬂected in both EU actors’ discourse and the ﬁnal policy and institutional changes. In line with our expectations, we observe some discursive discontinuity in ﬁnancial market regulation because ICT risks and security threats are now considered part of ﬁnancial market regulation in Europe. There is no signiﬁcant reference directly to digital sovereignty. However, DORA needs to be seen in a policy discussion involving all the major decision-making and regulatory institutions that seek to ensure that digital ﬁnancial services are regulated from inside the EU, and prepared to meet cybersecurity attacks. They promoted policy choices by referring to narratives that are new to ﬁnancial regulative discourse and that highlight in many respects threats more than risks and originate from the tradition of cybersecurity (and not ﬁnancial regulation). DORA also constitutes a signiﬁcant policy change for cyber security and ﬁnancial market regulation. While cyber security elements are new to ﬁnancial regulation, DORA also entails elements that are new for cybersecurity. The 2019 Cybersecurity Act (and the NIS 1 and 2 Directives) does not address the issue of critical third-party service providers and provisions for their supranational supervision through ENISA. By contrast, this is the case in DORA which establishes for the ﬁrst time these powers for the ESAs by designating them as lead overseers of critical providers.

Discussion and conclusion This paper examined three legislative components of the EU’s Digital Finance Package to shed light on whether digital sovereignty is a motivating factor in how Europe views and regulates digital ﬁnance, with a focus on crypto-assets. Rather than undertake a narrow search for overt narratives of digital sovereignty, we examined concrete moves towards strengthened regulatory control. This constituted whether EU institutions were intent on stronger regulating digital ﬁnance and crypto-assets in particular, why they did so, and how they designed those policies and institutions. In this way, we could speak to the special issue’s framework of determining the direction and degree of moves to extend European control over the digital world, even in the absence of speciﬁc references to digital sovereignty. We covered rules governing the issuance and trading of crypto-assets (the Markets in Crypto-Assets Directive), on providing market infrastructure to ensure that trading could take place safely (the DLT Pilot), and for ensuring that all these activities would have high cybersecurity provisions (Digital Operational Resilience Act).

Our ﬁndings show two things. First, EU actors in the ﬁnancial services sector exist in an environment where most issues, institutions, laws and paradigms about what the EU is there to do and the challenges it faces are inherited from existing ﬁnancial market regulation. Control is central, problems

and solutions are well-understood as risks that can be managed, and digital regulation is modelled on upgrades of pre-digital versions without an explicit reference to digital sovereignty. The EU extends its control, and one could say digital sovereignty, but at best, references to digital sovereignty are muted, and at best coalition magnets to garner legislative support from political generalists, having no impact on the legislation itself. The form and content of EU law and regulation is ﬁrmly built on historical principles, institutions and legislation. EU actors intend to be as strong in the digital realm as thy are in the traditional one, aligning digital control with traditional regulation of ﬁnancial markets.

Second, EU actors advocating for cybersecurity in digital ﬁnance exist in an environment where most issues, institutions, laws and paradigms about what the EU is there to do and the challenges it faces are new and diﬀerent from anything the ﬁnancial sector has confronted before. Regulatory control is again central, but accompanied by a sense of threat and malicious intent that requires urgent, mandatory and robust preparation in an area unfamiliar to ﬁnancial services companies and regulators, and that requires cross-overs between cybersecurity and ﬁnancial services regulators. This makes ﬁnancial services more robust and resilient by mandating and mainstreaming cybersecurity measures into the activity of both European companies and supervisory authorities. These innovations took place due to discursive narratives around response to threats that rallied support for this new extension of EU control over cybersecurity in ﬁnancial services as critical infrastructure. This combination continues to the present day in the European Commission’s (2023) newly released Economic Security Strategy, which goes even further to identify ﬁnancial services as critical infrastructure, and protection of critical infrastructure from inter alia cybersecurity attacks as a matter of national security. This is a novum for the EU. Even without this last move by the Commission, the three regulations studied here constitute the ﬁrst attempt globally to comprehensively regulate crypto-assets and set-up a supervisory structure for the cryptoverse, so that risks are regulated and supervised as strongly as in the traditional realm.

Within the debate about digital sovereignty, and within this special issue we show signiﬁcant advances in regulatory control of digital ﬁnance, particularly of crypto markets. The changes are simultaneously strong and inconspicuous, extending control without hammering on digital sovereignty as a motivator. We see the EU continue to permit crypto market participants to pursue innovation and provide market infrastructure along pre-crypto patterns; we see sweeping new responsibilities for crypto companies and supervisors; and we see innovative new links between ﬁnancial regulation and cybersecurity designed to marry innovation, risk management and security in ways found nowhere else. Thus, our results also indicate an intrusion of a security-related discourse to other EU policy areas. In this regard, our

Page 21

results suggest the increasingly important security discourse (Backman, 2023; Carrapico & Farrand, 2020) will gain track in EU ﬁnancial regulation, with longer term consequences for the EU.

From here, our ﬁndings point to questions for further research about how eﬀective these regulatory incursions into the cryptoverse will prove to be in practice, and to what extent EU regulations spread to the United States and other signiﬁcant markets (Bradford, 2020). This follows since the EU eﬀectively is trying to regulate not only EU companies, but others like American cloud service providers (Brandl & Dieterich, 2023; Langley & Leyshon, 2021).
