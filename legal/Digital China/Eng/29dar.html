Data Autonomy: Recalibrating Strategic Autonomy and Digital Sovereignty

Oskar J. GSTREIN*

The datafication of society comes with a recalibration of power structures. European states can no longer depend on territorial control to govern (cyber)space, powerful corporations own essential cloud infrastructure, state actors denying the importance of democracy and human rights have crucial roles in supply chains, and users are left without choice when using digital services. This article proposes data autonomy as a value-based framework, in response to this ongoing powershift undermining the European Union’s strategic autonomy. In contrast to debates around digital sovereignty and international data flows in data protection law, data autonomy aspires to establish a framework that puts human dignity at its core. Essentially, data autonomy is based on informational self-determination, yet expands it in three dimensions: First, informational selfdetermination remains limited to the citizen-state relationship. The scope of data autonomy expands to private actors, in cases where those are particularly powerful. Secondly, data autonomy expands beyond individual rights and duties, including organizational autonomy as an enabler for individual autonomy. Thirdly, data autonomy addresses harmful inferences resulting from the use of systems based on machine learning or artificial intelligence. Therefore, it is not limited to risks stemming from statically labelled data (e.g., data stored in databases).

Keywords: Strategic Autonomy, Human Dignity, Digital Sovereignty, Governance, Datafication, Data Ownership, Data Governance, Data Protection, Privacy, Informational Self-Determination

To consider this question two basic concepts need to be defined. First, scholars such as Helwig and Sinkkonen propose to approach strategic autonomy by considering four waves of politically and institutionally driven European discourses. Going back to the 1990s, the concept is routed in the desire for more European military strategic independence from the United States (US). The crises in Libya, Syria and Ukraine continue to highlight this dependence in the security sector through the 2010s until today. Additionally, the presidency of Donald Trump with a severe disruption of the north-transatlantic relationship, as well as the global pandemic with its supply chain and trade disruptions added even more urgency to the debate.15 Taking this further in an attempt to combine a political and legal interpretation – building on EU law provisions such as Article 21(2)(a) of the Treaty on the EU – Hoffmeister proposes to define strategic autonomy as the EU’s ‘[s]triving for multilateral solutions, while being able to take lawful action alone to safeguard the Union’s values, fundamental interests, security, independence and integrity[.]’16 However, exploring the path towards a sound data infrastructure from the perspective of traditional EU foreign policy and related regulatory frameworks alone is not enough. Secondly, it is necessary to integrate the current debates about cyber policy and Internet Governance when approaching it. In this regard, Pohle and Thiel argue that after decades of ‘cyber exceptionalism’ and ‘multi-stakeholder’ approaches to Internet Governance, sovereignty is back as a principle since the 2010s.17 Topics such as state autonomy and the security of national infrastructures, economic autonomy and competition, as well as user autonomy and self-determination are at the centre of political debates. They conclude that ‘[d]igital sovereignty is – especially in Europe – now often used as a shorthand for an ordered, value-driven, regulated and therefore reasonable and secure digital sphere’.18 Correspondingly, the EU raised its profile as a regulator. However, as argued elsewhere this global spread of European data protection norms should not be appreciated uncritically. The GDPR’s rules and procedures regulating international data flows lack clarity on whether the promotion of European values or power is the ultimate objective.21 This article reflects on the discussion about strategic autonomy in the light of digital sovereignty and data protection, arguing for recalibrating the debate. It discusses data autonomy as a framework to approach strategic autonomy in a ‘datafied society’,22 thereby enabling to put European values – and particularly human dignity as enshrined in Article 1 of the Charter of Fundamental Rights (CFEU)23 – at the core. The central research question of this article is: Can data autonomy facilitate strategic autonomy in the EU, while promoting human dignity and circumventing negative consequences of emphasizing digital sovereignty and simplistic approaches to data protection?

The following sections outline and highlight the challenges associated with approaching strategic autonomy through the lenses of digital sovereignty and data protection, before introducing and discussing data autonomy as an alternative concept to ground the discourse. This conceptual introduction is concretized with the example of public universities. Their position in a European context is particularly noteworthy, since they need to guarantee both individual and institutional autonomy through their commitment to academic freedom.24 In conclusion, it is argued that data autonomy allows to pursue a value-based approach to strategic autonomy, which takes both individual and organizational autonomy into account while promoting human dignity.

2 STRATEGIC AUTONOMY AND DIGITAL SOVEREIGNTY In the context of datafication, the notion of digital sovereignty has gained prominence in Europe over the last years.25 Historically, the European single market is very open to business activities of American and international tech giants, which – fuelled by rapid technical advancements (e.g., Moore and Metcalfe laws), as well as a ‘code is law’ attitude26 – became instrumental in shaping datafication as an influential part of European life.27 This became particularly apparent with the ‘techno solutionist’ attitude to mitigating the effects of the COVID-19 pandemic.28 Digital contact tracing apps could only be implemented with the help and standardization efforts of Apple and Google – despite the French outcry due to sovereignty concerns29 – similarly to the EU digital COVID-certificate which was dependent on using the app stores of the Californian tech giants.30 Still, digital sovereignty remains a contested and vague term. The reasons why increasingly connected and diverse countries – which owe a large portion of their wealth to having access to global resources, talent, and expertise – care for being sovereign in the digital age, simply differ. Only focusing on the visions of France and Germany – whose fruitful relationship is crucial for modern European integration after World War II – one can identify stark differences as Pons summarizes: ‘France defines digital sovereignty in terms of infrastructure, while the Germans prefer to talk about data sovereignty (Datenhoheit)’.31 For the export-oriented German economy, sovereignty is something that needs to be overcome to access global markets, whereas France thinks digital sovereignty is a protective barrier to guarantee the integrity of society. Nevertheless, one also sees calls for increased autonomy when it comes to data infrastructure in Germany, especially in connection to the protection of trade secrets and ‘know-how’.32

Looking at digital sovereignty from a scholarly perspective, more complexity emerges. Essentially, digital sovereignty morphs into something like an overarching theme, rather than a concrete concept. As already mentioned, Pohle and Thiel identify typical topics such as state autonomy and the security of national infrastructures, economic autonomy and competition, as well as user autonomy and self-determination.33 In a forthcoming report commissioned by the University of Amsterdam, Meiring et al. argue that digital sovereignty is ‘predominantly used as a discursive tool to support a variety of narratives and objectives’,34 connecting also to topics such as ‘technological sovereignty’ and ‘data sovereignty’.35 Therefore, they propose to rather talk about ‘digital sovereignty claims’.36 Expanding the scope internationally, Lukings and Habibi Lashkari define data sovereignty as the ‘idea that the laws and governance structures of a nation extend in application to the data collected within that nation’.37 In the Canadian context they focus on, data sovereignty covers the nation’s right to control the access to and disclosure of digital information as regulated through Canadian law. In the US, issues around digital sovereignty frequently appear in connection with the People’s Republic of China (PRC), such as the attempt to ban the popular social media application TikTok.38 Kokas argues in her 2023 book ‘Trafficking Data’ that exploitative data governance practices of Silicon Valley companies ultimately facilitate the efforts of the PRC in building global infrastructures of control.39 In other words and put to the extreme, the global interconnectedness of the American tech sector threatens the sovereignty of the US and the American way of life. However, purely labelling the PRC as expanding and offensive actor seems over-simplified. 3 STRATEGIC AUTONOMY AND DATA PROTECTION The difficulties around the establishment of an autonomous cloud infrastructure through Gaia-X might not be surprising. As argued, when it comes to datafication the EU adopted a stance that leaves its single market open to international companies to offer hardware and services, while adopting an increasing stringent set of regulatory frameworks. The cliché goes that the US develops cutting-edge digital services and software, while the PRC manufactures the hardware and works on the ‘knock-offs’, and the EU regulates both. 4 DISCUSSION: USING DATA AUTONOMY TO RECALIBRATE THE STRATEGIC AUTONOMY DEBATE?

In summary, it seems that digital sovereignty only offers a vague framing to facilitate European strategic autonomy in the datafied society. As outlined in

section 2, when referring to digital sovereignty there seems to be a tendency towards (direct or indirect) patterns of discrimination and economic protectionism, while emphasizing nationalism and data (infrastructure) localization. The concept as such remains incredibly vague. In section 3, it appeared that considering strategic autonomy through data protection raises doubts as to whether this approach is ultimately driven by a desire for European values or power. At the same time, adequacy assessments as a tool to enhance European strategic autonomy have a mixed track-record and some problematic features emerged in their current substantive and procedural form. This questions their usefulness and desirability as such.

Certainly, data protection law has had a profound impact on shaping datafication and increases the responsible use of personal data, especially with the emergence of GDPR as a gold standard. While much of this had a positive effect for the promotion of human dignity, data protection as a concept also comes with its challenges. First, Purtova questions whether data protection law is bound to become the ‘law of everything’,74 as more and more elements of our lives have some, or even an inextricable connection to datafication. Secondly, Sharon highlighted that in cases such as the standardization of digital contact tracing apps by Apple and Google to mitigate the impact of COVID-19 through Bluetooth-based contact tracing, it might have been relatively easy to formally address data protection concerns without addressing the underlying aspects of individual and institutional autonomy, as well as sovereignty.75 Thirdly, the requirement to label data from the outset (e.g., distinction between personal/non-personal data,76 identifying

4.1 WHAT IS DATA AUTONOMY?

In response to the challenges outlined, data autonomy might serve as a novel and alternative concept to recalibrate the debate around datafication and strategic autonomy in the EU. In contrast to digital sovereignty claims focusing on the analysis and interpretation of power/international relations, it is a value-based concept which can be derived from the existing European legal framework. Essentially, data autonomy is based on informational self-determination, which is a constitutional right that has its origins in 1983 in West Germany.82 The essence of this right is that citizens in a democratic society, in which authorities are bound

4.3 DATA AUTONOMY AND PUBLIC UNIVERSITIES – A CONCRETE EXAMPLE To provide a concrete example of how data autonomy could look like in a specific setting, this section briefly outlines strategic autonomy from the perspective of public universities in the EU. As demonstrated throughout the process of setting up a coordinated enforcement network of the European Data Protection Board (EDPB) that started in October 2020, European public institutions at the level of the EU and its Member States are increasingly dependent on international data flows, cloud-based commercial applications, and remote data storage.90 While these infrastructures promise efficiency, the power of public institutions to make decisions about ‘their data’ seemingly disappears. The position of public universities in addressing this tension is particularly interesting.

On the one hand, public universities are based on the promotion of public values. Universities require ‘academic freedom’ and independence to flourish. In the CFEU, academic freedom is enshrined in Article 13 CFEU, together with the Freedom of the Arts. The explanatory note to Article 13 CFEU states that this right is primarily deduced from the right to freedom of thought and expression, as enshrined in Article 11 CFEU. According to the AQ5 UNESCO’s Recommendation concerning the Status of Higher Education Teaching Personnel, academic freedom entails (1) institutional autonomy, (2) individual rights and freedoms to expression,

(3) protection provided by the state. A 2020 report by the UN Special Rapporteur on the promotion and protection of the right to freedom of opinion and expression, cited the UNESCO Recommendation and stated further that academic freedom involves institutional protections, including autonomy and selfgovernance.91 On the other hand, like any other public institution universities are required to use funds efficiently, and provide state-of-the-art working and research environments for students, researchers, and staff. 5 CONCLUSION This article discusses EU strategic autonomy in the light of digital sovereignty and data protection, arguing for a recalibration of the debate. The central research question is how data autonomy as an alternative and novel concept could facilitate strategic autonomy in the EU against the background of datafication, while promoting human dignity and circumventing challenges emerging when emphasizing more traditional approaches such as digital sovereignty and data protection law. Section 2 on digital sovereignty revealed that it remains a vague term and can hardly be considered as a single/concrete concept. It is mostly used in the context of broader (political) narratives, such as ‘technological sovereignty’. Furthermore, the associated capacity building initiatives relating to manufacturing in Europe (e.g., EU Chips Act) or service development (e.g., establishment of Gaia-X as a European cloud platform) prove challenging, and their added value might be questioned. Additionally, debates around digital sovereignty tend to emphasize nationalism, economic protectionism, and patterns of (in-)direct discrimination.
