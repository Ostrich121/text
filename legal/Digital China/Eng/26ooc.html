Keywords: EU cybersecurity strategy Open-source software Digital sovereignty Cyber Resilience Act Technology governance

‘Digital sovereignty’ is the geopolitical mantra of the moment. A key agent of that policy shift, the European Union (‘EU’) has increasingly embraced ‘digital sovereignty’ as both the ideological foundation and impetus for building its digital future in accordance with ‘European values and principles,’ often driven by and intersecting with cybersecurity concerns as articulated in its 2020 Cybersecurity Strategy for the Digital Decade (‘Strategy’). Yet it is impossible to consider cybersecurity without open-source software (‘OSS’). Increasingly, the EU, USA and other Governments have recognised that fact in the wake of HeartBleed and Log4j incidents. OSS’ decentralised governance and ubiquity, underpinning most software worldwide, may amplify vulnerabilities and adverse effects of cyberattacks, whilst its typically collaborative model of development and innovation often fosters valuable, open cybersecurity solutions.

In navigating that policy tightrope of OSS as a double-edged sword for cybersecurity, the EU has adopted ‘closed’ language of ‘digital sovereignty’ that is ostensibly contrary to the ‘open’ nature of OSS. That rhetorical duality is particularly pronounced since the EU described OSS as a tool for realising its ‘digital sovereignty,’ in addition to policy support for ‘a global, open, interoperable cyberspace’ alongside the pursuit of ‘digital sovereignty.’ While there is a epistemic gap in understanding the relationship between the EU’s rhetoric of ‘digital sovereignty’ and reality, nascent studies indicate that it has a tangible effect on policy change in multiple digital spheres, generally furthering a degree of ‘control.’ However, that relationship within the OSS cybersecurity context has underexplored and poorly understood, although that policy is a priority for the EU and may bear significant implications for OSS globally.

Particularly analyzing the Cyber Resilience Act (‘CRA’) as key means for implementing the EU’s Strategy and its first cybersecurity legislation that would comprehensively engage OSS if adopted by the Council, this article argues that the EU’s desire to strengthening cybersecurity in OSS is generally welcome. Yet there is an ostensibly a disjunct between ‘digital sovereignty’ that underpins that legislation and OSS cybersecurity, with too much control of OSS potentially proving counterproductive for EU cybersecurity. This paper illustrates that (i) it is imperative for the EU to address OSS cybersecurity; (ii) yet the lens of digital sovereignty is ostensibly a rough fit for that approach, considering OSS’ philosophy and practice; and (iii) based on the CRA, EU’s practice of translating ‘digital sovereignty’ into policy change is mixed, leaving uncertain ramifications for OSS cybersecurity in the EU and beyond. On the one hand, it moves towards more ‘control’ at least in determining definitional parameters and power dynamics with novel ‘stewardship’ positions for certain OSS entities. That said, the EU generally seeks to leverage OSS to further their regional embrace of OSS rather than to exclude others. Ultimately, the EU has a valuable leadership opportunity to drive forward solutions to OSS cybersecurity in collaboration with others whilst avoiding fragmentation, keeping doors open in recognising that global challenges demand global solutions. That is in its enlightened self-interest.

1. Introduction ‘Digital sovereignty’ is the geopolitical mantra of the moment. In a world grappling with political uncertainties, shifting alliances and emerging technologies, several States have been seemingly abandoning the post-World War II order’s promise of multilateralism for the pursuit of ‘digital sovereignty’ in their socio-economic, political and national security strategies, even as digital integration and interdependence grows.1 Concerned over losing ‘control’ while wanting to act independently in a digital world, especially in the wake of the COVID-19 pandemic, the European Union (‘EU’) is no exception of this trend and has arguably driven it forward.2 In 2024, ‘digital sovereignty’ has remained central to its high-level policy discourse, often intersecting with security considerations. For instance, on 17 September 2024, the President of the European Commission, Ursula von der Leyen – under whose leadership, such rhetoric became ‘more robust’3 – created its first Executive Vice-President position for ‘Tech-Sovereignty, Security and Democracy.’4 Earlier that month, the Draghi report similarly iterated ‘that EU companies [must] maintain a foothold in areas where technological sovereignty is required, such as security and encryption.’5 It is impossible to consider the EU’s digital future – of cybersecurity or otherwise – without OSS. Depending on how its rhetoric of ‘digital sovereignty’ translates into reality, the EU may have intentional and unintentional consequences, build coalitions or divide them with real-world ramifications. They may mitigate, remain vulnerable to or even exacerbate cybersecurity concerns stemming from OSS vulnerabilities, even while OSS is critical for many cybersecurity tools. Importantly, as a world leader in digital technology governance, how is the EU positioning itself through its 2020 Cybersecurity Strategy for the Digital Decade (‘Strategy’) to walk the regulatory tightrope between strengthening OSS cybersecurity whilst inter alia safeguarding OSS developers’ ability to contribute to this open collective endeavour – which often fosters cybersecurity solutions? The article does not purport to present comprehensive solutions for this conundrum. However, in critically analyzing whether and how the Strategy’s rhetoric translates into reality – notably, through the recently finalised Cyber Resilience Act (‘CRA’) as a central plank that unifies disparate cybersecurity policies and regulation15 and would be the EU’s first cybersecurity legislation that comprehensively engages OSS when formally adopted by the Council – to better understand whether and how it has implications for OSS cybersecurity, whether positive or negative, deliberate or not. Thus, it aims to facilitate conversations on sustainably and effectively improving OSS cybersecurity for the EU and world broadly. Given the EU’s significant influence over digital technology governance by influencing standards and practices abroad – often described as the ‘Brussels effect’ – many stakeholders including the UN, States, international and regional organisations and corporations amongst others may draw inspiration from or emulate its approach to OSS cybersecurity, making this particular research endeavour all the more pertinent and practical.

It is acknowledged that there may be multiple reasons why the EU would adopt a new or revitalised approach to OSS cybersecurity, encompassing yet potentially extending beyond ‘digital sovereignty.’ Yet since ‘digital sovereignty’ is a key foundation of the EU’s high level policy, the Strategy and approach to OSS, it warrants particular consideration.

Essentially, this article asks ‘within the timely context of the EU’s rhetorical embrace of "digital sovereignty," what are practical implications of that approach in the EU’s Strategy, especially as implemented in the CRA, for OSS cybersecurity?’ Firstly, it establishes why it is imperative that the EU considers OSS as a vital double-edged sword for cybersecurity within Europe and beyond, necessitating outward consideration of how OSS operates beyond its borders. Secondly, having critically reflected on the driving factors behind the EU’s embrace of ‘digital sovereignty’ in the Strategy, it argues that this ‘closed’ mantra is

ostensibly incompatible with the philosophy and practice of OSS cybersecurity.

Finally, having analyzed the CRA in particular, the article observes that the EU’s embrace ’digital sovereignty’ have translated into legislative provisions that may affect OSS cybersecurity in largely unknown ways in the EU and beyond. Adopted as an analytical lens, it considers how the EU’s relationship to ‘control’ over OSS cybersecurity as a metric that previous scholars have deployed in mapping how the rhetoric of ‘digital’ sovereignty translates into reality, with potential global implications for OSS cybersecurity and geopolitics.16 This is appropriate given that the EU’s status quo for digital policy was relatively hands-off – largely relying on market forces, multistakeholder engagement and expertise.17 That trend was especially evident for OSS cybersecurity given that EU legislation has never comprehensively covered it – which the CRA would change. In Section IV, that lens of ‘control’18 is applicable to four key aspects of the EU’s relationship with OSS cybersecurity, namely (i) the status and participation of the OSS community; (ii) regulatory carve-outs for OSS; (iii) mainstreaming of OSS within cybersecurity policy; and (iv) international coordination and cooperation on OSS cybersecurity (including as a potential way to moderate control). The focus on the CRA is deliberate, allowing detailed analysis of a major plank of the Strategy that purportedly stitches together a veritable patchwork of European cybersecurity policies and legislation, but that also would enshrine OSS in EU cybersecurity legislation for the first time should the Council formally adopt it. Meanwhile, the article’s core findings are based on comprehensive desk-based research of relevant studies, statements and other responses from OSS contributors and representatives and EU and European Commission’s official public documentation, primarily the Strategy and subsequent (i.e. post 16 December 2020) related output19 that illustrate its strategic policy approach to ‘digital sovereignty,’ ‘open source software’ and ‘cybersecurity,’ using those key terms to identify any relevant materials. For the purposes of this paper, ’cybersecurity’ is defined using EU law as the apt starting point. Article 2(1) of the EU Cybersecurity Act states that ‘cybersecurity means the activities necessary to protect network and information systems, the users of such systems, and other persons affected by cyber threats.’20 Whilst there shall is, adopting Vagelis Papakonstantinou’s phrase, a ‘definitional impasse’ for ‘cybersecurity’ because of the diverse definitions offered across other instruments and scholarship, the aforementioned definition has notably considered its subject-matter and scope, protecting people and systems alike.21 Ultimately, (re)defining ‘cybersecurity’ is beyond this article’s scope.

2. Open-source software: opportunity and obstacle for cybersecurity The EU must engage OSS cybersecurity in a manner befitting the nature of OSS as a ‘double-edged sword’ for cybersecurity, acknowledging that it simultaneously presents a major opportunity and obstacle to a secure digital future in EU borders and beyond. This is especially timely as recent European Commission studies on OSS (prior to the CRA) have typically focussed on reaping its economic benefits, rather than tackling vulnerabilities.22 To respond to a phenomenon, one must understand it. Therefore, this section illuminates key structural features and drivers behind OSS cybersecurity, in considering how the EU and others may develop sustainable, forward-looking solutions.

Firstly, vulnerabilities within OSS pose a serious (and growing) threat to the EU and other Government’s cybersecurity. Indeed, the Log4j incident provides an important example. On 9 December 2021, a newly discovered bug in obscure yet ubiquitous OSS source code called ‘Log4j’ began to spread like wildfire across the world’s digital ecosystem, plunging Governments and major software companies into crisis management mode to investigate whether – and how badly – their systems were compromised and how to extinguish the flames. Secondly, as a double-edged sword for cybersecurity, OSS may partly prove the salvation for Governments worldwide even as it strikes fear into their hearts. It is widely regarded as a critical mechanism for enabling human society to transition towards more secure, safe and resilient software, largely thanks to the ‘global village’ of individuals, groups, States, organisations and (increasingly) companies who develop and support encryption and other cybersecurity products and processes grounded in open source code. These valuable tools include password managers (e.g. LastPass, KeePass), penetration testing tools (e.g. Wireshark, Nmap) and Host-Based Intrusion Detection System tools (e.g. OSSEC, Tripwire), whose open nature increases equal accessibility of cybersecurity solutions for all, including EU Member States. Open standards facilitate interoperability between security tools, benefitting the security of industrial stakeholders.’33 Yet this potential of OSS to improve the EU’s cybersecurity simultaneously facilitates and depends upon a global, free and open cyberspace to collaborate in seamlessly sharing technical knowledge across borders and between communities.

Thirdly, the EU should consider three important structural drivers and factors behind OSS vulnerabilities in developing sustainable solutions for a more secure digital future. To begin, it is important to note that cybersecurity vulnerabilities are not unique to OSS, nor is it intrinsically less secure than proprietary code.34 A 2022 Red Hat report has found that 89 % of the nearly 1300 IT decision-makers surveyed agreed that ‘open source is at least as secure as proprietary software’ – reflecting a growth in industry trust and confidence.35 Proprietary software is ‘closed source’ software over which its owners enjoy an exclusive legal right to its access and licensing to users,36 while also exercising discretion over whether and how they maintain it. However, when OSS is published on online repositories and is subsequently used or modified in derivative works, there is not necessarily an entity with centralised oversight (and responsibility) to survey potential cybersecurity weaknesses.

Secondly, what makes cybersecurity vulnerabilities in OSS distinctive is broadly twofold: (i) its sheer ubiquity; and (ii) that decentralised governance model without dedicated oversight and/or funding including for maintenance efforts, meaning that many vulnerabilities may languish unresolved. To begin, the ubiquitous nature of OSS makes it more valued and valuable, yet amplifies effects of a cybersecurity incident, affecting users without discrimination, whether they are States, corporations, NGOs or individuals. However, cybersecurity incidents arising from OSS are mostly borne from ‘zero-sum’ vulnerabilities, stemming from human error or oversight when a (typically volunteer) programmer has scarce time, resources, inclination and/or technical capacity for maintenance work.42 Legal scholar Chinmayi Sharma has aptly described this systemic institutional challenge of maintenance of OSS as a ‘tragedy of the digital commons.’43 She analogises from Gareth Hardin’s concept of ‘tragedy of the commons’,44 since individuals who access a shared resource (namely, OSS) often act in their own interest to overexploit it, thereby resulting in negative externalities to third parties and harming the collective good (i.e. the decline of the digital commons, including through a failure to support OSS upkeep and maintenance). In the collectivist world of OSS, many actors – including Governments – are free-riders, benefiting from without contributing to OSS. However, Governments hold a pivotal role in the ‘coordinated, comprehensive response [needed] to address the threat of open-source security,’45 and would greatly benefit from such endeavours.

3.3. Does the EU’s ‘digital sovereignty’ approach make sense for OSS and its cybersecurity?

In parallel to the Strategy’s objective of deploying the three primary instruments of policy, regulation and investment to further its key action area of ‘resilience, technological sovereignty and leadership,’ the EU commits to ‘advancing a global and open cyberspace.’96 The philosophy and practice of OSS ostensibly better complements this latter objective, as a beneficiary as well as an enabler of the very existence of a global, open cyberspace. 4.3. Re-examining regulatory carve outs for OSS The CRA provides a general carve out for OSS, perpetuating what is a common practice for Governments seeking to leverage the benefits of the open model of collaboration, innovation and development. However, its exception for OSS is contingent on it satisfying certain definitional parameters including that it is ‘openly shared’ software source code and not ‘made available on the market’ (i.e. not ‘supplied for distribution or use in the course of a commercial activity’) (Recital 18), with a separate ‘light touch’ regulatory category for OSS stewards (Recital 19). These two key developments – namely, the stipulation that it is ‘openly shared’ and creating a separate novel regime for ‘stewards’ – has evoked an important debate within the OSS community and beyond over what best serves their collective interests and, by extension, OSS itself: Governments carving out regulatory exceptions for OSS or not, and how?

Often explored in international environmental law, it was considered as a normative basis for transboundary governance of oceans, biological diversity and climate change.201 For Franz Xaver Perrez, sovereignty ‘means a responsibility to cooperate’ as a member of the international community, meaning that ‘cooperation should be conceived an element of the very notion of sovereignty itself.’202 Similarly, Eyal Benvenisti has described sovereign States as bearing external obligations beyond their territorial borders, ‘as trustees of all of humanity,’ akin to neighbors in a densely packed apartment rather than individual estates.203 That analogy is apt, considering the transboundary nature of OSS, simultaneously dependent upon and facilitating an open, global cyberspace. Therefore, EU should consider seriously how its practices and standards towards OSS cybersecurity may have global effects, for better or worse. Thinking critically about ‘sovereignty’ as both a source of power but also responsibility is therefore helpful for forging multilateral cooperation in protecting an open, global, and interoperable cyberspace and promoting OSS cybersecurity.

Therefore, although it is welcome news that the US and EU are not working entirely in silos, OSS cybersecurity is a global challenge requiring global solutions beyond those global powers. Speaking of the need for collective action on cybersecurity within the EU, President Ursula von der Leyen has emphasised that ‘[since] resources are scarce, we have to bundle our forces.’204 Relatedly, a 2020 EU briefing paper on digital sovereignty has identified ‘insufficient coordination in matters of cybersecurity’ as ‘one of the main issues EU policy-makers must tackle,’ albeit looking towards internally rather than externally.205 The UN, having recently taken a more proactive stance on supporting the global governance of OSS,206 albeit within the context of the Sustainable Development Goals, may serve an apex coordinating role. Drawing on that same collectivist, resource-pooling mentality, the EU has a valuable opportunity as a leader in digital technology governance to encourage multilateral and multistakeholder avenues for strengthening OSS cybersecurity, thereby advancing a truly (more secure) global, open and interoperable cyberspace for the collective benefit of humanity. Governments racing to the bottom in the pursuit of stringent, solipsistic visions of digital sovereignty may risk fragmenting not only OSS as a digital public good, but also the very foundations of our digitally interconnected and interdependent world. As Hila Lifshitz-Assaf and Frank Nagle astutely observe, hitzith the number of stakeholders involved in the []OSS ecosystem, it is difficult for any single actor to solve all of the issues.’207 Thus, coordinating and collaborating with diverse stakeholders beyond their borders in a ‘multiparty effort including companies, governmental organisations, and individual contributors’ would be ‘necessary to ensure the security and vitality of the [] OSS ecosystem.’208 Otherwise, this collective action problem of OSS governance shall persist, weakening OSS for everyone and leaving its cybersecurity vulnerabilities to languish unresolved. Finally, the EU should also consider whether its objectives align with a broader, more holistic conception of digital sovereignty in the OSS context, recognising that fully benefitting from the ‘international community’ of programmers requires reaching beyond borders and drawing on the contributions across the so-called ‘Global North and South’ divide. Otherwise, the EU may inadvertently create friction in the ease of information sharing and cooperation across borders to resolve vulnerabilities in OSS. As Trey Herr has noted, many ongoing efforts to discover vulnerabilities in OSS – whether by volunteers supporting OSS projects, small security firms, independent researchers or software companies – involve transboundary collaboration. Yet ‘[d]efenders and researchers often take for granted the free flow of information across political boundaries,’ which may be stymied by restrictive regulations.209 Therefore, the so-called ‘Cybered Westphalian Age,’ coined by Chris Demchak and Peter Dombrowski, may exacerbate cybersecurity risks and should not be taken as a foregone conclusion.210 Aaron Brantly has offered an alternative perspective. Specifically, that the EU could explore the ‘entanglement’ model adopted by Joseph Nye, seeing that ‘entanglement with traditional or potential adversaries can serve as a self-deterrent mechanism that promotes risk aversion.’211 Since everyone is dependent upon OSS as the foundation of their digital ecosystem, they share the risks and benefits alike. If Governments engage in ‘Balkanisation’ of OSS, erecting geographical barriers for OSS access and standards, they may expose themselves to greater risk. To conclude, the EU is ostensibly adopting a ‘moderate’ digital sovereignty approach vis-a-vis OSS cybersecurity, remaining relatively hands-off in retaining carve-outs for OSS in the CRA while its Strategy and other high-level documents actively look towards building a culture of OSS in the EU (without necessarily excluding others) and developing avenues of global (or at least, bilateral) collaboration. Others may draw a comparison to the EU’s drafting process for ENISA’s European Cybersecurity Certification Scheme for Cloud Services, with the November 2023 amendments reportedly indicating a ‘more flexible, tiered approach to sovereignty, considering [and finding a balance between] EU states’ and non-EU providers’ concerns.’212 However, the CRA’s novel designation of OSS stewards in particular has shifted key power dynamics by elevating certain OSS entities, even if they are subject to ‘light touch’ regulation. The effects, good or bad, remain to be seen – but are likely to cause global ripples given the global nature of OSS. 5. Conclusion A US Deputy Secretary of Defence once observed aptly that ‘[i]n the 21st Century, bits and bytes can be as threatening as bullets and bombs.’214 Amidst rising geopolitical polarisation and fears of a technology trade war, the EU has lofty ambitions for securing a digital future that accords with its European values and vision – including ‘digital sovereignty’ – through its Strategy and, in the process, shaping OSS cybersecurity. OSS, which the EU has described as both a ‘digital public good’ and as an ‘enabler’ of its ‘digital sovereignty,’ is a critical component of that future. Having readily reaped benefits of OSS for decades, the EU is now engaging in nascent efforts to rectify its historically overlooked cybersecurity risks, which have been increasing commensurate with further integration of OSS within all EU systems, industries and public and private sectors.

It is impossible to consider cybersecurity without OSS. Increasingly, the EU, USA and other Governments have recognised that fact in the devastating wake of the HeartBleed and Log4j incidents. OSS’ decentralised governance and ubiquity, underpinning most software worldwide, may amplify vulnerabilities and adverse effects of cyberattacks, whilst its typically collaborative model of development and innovation often fosters valuable, open cybersecurity solutions. Ultimately, the EU has a clear socio-economic and political incentive to tackle cybersecurity vulnerabilities arising from OSS, an increasingly significant challenge, while also providing an environment where OSS may develop and flourish, thereby producing and supporting cybersecurity solutions. Yet the road ahead is bumpy. Worldwide, several Governments are swept up in the geopolitical fervour of digital sovereignty, interpretations of which could sit uneasily with OSS as a digital public good and, by extension, its cybersecurity, and raise the spectre of its fragmentation. In navigating that policy tightrope of OSS as a double-edged sword for cybersecurity, the EU has adopted ‘closed’ language of ‘digital sovereignty’ that is ostensibly contrary to the ‘open’ nature of OSS. That rhetorical duality is particularly pronounced since the EU has described OSS as a tool for realising its ‘digital sovereignty,’ in addition to policy support for ‘a global, open, interoperable cyberspace’ alongside the pursuit of ‘digital sovereignty.’ Although there is an epistemic gap in understanding the relationship between the EU’s rhetoric of ‘digital sovereignty’ and reality, nascent studies indicate that it is not empty rhetoric with tangible effects on policy change in across various digital spheres, generally furthering a degree of ‘control.’215 That relationship in the OSS cybersecurity context is broadly underexplored and poorly understood, although that nascent policy is a priority for the EU and may bear significant implications for OSS globally. Eluding definitional consensus and courting controversy, the EU’s adoption of the often geopolitically fraught concept of ‘digital sovereignty’ as a driving force for and justification of certain cybersecurity regulations towards OSS must nevertheless be treated with caution. This paper has illustrated that (i) it is imperative for the EU to address OSS cybersecurity, as it is a critical double edged sword for cybersecurity; (ii) yet the lens of digital sovereignty is ostensibly a rough fit for that approach, considering OSS’ philosophy and practice; and (iii) based on the CRA, EU’s practice of translating ‘digital sovereignty’ into policy change is mixed, leaving uncertain ramifications for the future of OSS cybersecurity in the EU and beyond.
