‘Software as Medical Device’ Regulation: What the US Can Teach to the EU for a Fairer Balance Towards Its Digital Sovereignty?

Anna Fiorentini | ORCID: 0009-0003-2164-6169 Department of Legal Studies, University of Bologna, Via Zamboni 33, 40126 Bologna, Italy anna.fiorentini3@unibo.it

Abstract

The AI presence in healthcare (e.g., telemedicine platforms and Software as Medical Devices) is uncontroversial by now. Beyond the Big Tech already for some time large investors in this field, the States, repeatedly accused to be unable keeping pace with the exponential technological development, are growingly called upon to deal with it. Taking the distance from those who perceive the US as a regulatory model to oppose in order to assert the EU digital sovereignty, the present analysis will prove that a glimpse across the Atlantic could only help the EU legislator. With a specific focus on the SaMD regulation, it will be shown how the choices made in the US appear to be grounded on a fair balance between patient and economic operators’ rights. Building on it, a new balancing formula needs to be put forward to guide the EU intensive legislative activity for the digital world.

3 The Key Role of Public Regulation Having addressed the self-regulative attempts made by private actors to deal with the concerns raised by the deployment of AI/ML, it was not necessary to offer a precise definition of Clinical Decision Support System (CDSS). However, turning to public regulation, a few words are needed on CDSS so as to correctly identify the applicable legal framework. In particular, it should be preliminarily specified that the CDSS can be included in the wide category of SaMD which is defined by the International Medical Device Regulators Forum (IMDRF) as “software intended to be used for one or more medical purposes that perform these purposes without being part of a hardware medical device”.45 More precisely, a CDSS is qualified as a medical device when it is able to carry out an automated reasoning with regard, for example, to the monitoring of symptoms, playing a predominant role in guiding the professional’s decision-making process. 3.1.3.

Post-market Surveillance After the approval and subsequent placing on the market, the US legislator laid down surveillance regimes that include the Quality System Regulation and the Medical Device Reporting.81 As for the former, the FDA provides a set of requirements on the methods and structures to be used and the checks to be carried out at the different stages of design, production and maintenance of the devices. Among the essential elements of the quality system outlined by the FDA, the manufacturer is left with a certain margin of discretion in the elaboration of the good manufacturing practices since the main aspect concerns the safeguard of device safety and effectiveness. In relation to the Medical Device Reporting, this mechanism allows the manufacturer to readily report all suspected deaths, serious injuries as well as some specific types of malfunctioning to the FDA in order to identify and promptly solve any safety problems that may arise in the use of a device.

Against this background, further analyses should be put forward to delve into what are the rights that the EU digital sovereignty aims to safeguard. On this point, the recently adopted EU Declaration on Digital Rights and Principles for the Digital Decade108 is worth mentioning. The relevance of this Declaration is not so much due to the reaffirmation of the “human-centric approach” and the citizens’ empowerment in the digital world, but rather to an obiter dictum situated in its Preamble. At the sixth paragraph, the Commission states, out of the blue, that the Declaration “does not affect lawful limits on the exercise of legal rights, in order to reconcile them with the exercise of other rights, or necessary and proportionate restrictions in the public interest”. Although apparently irrelevant, this clarification seems crucial as it emphasizes the need to strike a (maybe new) balance between the fundamental rights at stake.109 This is not self-evident given the propensity of the legislator, or rather of the interpreters at supranational and national level, to make certain rights constantly prevail over others. For instance, also for reasons related to its legal basis (Article 16 of the TFEU), GDPR, and thus the right to data protection, is gaining a key role in the achievement of digital sovereignty110 and we are witnessing the creation of a “European Personal Data Fortress”111 where the protection of data, and especially of personal data, overwhelms that of other fundamental rights, such as the right to health and economic initiative.
