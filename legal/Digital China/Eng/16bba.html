EDITORIAL

Borders, bordering and sovereignty in digital space Chenchen Zhang a and Carwyn Morris b

ABSTRACT The production of digital borders and the development of digital sovereignty have effects that are felt within the bordered digital spaces and outside of them. Digital borders are not just national borders, and the effects of digital borders can be analysed at a multitude of geographical scales, from the regional to the neighbourhood, and from the national territory to the bordered and bounded instant messaging group. The contributions to this special issue reveal the diverse ways in which borders and sovereignty are developed as well as how these processes are contested in ways that call into question pre-existing conceptualisations of digital relations, particularly the understanding of the internet as borderless.

KEYWORDS digital borders; digital sovereignty; sovereignty; bordering; digital space; digital territory

HISTORY Received 1 May 2023

1. INTRODUCTION At the recent US House Energy Committee’s hearing with the TikTok Chief Executive Ofﬁcer, Shou Zi Chew, Chew repeatedly presented Project Texas as a means to get ‘American data on American soil by an American company looked after by American personnel’ (Tech Policy Press, 2023). The security anxieties around TikTok and the company’s promise to ‘keep American data on American soil’ epitomise the resurgence of borders and territory in the infrastructures and imaginaries of information technology. Despite earlier predictions about the internet’s capacity to create a borderless world, digitality has been shaped by and embedded in bordering processes within and beyond the nation-state (Chouliaraki & Georgiou, 2019; Vukov & Sheller, 2013). From regulatory regimes governing cross-border data ﬂows to the discourse of digital sovereignty, from the geopolitics of cybersecurity to the reassertion of the importance of national information infrastructures (Farrand & Carrapico, 2022; Glasze et al., 2022; Kohl, 2017; Kokas, 2022; Schou & Hjelholt, 2019), scholars have called attention to how socio-technical practices regarding and in digital spaces not only reproduce existing power structures, but also give rise to new forms of bordering, securitisation and territorialisation. The securitisation of TikTok in the United States and Europe is but one of the most recent examples of these trends, and digital geographies are increasingly sites for and instruments of geopolitical rivalries and geoeconomic competitions (Ash et al., 2018). Under the Donald Trump administration, for instance, the United States announced the ‘Clean Network’ initiative that revived the Cold War narrative about the freedom and security of the ‘free world’ being

The Joe Biden administration has not abandoned the Trump-era digital strategy, choosing to build on rather than revoke the ‘Securing the Information and Communications Technology and Services Supply Chain’ Executive Order, a departure from earlier US digital strategy (Couture & Toupin, 2019). Meanwhile, the development of China’s national ‘internet infrastructures’ – an ecosystem built on ‘domestically developed and owned digital platforms’ (Mihelj, 2023, p. 14) – has been shaped by national socio-technical imaginaries and policies in which ideas of ‘cyberspace sovereignty’ are implicated (Morris, 2022). Against this background, this special issue sets out to explore processes of bordering and territorialisation and the rearticulation of sovereignty in digital space.

2. TERRITORIALIZING THE INTERNET

Although the methodological approaches and geographical foci vary across contributions, a common starting point of this collection is an emphasis on the socially and politically constructed nature of borders and territory (Brambilla, 2015; Elden, 2013, 2017; Newman, 2006), a theoretical orientation familiar to the readers of Territory, Politics, Governance. If modern statehood merges together different and interrelated dimensions of territory – a political–legal concept of territory as bounded jurisdiction, a geophysical concept of territory as ‘brute, physical terrain’, as well as a normative one taking the territorial nation-state as the primary framework of identity and belonging (Shah, 2012, p. 58) – then the state’s ongoing efforts to territorialise the digital seeks to produce digital territories on all these fronts: with regard to the jurisdiction of data, the securitisation of information infrastructures, as well as normative imaginaries about the nation and citizenship (Möllers, 2021).

Furthermore, critical geographical scholarship has elucidated the double logic of circulation and closure intrinsic to the technologies of borders and territory, calling attention to how walls and barriers do not just blockade or demarcate, but also produce governable populations through management and interruptions of mobility (Aradau & Blanke, 2010; Pallister-Wilkins, 2016; Weizman, 2012). In his famous account of the historical transformation of governmentality in Europe, Michel Foucault observes the emergence of a:

completely different problem that is no longer that of ﬁxing and demarcating the territory, but of allowing circulation to take place, of controlling them, sifting the good and bad … but in such a way that the inherent dangers of this circulation are cancelled out. (Foucault, 2003, p. 65) Recognising that concerns with circulation have always been central to border regimes in ‘analogue’ settings helps us historicise and contextualise the ways in which the rationality of circulation or connectivity and the rationality of security or closure are intertwined in various projects of territorialising the digital across national contexts. While legal frameworks of data governance vary in their prioritisation of facilitating international trade, national interests, and the protection of individual rights (Voss, 2020), the discourses and practices of cybersecurity are typically underpinned by the image of territory as ‘physical container that secures and delimits’ (Möllers, 2021,

p. 114). For the French Ministry of the Armed Forces (Ministère des Armées, 2019), for example, any cyberattack against French computer systems or any production of effects on French territory through digital means ‘can constitute, at the very least, a violation of sovereignty’ (Ministère des Armées, 2019, p. 6). Crucially, whether it is legal measures regulating cross-border data ﬂows or the emerging geopolitics of cybersecurity (Kokas, 2018), it is through

Strategies of territorial closure and transnational interconnectedness (Glasze et al., 2022) are conﬁgured differently across different national contexts. As the wealth of literature on the discourses and practices of digital sovereignty has shown, positive invocations of the idea of sovereignty typically come from places where it is most lacking – outside of the United States which enjoys global hegemony in technological innovation and standard setting (Couture & Toupin, 2019). While China and Russia were among the ﬁrst to advance the narrative of internet sovereignty (Budnitsky & Jia, 2018), calls for stronger autonomy amidst concerns with foreign inﬂuence have emerged in other countries and regions such as India, Brazil and the EU (Farrand & Carrapico, 2022; Vila Seoane, 2021). By contrast, the United States has been known for being critical of the notion of digital sovereignty, advocating for free ﬂows of data, and opposing data localisation laws. In this context, TikTok’s promise to ‘store American data on American soil’ is suggestive of the changeable and context-dependent ways in which representations of digital territoriality, which may or may not explicitly employ the notion of digital sovereignty, could ‘problematize speciﬁc threats’ and ‘articulate speciﬁc images of an enemy’ (Glasze et al., 2022, p. 921). The norm of free ﬂows of data meets its limits in the global competition between Chinese and American digital capitalisms entangled with geopolitical tensions.

3. BORDERING BEYOND THE STATE

While a growing body of literature has explored problems of digital sovereignty and bordering in cyberspace from the perspective of state actors, regulatory initiatives and global governance, this special issue extends the analysis of digital bordering and contestations to grassroots and everyday practices. This approach thus both challenges assumptions about the obsolescence of the state and the methodological state-centrism that hides ‘the human agency and everydayness of sovereignty’ (Visoka, 2019, p. 169) and lived experiences of (digital) borders. The contributions in this special issue on grassroots authoritarianism in India and digital activism in China show that states and corporations are not the only actors engaged in bordering practices and projects of territorialisation are contested by individual citizens in their everyday use of digital technologies.

Cognitive or symbolic borders could be another way to think of the everyday effects of digital borders. Despite the fact that most prominent social media platforms are international and ﬂows of (mis)information travel across national borders, internet and media scholars are still able to represent (and construct) online interactions and discourses as national by using descriptors such as Indian or Turkish social media and ‘Runet’ (Asmolov & Kolozaridi, 2017). Amongst Chinese internet users, there is an acute awareness of a distinctly Chinese internet or technosphere because of the domestically developed ecosystem as well as the stringent censorship regime. The Chinese technosphere is routinely referred to as neiwang (internal net) or qiangnei (inside the wall) by users in everyday interactions, whereas the ‘international’ technosphere is referred to as waiwang (external net) or qiangwai (outside the wall). Social media users of the neiwang might emphasise that their contents should not be shared to the waiwang, especially when they feel that the posts in question might promote biases against China among international audiences. Similarly, Chinese users of the waiwang may warn that their posts should not be shared to the neiwang for concerns with censorship and personal safety. While these internalised perceptions of digital territoriality are the effects of the state’s territorialisation project that delimits the Chinese internet through legislative and technological means, they should also be considered as processes of symbolic bordering in their own right, and the politics of these mundane representations of digital territory

4. THE TECHNOLOGICAL, THE DISCURSIVE AND THE EVERYDAY

Douzet et al. (2022, in this issue) examine internet data routing in the Middle East to understand how digital borders and the digital geography of the Gulf region evolve in the face of foreign policy decisions. The innovative methodologies employed by Douzet et al. highlight that while there has been a longstanding lack of interconnectivity between countries within the Middle East, geopolitical issues, including the Qatar crisis and the Abraham Accords, can inﬂuence how digital borders are (re)produced. The Qatar diplomatic crisis with the region from 2017 led to a clear reduction in transnational internet connectivity in the region, a hardening of borders. On the other hand, the regional isolation of Israel is evident in their data, and while the Abraham Accords have led to a softening of the border between Israel and Bahrain, the authors show the continued regional isolation of Israel. Through these data we also catch a glimpse of how digital bordering can provide opportunities for new regional actors to emerge, and within their data the rise of Bahrain as a signiﬁcant actor in regional data trafﬁcking is evident, as is the role that non-regional (Western) institutions play. Bahrain’s position here draws parallels with Singapore, a country that has emerged as a regional data trafﬁcking hub in Asia despite its relatively small size. As Douzet et al. note, data routing requires trust, and many of these trust issues are related to broader issues around sovereignty. The paranoia around digital connectivity discussed here is not regionally unique, and the discussion brings to mind recent regulations around data transfers in the EU, such as the General Data Protection Regulation, as well as China’s Data Security Law (Christakis, 2020; Creemers, 2022). The methods employed provide a much clearer picture of transnational digital connections, enabling cross-regional comparisons on digital borders and sovereignty that were previously difﬁcult to make. Morris (2023, in this issue) maps China’s project of digital territorialisation and analyses its effects on contentious politics through a study of #BeijingSurgery#. Looking at a range of institutional, legislative, and epistemological developments revolving around the territorialisation of cyberspace, Morris shows how physical infrastructures and legal frameworks produce a digital territory underpinned by ideas of digital sovereignty in co-constitutive ways. The elaborate censorship regime enabled by both technology and the manpower of censorship workers profoundly shapes the ways in which users of ‘Chinese internet’ experience a bordered digital territory. In this context, Morris looks at how digitally centred activism in China copes with the socio-spatial effects of the territorialisation project by taking data and projects to the international/US ‘digital terrain’ and to physical spaces. The ‘transnational turn’ is also seen in the recent wave of protests against zero-COVID policies in November and December 2022. Protestors relied on platforms such as Telegram, Instagram and Twitter for communications, organisation and building connections (Feng, 2022), when posts and videos about the protests shared within China’s digital territory were quickly censored. Key anonymous accounts have grown popular as information hubs and ‘tree holes’, whereby followers can submit posts anonymously through private messages which are then shared publicly. The potentials and limitations of these new forms of digital activism will be a productive venue for future research.

Dammann and Glasze (2022, in this issue) combine many of the themes present in other contributions through their analysis of discourse around the circulation of digital data in Germany. In doing this, the authors expand scholarly understanding of how (digital) sovereignty is understood and discussed in public and political discourse, and they highlight how the ongoing debate around the necessity for greater sovereignty over digital affairs is often made in opposition to debates around a ‘free’, or more libertarian, internet. This study adds nuance to pre-existing studies of digital sovereignty and the political materialities of digital territorialisation projects,

enabling a conversation between scholarship on the US ‘Barlowian’ internet project (Couture & Toupin, 2019) and scholarship the examines how the development of digital sovereignty and digital borders is already ongoing (Morris, 2022; Möllers, 2021). While the article provides a succinct genealogy of tensions between competing political rationalities in Germany, it also provides a clear window into how imaginaries around digital life and sovereignty are changing. Pre-2010 a binary seemed to exist, with the US controlled ‘Free Internet’ on one side and the bordered, authoritarian Chinese internet on the other. But since 2010, a number of events have confused this binary, and through the German experience it becomes possible to understand how new forms of digital state-hood are emerging, offering a clue not just to Germany’s digital future but providing evidence that alternative digital futures are not just possible, they already exist. In doing this, they provide evidence that a territorial imaginary is central to ongoing German and European digital governance. This is not, as the contributors note, a new hegemonic rationality in the governance of data circulation, but it is evidence of an important discursive evolution that may help scholars understand how the digital geographies of nation-states and international organisations are moving in distinct directions.

Kamra et al. (2023, in this issue) explore how members of resident welfare associations use everyday digital technologies to reconﬁgure socio-spatial boundaries against the backdrop of the COVID-19 pandemic and India’s technocratic solutionism. They show that WhatsApp has become a crucial technological tool for middle-class residents to perform care and surveillance, operationalise cultures and fear, whereby (re)producing geopolitical, racial and class boundaries. While the case study is focused on India, similar micropolitics of care, surveillance, and fear mediated through digital technologies at the grassroots level can be observed elsewhere. In China, where technocratic solutionism has also emerged as a dominant mode of governance (Zhang, 2020) and ‘zero-COVID’ policies were once taken to the extreme, the symbolism of the white protective suits, worn by grassroots pandemic control workers known as dabai, shifted from care and protection to violence and control (L.G., 2022). Here WeChat (Weixin) functions similarly in neighbourhood communities to enable practices of care and surveillance, such as distributing daily essentials, making announcements of quarantine rules, and ensuring resident compliance of the rules. The messaging app also plays a prominent role in the spreading of Islamophobic conspiracy theories and misinformation both within China and among Chinese diaspora communities elsewhere.

5. CONCLUSIONS Reﬂecting on this special issue, the discussions within it are building on geographical scholarship that questions borders and their construction and exploring the multiscalar complexities of digital borders (Schiller, 2015; Xiang, 2013). While previous scholarship on digital borders has tended to prioritise the nation-state as a key way in which digital borders can be understood, scholars of digital borders must be careful not to uncritically reproduce the centrality of the national border in everyday life. Borders cannot always be represented with ‘unambiguous legibility [through] cartographic representations’, through clear topologies, or as linear constructs (Billé, 2018,

p. 63), and this is as true of digital borders as it is for non-digital borders. While the contributions in this special issue have taken important steps in moving beyond a methodologically nationalist reading of digital borders (Wimmer & Schiller, 2002), we would encourage scholars of borders, bordering, and sovereignty in digital space to consider the digital border at multiple scales and to consider how digital borders and sovereignty can be read through transnational and translocal lenses (Brickell & Datta, 2011).

Digital borders are constantly being (re)produced, and, as technology develops, the geographies of digital borders will change and the sociospatial effects of digital borders may be felt wherever one encounters digital technologies. We would encourage scholars of digital borders, bordering, and

We would also encourage exploration of other non-visual dimensions of borders, bordering, and sovereignty, with the symbolic and cognitive border being one aspect of digital borders and bordering that we as editors of this special issue feel warrants further exploration. We hope that this special issue is one starting point for a theoretically, conceptually, and contextually wider examination of digital borders, bordering, and sovereignty in digital space, which appear to be of increasing relevance at the current conjunction.
