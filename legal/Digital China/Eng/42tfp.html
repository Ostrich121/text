K E Y W O R D S artiﬁcial intelligence, China, data governance, digital sovereignty, European Union

With the surge of the digital sovereignty debate, countries and blocks in the world sought to build technological and regulatory capacity to ascertain technological autonomy—deﬁnitions notwithstanding (Pohle & Thiel, 2020). At the same time, these actors sought to position themselves discursively, differentiating their own understanding of digital sovereignty from that of competing powers (Cong & Thumfart, 2022). In this context, the European Union (EU) elaborated the concept of digital sovereignty as something obtainable on the individual level, where regulations are put in place purportedly for the users of digital technologies to be fully informed about an application's technical functioning and be able to choose what personal data (not) to share (Floridi, 2020).

Meanwhile in China the government launched a number of artiﬁcial intelligence (AI) and data protection regulations along with an antitrust crackdown on numerous platform companies. This aimed at bringing technological giants (namely platforms), capable of handling massive amounts of data and inﬂuencing people's everyday lives, under stricter government rule (Creemers, 2022). While the Chinese government has only partially framed these actions within conceptual frameworks akin to ‘digital sovereignty’, the purported aim was accruing individual autonomy vis‐à‐vis big techs (Li, 2023), arguably falling close to the EU's individual‐level ‘digital sovereignty’ framework on a discursive level. Is individual digital sovereignty attainable for the EU? Comparing EU and Chinese AI and data governance regulations, this article unpacks the EU discourse on individual digital sovereignty and ﬁnds the EU regulatory effort insufﬁcient to guarantee its declared objective to uphold individual autonomy. Both China and the EU are adopting these kinds of regulations to increase their own regulatory inﬂuence over global capitals that control a vast amount of data and sensitive communications infrastructure. However, the two have deeply different political systems: the EU is a block of 27 liberal democracies, whereas in China the State and the Communist Party are integrated (despite the formal recognition of a number of smaller parties holding seats in the National People's Congress). While this makes China a more centralised system of authority, it does not mean China can be treated as a monolithic whole. On the contrary, a number of public and private interests interact within China alternating competition and cooperation (Nanni, 2022). By ﬁnding overlaps and similarities across the two actors' respective legislations, the authors ﬁnd ﬂaws in the EU's promise of individual‐level digital sovereignty. To run the comparison, the authors conducted a qualitative thematic analysis on selected pieces of legislation of China and the EU. This article identiﬁes three realms of application of digital regulation within the scope of digital sovereignty: infrastructure, platforms, and data. Of these three, the latter two (platforms and data) concern the individual dimension of digital sovereignty inasmuch as individual rights' protection vis‐à‐vis (AI) platforms is concerned. These will therefore be central in the article's analysis, motivating a focus on AI and data regulations over other aspects. Coherently with the objectives of this special issue on Unthinking Digital Sovereignty, this article deconstructs the EU's false promise of individual digital sovereignty. The next section discusses the existing literature on AI and data governance. The methodological section illustrates and discusses the methods adopted in this research. The following section is an empirical analysis of selected pieces of legislation in the EU and China, then discussed in the discussion section. The ﬁnal section draws conclusions.

The concept of digital sovereignty is not a European uniqueness. On the contrary, China elaborated its concepts of Internet sovereignty (网络主权wangluo zhuquan or 互联网主权 hulianwang zhuquan)1 and information sovereignty (信息主权xinxi zhuquan). The latter dates back to the 1990s and refers to the Chinese government's attempts to control information ﬂows within the country amid the rise of digitalisation and the spreading ubiquity of Internet connectivity, on which China invested actively since the late 1980s to push its own economic development (Cong & Thumfart, 2022; Gong, 2005). The 2010 White Paper on (the Status of) the Internet in China (中国互联网状况, zhongguo hulianwang zhuangkuang) is credited in literature as China's ﬁrst instance of formalisation of the Internet sovereignty concept in a policy paper (Creemers, 2020). Tellingly, the concept is mentioned under

Chapter 5 ‘Protecting Internet Security’ (Information Ofﬁce of the State Council of the People's Republic of China, 2010), thus explicitly making Internet sovereignty a facet of national security.

AI and data ﬁt straightforwardly into the digital sovereignty debate. They are two assets identiﬁed by most states across the world as strategic (Belli & Zingales, 2022; European Commission, 2020). The two are deeply intertwined, as data is raw material for AI development. Generally speaking, AI systems carry out tasks that traditionally require human intelligence by learning through a vast amount of data (Thirunavukarasu, 2023). Here, the authors look at what the EU and China consider of relevance within the ﬁeld of AI to identify key elements of individual‐level digital sovereignty for the EU. To begin with, a number of policy papers have been published within the EU, including ethical guidelines (European Commission, 2019). These culminated in the drafting of the so‐called AI Act, a regulation that completed its legislative process in 2024. The ethical dimension of AI is stressed time and again in policy papers and within the AI Act itself (European Commission, 2019; European Parliament, 2021). It is particularly oriented towards data protection and individuals' possibility to retain control over their data in AI settings, but it is also oriented towards limiting AI misuses by public authorities as well as companies. In particular, the AI Act identiﬁes a risk scale that seeks to classify AI systems based on how acceptable or unacceptable the risk level is for the EU legislator (European Parliament, 2021). The scope of application and effects of the AI Act are yet to be seen at the time of writing as the regulation is not yet in force. Nonetheless, the regulation's ﬁnal text allows scholars to make preliminary analysis. Alongside AI governance, data governance is key to studying the individual dimension of digital sovereignty for the EU. First, data protection is a core policy concern for the EU and its legislation created a global benchmark for extraterritorial data protection. Second, data is the raw material for AI functioning. The EU Ethics Guidelines for Trustworthy AI provide that ‘AI systems must guarantee privacy and data protection throughout a system's entire lifecycle’ (European Commission, 2019). Furthermore, the European Parliament Research Service (EPRS) portrayed individuals' control on their own data as key to individual self‐ determination within the framework of data sovereignty (Madiega, 2020). The entangled nature of AI and data governance, as well as their centrality for the EU digital sovereignty quest, have been stressed in literature. Data and new technologies (including AI) governance are identiﬁed among the EU's strategic sectors for digital sovereignty, along with platform and infrastructure governance. This is key for the EU to emerge as a regulatory power amid a race that is dominated by the United States and China in terms of technological development (Roberts et al., 2021). Furthermore, when AI is involved in creating visual and written content, the spread of disinformation and misinformation can be multiplied, with negative impacts on democratic processes (Casares, 2018). political tradition, the EU has addressed these aspects with regulatory tools, the outcomes of which have raised scepticism in literature as the EU lacks a domestic industry capable of pushing EU‐made regulatory standards globally (Calderaro & Blumfelde, 2022). Overall, this demonstrates the centrality of AI and data governance in EU policy making amidst emerging questions of digital sovereignty.

The Chinese government has also engaged in a growing regulatory effort towards AI and data governance. For China, too, there is a matter of industrial advantage at stake, but also one of societal stability, with algorithms being capable of inﬂuencing people's preferences and behaviour (see, e.g., recommendations algorithms) and data ﬂows representing potentially sensitive ﬂows of information (Creemers, 2022). Starting in 2020, China engaged in the so‐called ‘Common Prosperity’ campaign. While not only limited to the digital economy, this campaign was strongly targeted at the platform economy, with antitrust crackdowns on companies capable of handling massive amounts of users' data. The antitrust crackdown was accompanied by a regulatory effort, with the introduction of the Personal Information Protection Law (‘PIPL’), the Data Security Law (‘DSL’), and the Internet Information Service Algorithm Recommendation Management Regulations among others. All these pieces of legislation were built upon the Cybersecurity Law, introduced in 2017 before the crackdown was even expected (Creemers, 2022).

While the new Chinese regulations on data and AI ﬁt within the rhetoric of Internet sovereignty, with data ﬂows being treated as a facet of cybersecurity, the Common Prosperity campaign is not a pure ‘digital sovereignty spree’. On the contrary, it was presented as an attempt to reduce disparities and increase people's trust in the economy and the authorities (Li, 2023). The new legislation makes companies responsible for data security as well as privacy preservation—although it falls short of making the government responsible vis‐à‐vis its citizens in terms of data protection (Creemers, 2022). In short, China's quest for AI and data regulation is oriented towards increasing citizens' trust in the digital economy and bringing stronger regulatory control on companies. In this sense, the Chinese effort resembles the European promise of individual digital sovereignty, although under different labels. This aspect is better illustrated throughout the analysis. However, it is worth mentioning that while data and personal information protection legislation in both the EU and China has a strong focus on data minimisation and individual self‐determination, the absence of state responsibility vis‐à‐vis citizens (i.e., the lack of options for redress in case of data protection violation by state authorities) in Chinese legislation is a major difference (Creemers, 2022). In other words, a similar wording of the legal text is made different in practice by the different nature of the Chinese state system in comparison to the EU. Furthermore, the EU has elaborated legislative unicums such as the right to be forgotten, which does not feature in Chinese legislation (Thumfart, 2022).

On this ground, this article questions the aforementioned European promise. Before delving into that, that next subsection contextualises the individual dimension of digital sovereignty.

Digital sovereignty and the individual dimension Digital sovereignty is a catchall term that has risen to popularity in the last decade, although its foundational moments date back to the 1990s in various places including the EU (Thumfart, 2022). At a high level, digital sovereignty is a process rather than a status. In other words, it is the process by which public authorities (re)assert control over policy aspects that have previously been controlled by private actors. Regulating the behaviour of platforms on digital markets and companies' business‐related handling of personal data fall under this conceptual umbrella. Indeed, data regulation is a broad ﬁeld in EU legislation,

One speciﬁc characteristic of the EU deﬁnition of digital sovereignty is individual empowerment or, in short, individual digital sovereignty. For example, the EPRS stresses that the ‘EU has adopted a very stringent framework for privacy and data protection, with the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) at its centre, and has introduced a protective “right to be forgotten” and a data portability right to enhance individuals’'control of their own data’ (Madiega, 2020). The empowerment of the individual vis‐à‐vis digital service providers is visible in the EU's AI legislation, although EU attempts at building a globally competitive AI industry are minimal (Calderaro & Blumfelde, 2022). Overall, individuals' capacity to choose which data to share and the possibility to always withdraw consent to data processing (when such processing is based on consent) is key to the EU's concept of digital sovereignty, along with users/citizens' possibility to choose which services to use free from impositions deriving from quasi‐monopolistic situations (Madiega, 2020). While the enforcement of these principles is open to discussion, these principles are incorporated into the texts of the GDPR, the Data Act, and the Data Governance Act, as well as the Digital Services Act and Digital Markets Act (DSA/DMA) (Zanﬁr‐Fortuna, 2024). In the EU, the individual element of digital sovereignty stems from the liberal idea of individual freedom entailing the capacity of a person to make choices about themselves (and, by extension, their data) in the public space (including online spaces) (Thumfart, 2022).

As anticipated above, the individual dimension is intertwined with digital sovereignty in China, too. Indeed, the EU's digital sovereignty concept was created to counter China's, while also remarkably overlapping it in its aspiration to counter the power of foreign private capitals (Thumfart, 2022). China had already introduced the Internet sovereignty and information sovereignty concepts starting from the 1990s (Creemers, 2020). The former encompasses sovereign control on the Internet infrastructure, while the latter is more oriented towards the ﬂow of information that takes place online (and ofﬂine alike). While the EU followed the US's laissez‐faire approach to digital development in the 1990s, China treated the domestic development of the Internet as an issue of social concern to be addressed at a regulatory level (Thumfart, 2022). This, however, should not be construed as total control by the state over companies through an all‐encompassing industrial policy. On the contrary, a strong regulatory effort to control the ﬂow of opinions online was accompanied by laissez‐ faire on the industrial level—at least until 2009, when state interventionism became strong with the earliest bans on Western platform companies (Mueller & Farhat, 2022). Although on a discursive level the match between the EU and Chinese concepts of digital sovereignty is weaker, the emerging data protection legislation brings both dimensions together (Liu, 2020): personal data protection and algorithmic transparency to enhance people's trust is coupled with an extraterritorial application of the Personal Information Protection Law borrowed from GDPR (Creemers, 2022). It is worth mentioning, however, that while GDPR is a longer and complex piece of legislation, PIPL is shorter and more vague as application is delegated to the jurisprudence and the forthcoming legislation. This, to be sure, is a typical process in Chinese legislative development (Timoteo, 2017). GDPR is however a cornerstone of EU legislation on digital technology and data‐driven economies, albeit being a generalistic data protection piece of legislation. Indeed, it is the ﬁrst legislation recognising the EU a form of extraterritorial reach (Gstrein & Zwitter, 2021), thus setting the scene for a EU capable of casting its inﬂuence with a potentially global scope. Discursively, it is often presented as the EU's main tool for citizens' protection and right enforcement in the digital realm all over the world. Justiciability notwithstanding, GDPR is also a discursive tool to show the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights has ‘teeth’ in the online realm (Zanﬁr‐ Fortuna, 2024). METHODS This article compares EU and Chinese AI and data governance legislation to question and problematise the EU's promise for individual digital sovereignty. To do so, the authors conduct a qualitative thematic analysis on selected pieces of legislation of China and the EU.

The selected pieces of EU legislation are the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), the AI Act, the Data Act and the Data Governance Act. The selected pieces of legislation for China are the Personal Information Protection Law (PIPL), the Data Security Law (DSL) and the Internet Information Service Algorithm Recommendation Management Regulations. The analysis also includes references to the Cybersecurity Law and the Critical Information Infrastructure Security Protection Regulations connected to it, as well as the Outbound Data Transfer Security Assessment Measure connected to the Data Security Law. These laws have been selected as they represent the core of what the two actors are building in terms of AI and data regulations (Creemers, 2022; Floridi, 2020). GDPR, introduced in 2016 and enforceable since 2018, is the cornerstone of the EU's data protection legislation. It presents legislative unicums such as the right to be forgotten and its extraterritorial application is a key facet of digital sovereignty through legislative means. The Data Governance Act and the Data Act were introduced in 2023 and 2024 respectively and aim to counterweight the GDPR in terms of setting the rules for data sharing and facilitating public–private data ﬂows. They aim at balancing public needs, business interests and privacy. In this view, these three pieces of legislation constitute the core of the EU's emerging data legislation and a benchmark for individual digital sovereignty (Zanﬁr‐Fortuna, 2024). Finally, the AI Act approved in 2024 is the EU's ﬁrst all‐ encompassing legislation on AI and it represents a ﬁrst‐of‐its‐kind on the global level (European Parliament, 2021). For these reasons, these pieces of legislation were chosen as a focus for this analysis.

In China, the 2016 Cybersecurity Law set the general rules for infrastructure, platform, and data security. All the following pieces of legislation refer more or less extensively to the Cybersecurity Law. In 2021, PIPL and DSL were introduced to set speciﬁc rules for handling personal data and for sharing sensitive data in general, with a view to setting the scope of state supervision over cross border ﬂows of sensitive data. The Outbound Data Transfer Security Assessment Measure was introduced after the DSL to provide further procedural speciﬁcations to the latter's security assessment mechanisms, while the Critical Information

On the AI side, the Internet Information Service Algorithm Recommendation Management Regulations is key in China's quest for platform (and AI) regulation: it mandates companies to disclose the code of recommendation algorithms to the public authority when such algorithms can impact public opinion. AI and data governance have been targeted as they feed onto each other, being data essential raw material for AI development, and because of the two actors' position in the AI competition: the EU is the world's largest common market by gross domestic product (GDP), made of wealthy economies and therefore capable of inﬂuencing the global AI market through regulation. China, other than being a large market, is an advanced technology producer and its domestic industrial players are among the world's major deployer of AI‐ powered surveillance technologies around the world, which has been problematised in terms of individual freedom (Dixon, 2022).

The analysis of these texts is qualitative and thematic, with codes elaborated throughout the analysis rather than ex ante (Castleberry & Nolen, 2018). This allows the analysts to observe the emergence of themes through the texts without any preconceived notions, although the target remains within the framework of the individual level of digital sovereignty. This analysis suffers from the fast‐developing nature of the legislation under analysis. Therefore, some considerations may be subject to change in the near future. Furthermore, other pieces of legislation are considered for context in the analysis albeit falling outside its scope. Nevertheless, the overall reasoning around the feasibility of individual‐level digital sovereignty as a concept remains valid as the concept is at least one decade old.

ANALYSIS To better gauge the scope of digital sovereignty‐related legislation in China, one should start from observing relations across pieces of legislation. In 2017, the Cybersecurity Law became China's staple legislation in terms of digital sovereignty: this law expressly provides for users' rights vis‐à‐vis actors who collect personal data without consent, it limits the use of personal data by private actors and sets responsibility for infrastructure and service management in terms of network security. The content of this law is then reﬁned and put into practice by other sets of laws. As illustrated in Table 2, these sets of laws address three main macro‐ﬁelds: data protection, platform regulation (algorithms) and infrastructure management. These represent the three regulatory realms of digital sovereignty, with data and platforms being key areas for AI governance.

On a similar note, this tripartition can be found in EU legislation as per Table 3. However, the stress on infrastructure is much milder. There is no prominent infrastructure‐oriented regulation in EU legislation, if we exclude the attempt to impose common cybersecurity regulatory frameworks between Member States.2 Instead, projects and programmes such as GAIA‐X and DNS4EU have been the EU's core instrument to address digital sovereignty at the infrastructural level. GAIA‐X is instead a public‐private attempt at creating a cloud‐based federated infrastructure for intra‐EU data exchange (GAIA‐X, 2023). Article 3 of the Internet Information Service Algorithmic Recommendation Management Provisions states that

Local cybersecurity and informatization departments are responsible for the overall coordination of nationwide algorithmic recommendation service governance and related supervision and management work within their administrative areas. The State Council telecommunications, public security, market regulation and other such relevant departments are, on the basis of their respective duties and responsibilities, responsible for algorithmic recommendation service supervision and management work within their administrative areas. (Internet Information Service Algorithmic Recommendation Management Provisions [互联网信息服务算法推荐管理规定], 2022)

In short, the Provisions establish governmental authority over supervising the functioning of recommendation algorithms. This piece of legislation moves forward in Chapter 3, ‘User Rights Protection’ (用户权益保护, yonghu quanyi baohu), by establishing users' rights to choose algorithmic recommendations options, including the possibility of stopping the algorithm from making recommendations based on personal characteristics. Furthermore, the law establishes the possibility for the user to delete the user‐based tags employed to build recommendations. Finally, article 24, comma 1, establishes that

Providers of algorithmic recommendation services with public opinion properties or having social mobilization capabilities shall, within 10 working days of providing services, report the provider's name, form of service, domain of application, algorithm type, algorithm self‐assessment report, content intended to be publicized, and other such information through the Internet information service algorithm ﬁling system […]. (Internet Information Service Algorithmic Recommendation Management Provisions [互联网信息服务算法推荐管理规定], 2022)

This provision is important because it creates an unprecedented legal requirement for public authority assessment of sensitive algorithms.

The Provisions on the Administration of Deep Synthesis Internet Information Services and the Interim Measures for the Management of Generative Artiﬁcial Intelligence Services play instead a more minor, targeted role. The former mandates mainly transparency rules, to make sure that the user knows that a content is AI‐generated, however realistic it may look, as well as the obligation to obtain consent before using someone's biometric data to generate a deep fake content.3 It also establishes speciﬁc obligations for deep synthesis services providers and users vis‐à‐vis existing legislations, such as the obligation not to spread defamatory contents or fake news (Provisions on the Administration of Deep Synthesis Internet Information Services [互联网信息服务深度合成管理规定], 2022). A similar reasoning applies to the Interim Measures for the Management of Generative Artiﬁcial Intelligence Services, which reiterate existing obligations vis‐à‐vis the Cybersecurity Law, the Data Security Law and the Personal Information Protection Law within the realm of generative AI (Interim Measures for the Management of Generative Artiﬁcial Intelligence Services [生成式人工智能服务管理暂行办法], 2023).

On the European side, the AI Act is the single piece of legislation that addresses the question of AI governance most centrally. The AI Act sets rights and duties for individual users. Article 13 establishes that high‐risk AI systems be equipped with jargon‐free instructions for users to understand their functioning and interpret the output, while Article 14 mandates ‘human‐in‐the‐loop’ supervision for high‐risk AI systems. For the purpose of implementing paragraphs 1, 2 and 3, the high‐risk AI system shall be provided to the deployer in such a way that natural persons to whom human oversight is assigned are enabled, as appropriate and proportionate: (a) to properly understand the relevant capacities and limitations of the high‐risk AI system and be able to duly monitor its operation, including in view of detecting and addressing anomalies, dysfunctions and unexpected performance; (b) to remain aware of the possible tendency of automatically relying or over‐ relying on the output produced by a high‐risk AI system (automation bias), in particular for high‐risk AI systems used to provide information or recommendations for decisions to be taken by natural persons; (c) to correctly interpret the high‐risk AI system's output, taking into account, for example, the interpretation tools and methods available; (d) to decide, in any particular situation, not to use the high‐risk AI system or to otherwise disregard, override or reverse the output of the high‐risk AI system; (e) to intervene in the operation of the high‐risk AI system or interrupt the system through a ‘stop’ button or a similar procedure that allows the system to come to a halt in a safe state.4

In short, the AI Act aims to provide a legal framework that ensures the protection and autonomy of users who interact with high‐risk AI systems. Simultaneously, the regulation assigns a level of accountability to the user in charge of such systems, regarding their impact on individuals affected by their application. In essence, the AI Act seeks to strike a balance between safeguarding both the user and those impacted by the AI's decisions. This approach differs from the Chinese legislation addressed earlier, and a comprehensive analysis of these differences can be found in the subsequent discussion section.

Data China introduced the Personal Information Protection Law (PIPL) and the Data Security Law (DSL) almost simultaneously in 2021. These two laws draw from provisions of the Cybersecurity Law in terms of data protection (not only in terms of personal data) and their security as a matter of national security more at large. This is coherent with the conception of Internet sovereignty that emerges in the aforementioned White Paper on the (status of the) Internet in China. The application of the Data Security Law has then been expanded and concretised by the Outbound Data Transfer Security Assessment Measures in 2022 (Data Security Law of the People's Republic of China [数据安全法], 2021; Outbound Data Transfer Security Assessment Measures [数据出境安全评估办法], 2022; Personal Information Protection Law of the People's Republic of China [个人信息保护法], 2021).

The Data Governance Act and Data Act, instead, present a new European attempt to regulate data ﬂows while facilitating their exchange in a protected way, with GDPR as the core legislation to abide by when it comes to personal data. The Data Governance Act and the Data Act have been in force for just a few months at the time of writing. Drawing from the legal text, the Data Act aims at binding companies—especially larger players—to share data and foster the data‐driven market provided that GDPR protections remain in place. It sets rules to avoid bigger companies from extracting data from smaller ones by leveraging their dominant position (Chapter IV, article 13) and it establishes users' right to request their data produced through the use of a product or service be transferred to a third party (Chapters II and III). In short, it aims at creating a level playing ﬁeld that sets the conditions for users and companies to beneﬁt from shared data, while empowering the user to request data, their sharing with third parties, and make choices on what to do with them. This element of individual empowerment is expressly stated in the Explanatory Memorandum attached to the legal text (p. 1) (European Union, 2023b).

DISCUSSION The question of the EU's implementation of digital sovereignty in its individual dimension is central in this article. This aspect has been cited as characteristic of the EU's conception of digital sovereignty time and again in EU policies. Literature ﬁnds that meaningful control over data is a key recurring aspect in EU policy discourse, often pinpointed on data protection infringement (Roberts et al., 2021). GDPR, Data Act and Data Governance Act ﬁt a broader data‐oriented legislation of the EU, with the Open Data Directive and the Database Directive to cite only a few other legislative means. These also sum to the Digital Services Act and the Digital Markets Act on the platform side. The same goes for China, where the analysed legislation adds to already existing norms. Tables 1 and 2 summarise this aspect, showing that the analysed legal text ﬁts within a broader legislative framework both in China and the EU. However, due to limited space, the texts analysed here have been chosen and deemed sufﬁcient to conduct the analysis in question.

In this article we maintain the individual dimension of digital sovereignty is a false promise as measures similar to those adopted by the EU are featured elsewhere, such as in China, despite a deeply different political system and no political reference to individual dimensions in digital sovereignty. In 2021, China adopts its ﬁrst comprehensive data protection law, namely the Personal Information Protection Law (PIPL), ‘to protect personal information rights and interests, standardise personal information handling activities, and promote the rational use of personal information’ (Article 1) (Calzada, 2022). In its unfolding, the Chinese law mirrors the European General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) with reference to the deﬁnitions of personal data/information and its territorial scope, although it is essential to recognize that these resemblances exist within distinct cultural, legal and geopolitical contexts. Both regulations emphasise the protection of personal data and grant data subjects an array of rights, ranging from access to cancellation. Similarly, they share a common emphasis on securing explicit consent for data processing activities, providing transparency and individual autonomy.

While these regulations may appear similar in some respects, they ultimately reﬂect the distinctive legal and cultural landscapes of Europe and China, with notable implications with respect of cross‐border transfer of personal data: while the GDPR poses at the foundation of the transfer the guarantee that ‘appropriate safeguards’ are present in the third country, the PIPL shifts its attention towards an assessment of security of the transfer (Creemers, 2022). Therefore While the EU legislation appears to feature a stronger element of extraterritorial protection, one that forced companies abroad to adapt their privacy and cookie policies to EU rules, the lack of a strong European industry abiding by EU law and providing a valid alternative to US and Chinese service and device providers weakens the EU regulation. Indeed, transatlantic data transfer have been a key issue in EU‐US relations for years, periodically addressed through ad hoc agreements (Murphy, 2022), but the vast majority of the EU users (including companies and research institutions) keeps relying on services provided by Silicon Valley‐based tech giants (Calderaro & Blumfelde, 2022). This led sectors of the literature to debate whether the global scope of GDPR will ever be fully realised

To be sure, the entry into force of the Data Act and Data Governance Act will contribute to the data protection framework established with GDPR in the medium run, also facilitating data ﬂows across the EU and regulating it when it comes to exchange with non‐EU actors. Similarly, however, China has adopted rules that limit and set procedures for cross‐border exchange of sensitive data, including personal data. In this view, the EU legislation is not unique, with the notable exception of being applicable to a block of 27 countries. Furthermore, projects such as GAIA‐X signal the attention of the EU on the infrastructural level as well, at least discursively (Adler‐Nissen & Eggeling, 2024).

It would be overly simplistic to downplay the EU's attempt to provide an individual dimension to digital sovereignty as a mere rhetorical effort. On the contrary, the EU is the world's largest free market area in terms of GDP and most companies have an interest in retaining access. To this end, they adapt to EU regulations and abide by it. Nonetheless, the notable absence of an alternative EU industry for EU users to opt for and the fact that the most prominent digital service providers in the EU are based abroad limits the EU's capacity to enforce its law (Chen et al., 2020). While the DSA and DMA seek to tackle this exact issue, their effect is yet to be seen at the time of writing.

To summarise, the absence of an EU industry capable of providing an alternative to US and Chinese industrial players for EU users, along with a set of regulations that only partially deviate from those adopted elsewhere in the world, we maintain at this stage the promise for an individual dimension in digital sovereignty remains unattainable.

CONCLUSION This article inquired: Is individual digital sovereignty attainable for the EU? In a nutshell, the EU's promise for individual digital sovereignty cannot hold through a purely regulatory effort unaccompanied by the establishment of a strong domestic industry. This article compared data and AI‐related regulations in the EU and China to observe whether the EU's promise of individual digital sovereignty is upheld in practice. The individual dimension of digital sovereignty is grounded on measures that are often similar to those found in other places, such as in China. Furthermore, the EU's reduced capacity to make its interests abided by outside its territory limits the likelihood of individual digital sovereignty to become a fulﬁlled promise. Indeed, the EU is inﬂuential outside its territory in regulatory terms as it is an attractive market for tech multinationals. On the other hand, the small number of EU industrial giants in the platform economy reduces the EU's capacity to enforce its values vis‐à‐vis industry from the rest of the world (Calderaro & Blumfelde, 2022). This research is grounded in qualitative analysis and a contextual comparison between the EU and China on speciﬁc, yet politically relevant, topics, namely data and AI governance. Indeed, the EU may prove better capable of carrying inﬂuence in other ﬁelds on a global level, thus the results of this article should not be overgeneralised to other ﬁelds. Furthermore, the Data Act and Data Governance Act have just entered into force at the time of writing and their full effect will not be measurable for a few years. Future research can focus on the infrastructure element to study digital sovereignty‐related policies and their impact. As far as platforms and data are concerned, the application of the Data Act, Data Governance Act and AI Act as they unfold will reveal new hints for future research. Notwithstanding limitations and the fast pace of legal and technological evolution in this ﬁeld, this article seeks to provide a constructive critique of the EU promise for individual digital sovereignty in the context of burgeoning technological evolution.
