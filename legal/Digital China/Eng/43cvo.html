Critical views on the phenomenon of EU digital sovereignty through the prism of global data governance reality: main obstacles and

challenges Lusine Vardanyan & Hovsep Kocharyan*

Summary: This article is devoted to determining the role and place of the EU in the international information space, as well as identifying problem­ atic aspects and existing challenges that the EU faces in implementing its digital strategy in the field of data governance and protection. In the context of the growing influence of such key political figures as, for example, the USA and China, which have dominant influence on the digital economy, the issue of proper ensuring the digital sovereignty of the EU in the field of global data governance comes to the fore. This is due to the fact that it is the digital sovereignty that should guarantee the strategic autonomy of the EU in the digital world in the context of the EU’s increasing dependence on foreign technologies and services and a shortage of investments in the digital sector. In this research, the authors focus on the need to develop and use a model of multilateral participation in the field of data governance and protection in the digital world, which, in turn, implies prevention of weakening the role of the state in regulating the digital market, since it is the states (including the EU) that are the primary guarantors of human rights protection in the digital world.

Keywords: digital sovereignty, data protection, digitalization, EU, digital strategy.

For example, it is impossible to equate the digital sovereignty of the EU, China or the USA, because we are talking about different phenomena, even though we use the same term. We can even point out the significant differences between the conceptual approaches to data governance and protection policies that the EU and the USA adhere to. They have different legal systems in the field of personal data governance and protection, as they are based on two radically different concepts of privacy. For example, the EU, after the adoption of the GDPR, created a single (centralized) mechanism for the pro­ tection of personal data for its citizens, based on the right to privacy, formulated in the framework of the European Convention on Human Rights (1950) (,,ECHR“) and the EU Charter on Fundamental Rights (2000) (,,CFT“), and subsequently developed by the case-law of the European Supranational Courts.

As for the USA, its legal system, unlike the EU, proceeds from the need to regu­ late the data governance and protection in certain areas of economy. In other words, in the USA, privacy is protected only in the context of commercial activity, where the data subject is considered not as a citizen, but as a consumer. This can even be seen by the example of what institutional mechanisms are used for data governance and protection in the EU and the USA. So, while in the EU the centralized national data protection authorities (DPA) are responsible for data protection, in the USA the Federal Trade Commission acts as the main data protection authority, i.e. an independent agency of the US government, which is designed to protect the rights and legitimate interests of consumers.13 As I. Jolly correctly observes: ,,(…) in contrast to the European Union’s data protection approach, which in many ways represents the gold standard of privacy protections, the dominant approach in the United States is grounded in consumer protection regulations (…) In stark contrast to the rights enjoyed by European consumers, American consumers do not possess ,,a right to be forgotten“. Nor is there a single federal notification law that mandates notification in case of a data breach“.14 Indeed, the GDPR and mainly its right to be forgotten, developed by the CJEU’s case-law, are trying to strengthen the EU’s digital sovereignty through „putting individuals in control of their data“ and turning the EU into ,,a standard-setter in privacy and data protection“.15

The EU’s digital sovereignty as a simple digitalization of Westphalian sovereignty in the field of data governance?

However, how to define such an EU territory in cyberspace, and how to ensure the functioning of the EU in cyberspace, which is considered to be ,,lim­ itles“? And in general, can the digital sovereignty of the EU be considered as a simple digitalization of Westphalian sovereignty, and digital space – as a new dimension of territory (along with land, water and air spaces)? The practice of international law shows that with the development of international relations, it is not the category of territory that changes, but rather its substantive aspect. We believe that it is not necessary to deny the importance of the territory as the legal basis for the exercise of sovereignty in cyberspace, but rather to rethink the content of this category in the digital world. This is especially important in conditions when the EU puts the issue of digital sovereignty as the basis of its digital policy on its political agenda.

At the same time, just rethinking the category of territory is not enough to determine the boundaries of the EU’s digital sovereignty: it is necessary to un­ derstand, on the basis of what principle should the borders of the EU territory be determined in cyberspace. We should not forget, that the development of the category of territory in international law has always been based on a certain principle. For example, when water and air spaces were included in the cate­ gory of territory, the land space, on which the State is located, was considered as such a principle (for example, when determining the height or width of the extension of the sovereignty of the State), while in the case of diplomatic and consular missions, water and aircrafts, scientific stations in Antarctica and/or space objects, the principle of flag was considered as the basis. However, unlike other categories of territory, cyberspace has a specific and multifaceted nature, which does not allow it to be viewed through the classical (Westphalian) understanding of the territory and the already existing principles for determining such a territory.39 It is not difficult to notice that the classical territory, whether it is land, water or air, always has physically tangible and geographical boundaries, already given by nature, while in cyberspace, the sub­ jects of the digital world themselves become the creator of such a territory and can create as many cyber territories as they please, for example, by developing new websites. In the conditions of its „limitless“ nature, cyberspace becomes similar to outer space, only in contrast to the fact that at the current stage of the development of human civilization, the digital world is available to us, while the outer space is not yet. If we draw parallels, then the situation with the devel­ opment of the digital world can be compared with the expansion of the territory of some European states (like Spain, Portugal and France) during the Age of Colonization at the expense of then unknown territories of America, Asia and Africa (i.e. terra nullius for Europeans). However, if in the offline world such a process of territorialization was easy, for example, through the principle of flag40 ot through concluded international treaties between European Powers (such as Treaty of Tordesillas (1494)),41 then in the online world, the situation is much more complicated not only geographically, but also subjectively.

In the above context, as M. Zook notes, the digital world („Internet“) be­ comes an incentive for the development of ,,new geographies“ of interaction between people and territories in technical, social, political, cultural, economic and other areas.42 In such cases, the question naturally arises: how to determine the limits of EU jurisdiction in the ,,limitless“ cyberspace, and what principle should underlie it? When trying to answer this question, a number of diffi­ culties arise. 6.

Conclusion The EU’s attempts to assert its digital sovereignty in the digital sphere face the following obvious challenges: 1) the ambiguity of understanding the EU’s digital sovereignty, 2) the EU’s increasing dependence on foreign digital services and technologies and 3) the lack of investments in the EU’s digital sector. Taking into account these obstacles, the EU is trying to overcome them by developing its digital strategies, as well as by flexibility in the interpretation of its data protec­ tion laws, where the case-law of the CJEU plays a significant role. However, the effectiveness of the EU strategies can only be assessed in the future perspectives, since it is not ,,mature“ enough yet.

The study shows that in the existing situation all the parties of the digital world (including the EU) are in a ,,no-win“ situation, and over time this situation is only getting worse as each of them tries to establish their hegemony in the digital economy (for example, as in the case of trading wars between the USA and China). We believe it is high time to seriously think about a new ,,digital Westphal“ system, only in the conditions of which it will be possible to avoid future digital cataclysms and informational wars between the leading subjects. It seems that for effective governance of the digital sector, it is necessary to use an approach involving the widest possible range of subjects interested in defining common rules for ensuring free data flow based on mutual trust at the global level.

However, the new system should not reduce the role of the state (including the EU) in regulating digital market. In the conditions of ,,digital chaos“, the state should play the fundamental role in the digital sector. We should not forget, that only the states (including the EU) can ultimately protect the rights and legitimate interests of their citizens as the real guarantors of the protection of human rights in the digital world.
