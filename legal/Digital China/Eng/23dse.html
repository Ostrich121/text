In the states of the European Union (EU), the question currently raised is to what extent dependence on technologies from the USA and China will have a lasting impact on state sovereignty. The concept of digital sovereignty represents the EU's efforts to compensate for the deficits of the past decades caused by an inadequate positioning of Europe as a location for software and hardware development. Autocratic states use the path of digital autarky, the USA a path of liberalisation and high degrees of openness. In the EU, on the other hand, regulation, data protection and liberal values developed over centuries play a major role in the less pronounced IT development. The path of European states to more digital sovereignty has been addressed politically as an “action plan”, but there is still no common understanding or definition of what digital sovereignty exactly means, where the EU and thus also an individual European state stands. There is a lack of a target and a measurable index as well as evaluated measures derived from it. The present article articulates the basis, namely the common understanding and the definition of digital sovereignty. It places the concepts of digitalisation and state sovereignty in a historical framework and locates them in the current literature, then analyses digital sovereignty as a composite term and places it in the context of current research. Finally, a definition is proposed that can serve as the basis for further research to identify an index of digital sovereignty. This definition can also become the basis for EU legislation to implement the “action plan”.

1 | INTRODUCTION

Sovereignty and digitization were long understood as two separate, unrelated concepts. But this is now over: the growing importance of information and communication technology (ICT), especially in this millennium, has recently reached what is now an outstanding social, economic, and political importance (Kukkola et al., 2019). And therefore, the article focuses on the emerging concept of digital sovereignty, the understanding of which, however, varies in many ways. Therefore, increased attention should be paid to the definition of the term in order to end the current confusion of terms.

The more socially or politically relevant the ICT industries are, the greater the impact of the technologies used in these industries on the performance of public life, politics and society. In the meantime, this has even given rise to a new, interdisciplinary field of research that examines the interplay between politics, society and technology: Science and Technology Studies (STS) (DeNardis et al., 2020, p. 87ff). In STS, tools are specified that can describe the multiple links between technological, social, and political aspects, as well as technological and non-technological actors, and analyze interrelationships.

For the relevance of technology impact on society or politics, the construction and operation of mobile networks or communication networks provides a good example. If the communication networks are only available to a limited extent, a large part of social communication would be reduced, interaction between citizens or with state organisations or parties would not be possible due to the lack of availability of Internet access. This would have a direct impact on the state's ability to act, which must ensure that communication networks are available to maintain government and administrative action. Availability means that the systems are technically available, but also controllable, that is the dependencies on third parties are reduced. This is called technological autonomy, which is particularly relevant since a large part of the communication technology is produced by Chinese manufacturers an technology from European companies is used only to a limited extent.

Another example is the use of software systems. Predominantly, software for office and communication systems from US manufacturers is used in private households, but also in government institutions. Cloud services and server farms hosting private and corporate data are predominantly operated by US-American companies: 92% of all electronically stored data in Western countries is thus stored in the United States. The big US manufacturers such as Google and Facebook set de facto standards, as Musiani notes (DeNardis et al., 2020, p. 91). The question arises as to what the impact would be if these systems or data were not available or only available to a limited extent. Could this be an attack on democracy and the fundamental values of freedom? Attempts to influence state actions and also elections by autocratic forces make us sit up and take notice.

So what technological dependence has there been on manufacturers from non-European states in order to still be able to act independently, and what impact does this have on state sovereignty? This is the subject of debate about Europe's digital sovereignty and is a topic of concern for European Union (EU) policy-makers (Leyen, 2020). There are already calls for an “action plan for greater digital sovereignty” to identify critical technologies, manage dependencies on suppliers and rigorously monitor progress in Europe (Merkel et al., 2021). However, the debate is still very unspecific and clarity in the definition and quantification of terms is needed.

Since the Middle Ages, the establishment and preservation of state sovereignty has been the focus of political debate in the European states. The concept of sovereignty emerged in late medieval Europe with the discussion of philosophers on the internal and external independence of states and on the effective exercise of state power. In the sense of a modern form of state, it was first mentioned in 1576 (Bodin, 1994). Bodin is thus regarded as the founder of the concept of sovereignty. In the following centuries, this also led to monarchical absolutism through the state-theoretical explanations of Hobbes, in whose main work “Leviathan” the foundation of the state is defined as a social contract, which includes a submission contract: citizens transfer their power to a sovereign, to whose will they submit to. The “state-legal sovereignty and sovereignty of a sovereign” become one (Kriele, 2003, p. 56ff). Those teachings marked the transition from the Middle Ages to modern Europe. In addition to the sovereignty, the so-called Westphalian model1 was above all characterised by a territorial principle. State borders delineated the boundaries of a political, legislative and executive space of the sovereign (Zimmer, 2008, p. 55). The three key principles of the Westphalian model – the principle of sovereignty, the territorial principle and the principle of legality – have remained stable to this day.

In his reflections on “governmentality” in the 1970s, Foucault proposes a division into the following categories: “The form of power is government; the object of government is the population; and the means of government is the political economy.” (Mohabbat Kar et al., 2018, p. 189). What is essential with Foucault is that the citizen is also part of the exercise of power, part of governing. There is talk of the “(...) change of speech from power as possession to power as a general structuring element of the social (...)” (Saar, 2007, p. 32).

He sees the government as the only state power, but as part of society, in that there are other stakeholders besides the state, the citizen as an individual and society, his approach corresponds to multistakeholderism which also plays an important role in the context of current scientific research on Internet-governance (DeNardis & Raymond, 2013).

Foucault's ideas thus address similar aspects from the perspective of state sovereignty as the discussions on Internet-governance, which is also confirmed by Braman. She notes that he has influenced many mainstream political scientist (DeNardis et al., 2020, p. 42). In the context of Internet governance, she also refers to Cerny, who describes the Foucauldian “governmentality” as the transformation of states in their underlying rationality. The transformation resulting from the effects of, for example, uncertainty and diversity in a globalising world becomes necessary in order to be able to survive in economic and political competition with others (Cerny, 2010, p. 3). The focus on the nation-state in recent decades has changed considerably as a result of globalisation, or at least requires that a multinational, global, overarching view of the role of the state becomes necessary in addition to the nation-state perspective.

Therefore, Foucault's approach is particularly suited to link state sovereignty, “good governance” and “governmentality” with the ideas and challenge of digitization and to support the necessary transformation. Foucault calls this “the art of governmentality in a more open world” (Cerny, 2010, p. 8).

As the approaches to defining digital sovereignty are discussed in many ways in the literature, the author has divided them into three main categories for a better overview for this article.

First, digital sovereignty in the sense of sovereignty in cyberspace. In the first growth phase of the Internet, when only 30 million people and thus only 1% of today's users used the Internet, the idea of a Westphalian-counter-model sovereign cyberspace independent of states arose, which was to be created without territorial borders and with its own rules for the users. This was to create an opposition to the traditional claim to sovereignty. With the encroachment of the state on the Internet, activists sought a dedicated space, cyberspace, that would be free of state regulations and state sovereignty (Turner, 2008, p. 13). This approach risked upsetting the nation-state, territorially limited and sovereign state system that had grown over 250 years. The discourse of the approach is well reflected in a manifesto by John Perry Barlow entitled “A Declaration of the Independence of Cyberspace”, in which he sees the colonial and, in his view, freedom-restricting rules of traditional states in cyberspace overcome and physical intervention possibilities for regulation reduced. He propagates a new cyberworld that is equally accessible to all, without privileges arising from origin, economic or military strength (Barlow, 1996). Although Barlow's approaches did not catch on, ideas related to the sovereignty of cyberspace are still present. In an essay published by Milton Mueller, he describes the idea of “popular sovereignty in cyberspace”, in which the state plays a subordinate role in cyberspace and the main actors should be the people who act in cyberspace. He equates popular sovereignty with “multistakeholder participation” and thus questions state sovereignty over the Internet (Mueller, 2017). Even if a separate cyber sovereignty space has not been established, but a certain degree of self-organising legal freedom is evident on the Internet (Dienstbühl & Weber, 2014).

As a second category for digital sovereignty, the concept of data sovereignty in the sense of data security. The question “How secure is my data?” is just as much a concern for citizens as it is for companies or state institutions. In this context, companies focus on aspects of digital law and data protection, while the state discussion aims more at maintaining national security. In addition, the state is given a role in protecting the data of its citizens, since in a democratic nation state, citizens demand the right to have the state protect their rights. Absolute data sovereignty means the freedom to decide anew at any time whether and how data is stored. The German Ethics Council distinguishes data sovereignty from data protection and defines it in the context of the use of big data in health care as informational self-determination, which is ethically as well as constitutionally founded (Deutscher Ethikrat, 2017, p. 40): even if individual data do not initially appear to be worth protecting, the combined analysis of individual data or big data analyses can result in information that is worth protecting. Or, on the other hand, data that is worth protecting at one point in time, such as the location of a person, is no longer worth protecting at the next point in time because the person has changed his or her position. Data protection and data sovereignty must therefore be rethought at any time and will no longer be static in the future. Data sovereignty will therefore require greater “normative accompaniment” in the future. The Ethics Council speaks of an “informational freedom of the individual in a just and solidary society” (Deutscher Ethikrat, 2017, pp. 253–261). Also, in the context of data protection considerations, the interests of states extend beyond the territorial boundaries of their own state. This means that data protection for the citizens of a state is somewhat at odds with the state sovereignty principle of independence from other sovereigns if data is stored outside the territorial sovereignty of a state (Goldsmith, 1998, p. 475ff; Woods, 2018).

In this context, the question arises as to whether and how a state can sovereignly store and make available data on its territory. This applies equally to data from citizens, companies and state institutions. For citizens and state institutions, this is clearly regulated (Charter of Fundamental Rights of the European Union of 2012, Article 8). The EU as a community of values protects the sovereignty of data and enables so-called informational self-determination (Schmitz, 2004, p. 692). This means that IT services must be transparent, and users must be given the opportunity to decide for themselves which data is collected, stored, deleted and processed by whom (Schmitz, 2004, p. 692). Other considerations for digital sovereignty of EU states focus on an exclusively technological, that is hardware and software-oriented definition. The construction of dedicated submarine cables, territorially localised data routing, national, encrypted mail systems, territorial, local data storage also for cloud services or own encryption technologies are politically motivated and technologically feasible approaches to achieve greater autarky and thus sovereignty (Maurer et al., 2015). The VDE7 distinguishes digital from technological sovereignty by considering aspects of digital sovereignty such as “trust”, “IT security” and “data security” as well as “access to raw materials”, “access to components” and “access to knowledge” as sub-areas of an overarching technological sovereignty (Illgner, 2020). So what does it mean to be technologically sovereign? It is not about completely mastering all technologies and achieving maximum autarky. Rather, it is about understanding and evaluating the relevance of specific technologies to the economy, society and politics, and then finding a way to use these technologies sovereignly, that is as far as possible without dependence on or influence from others. It is about maintaining the ability of independent action and decision-making (Illgner, 2020, p. 8). KETs are particularly relevant for the sovereignty of a state. Digital sovereignty is receiving the highest political attention in Europe: “[T]he ability that Europe must have to make its own decisions in accordance with its own values and rules (...)” is what Commission President v.d. Leyen formulates as the basis of digital sovereignty (Leyen, 2020).

However, it remains unclear what is meant by digital sovereignty. In the absence of a definition, conflicting security, economic, and industrial perspectives may also influence the understanding of the term. Economically or politically motivated positions are formulated, but there is no clear national or EU strategy for a future positioning of the European states for higher digital sovereignty. In this respect, the currently discussed measures of a European “action plan” can only have a limited effect, as the title of the article describes.

Digital sovereignty of a state can undoubtedly be achieved by becoming autark and investing in its own technology development and data security so that it is independent of others. Examples of this are Russia, China, Brazil and others (Belli, 2021). In authoritarian states, a largely territorialised Internet policy of isolation in the sense of the sovereign preservation of the state can thus be observed (Belli, 2021; Pohle & Thiel, 2019). The concept of sovereignty of autocratic states is also underpinned with digital sovereignty in order to justify autocratically motivated sovereignty internally and strong economic and regulatory policies externally (Arsène, 2021; Budnitsky & Jia, 2018; Creemers, 2020). Examples include Russia and China. Characteristics of these states are digital autarky, technological isolation and control of citizens.

This is not in line with the values of the EU and will not be the way of the EU member states. Democratic states define digital sovereignty more in terms of data protection, protection of the interests of their own citizens and, to some extent, economic aspects (Pohle & Thiel, 2019). When Germany assumed the EU Council Presidency in 2020, the strengthening of digital sovereignty was elevated to the European level. The term “technological sovereignty” was used at European level for the first time (Bauer & Erixon, 2020; Pohle, 2020). Technological sovereignty in the approach of national or European autarky, that is a policy of digital isolation, cannot be the goal, however, because sovereignty in digitization must go hand in hand with the sovereignty of the EU states in the context of European values.

This is the key message of the citation from Commission President v.d. Leyen. Complete independence in the sense of autarky is therefore not intended, nor is complete dependence.

It is therefore a question of striking a balance between autarky and autonomy. It will be a path that, on the one hand, allows limited technological, economic and thus also political dependence in order to remain capable of action and, on the other hand, promotes autonomy or even autarky in individual cases as a method of limitation. And even more than a question, the concrete definition of that balance will be the solution to digital sovereignty.

In the future, dependence, autonomy or autarky of technology will exist simultaneously in a digital sovereign state. The more relevant a KET is, the greater the autonomy or even autarky should be. The ability to act in the sense of dependence, autonomy or autarky can concern the following sub-areas, based on (Hoepner et al., 2016, p. 89):

1. Definition of KETs in close cooperation between politics, research and industry

2. Development of KETs and corresponding cooperation from research and industry, national, European and international

3. Ability to evaluate technology and its integration into existing ecosystems with a focus on trustworthiness and security

4. Risk assessment also of supply chains

5. Transparency of system architectures, associated interfaces, the exchange and storage of data

6. Choice of technologies, architectures and suppliers

An alternative path could therefore be for the EU to specify which KETs are relevant for the EU today and in the future and agree on a common definition of digital sovereignty. Digital sovereignty is a term often used by policy makers and industry, but it is neither clearly defined nor measurable. To pave the way for EU states to successfully develop the European Digital Sovereignty Action Plan, a common understanding of digital sovereignty, the ability to measure a level of digital sovereignty and a target picture of how digitalisation should be managed to remain sovereign are needed.
