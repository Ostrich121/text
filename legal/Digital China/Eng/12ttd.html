The trust deficit. EU bargaining for access and control over cloud infrastructures Filippo Gualtiero Blancato and Madeline Carr University College London (UCL), London, United Kingdom

ABSTRACT European Union (EU) governments rely on a handful of American cloud providers (hyperscalers) for critical state functions, which creates strategic dependencies. The EU data sovereignty agenda aims to manage such dependence, yet hyperscalers criticise it for excluding leading industry players from the EU market and slowing economic growth. In this paper, we argue that sovereignty policies in the cloud are the result of a trust deficit between European governments and American hyperscalers. The trust deficit consists of a continuous bargain by European governments for greater access and control over hyperscalers’ infrastructures. Hyperscalers, in turn, respond to such demands mainly through technology. But is their response sufficient to address the trust deficit? Drawing from technical documents and expert interviews, we find that states and hyperscalers have a fundamentally different understanding of sovereignty; that technology alone cannot address the trust deficit; and that data sovereignty policies in the cloud can be compatible with the digital economy and cybersecurity. Our study is a starting point for further research on the political economy of trust in the governance of digital technologies.

Existing research on digital sovereignty highlights governments’ struggle to reduce their dependence on foreign technology service providers. Yet it does not explain how this struggle unfolds in practice. In this paper, we argue that explaining the European sovereign approach to cloud computing requires understanding the political and economic dimensions of trust. Specifically, we argue that cloud sovereignty is the expression of a trust deficit between European governments6 and American hyperscalers. For states cannot achieve full sovereignty over a technology delivered by compa­ nies operating a globally distributed digital infrastructure. Rather, states have to actively manage their dependence on foreign vendors by bargaining for greater access and control over the infrastructure on which their data are stored and processed. To address the trust deficit and comply with data sovereignty policies, hyperscalers have tailored their services to the European market, offering bespoke technology solutions based on data residency, advanced cryptography, and granular access controls. Such solutions promise to protect the confidentiality of customers’ data while ensuring the continuity of hyperscalers’ business operations in the European market. Trusted clouds – joint ventures between a hyperscaler and a local European company – are also part of their offerings.7 Against this background, this paper puts forward three arguments. First, that European governments and American hyperscalers espouse two different conceptions of sovereignty. The former champion an understanding of sovereignty based on broader notions of strategic autonomy, while the latter frame it as the outcome of specific technical and organisational sol­ utions. The result is that the solutions proposed by the hyperscalers do not match exactly the strategic problems perceived by European governments. This brings us to the second argument, which is that technology alone cannot address the trust deficit. In an attempt to comply with data sover­ eignty rules while ensuring their business continuity in the European

Most advanced cryptographic techniques, for instance, are not commercially available at scale and can be vulnerable to cyberattacks. Moreover, such solutions do not address the economic dimension of the trust deficit, which relates to the uneven distri­ bution of cloud technology between the US and the EU. The third argument is that, contrary to what the industry maintains, there is not enough evidence to suggest that sovereignty policies will inevitably harm the EU cybersecurity and its digital economy more broadly. While cybersecurity thrives on harmo­ nisation and data sharing, findings from the study suggest that a sovereign approach to cloud computing may not be inevitably incompatible with such principles.

We support the above arguments through an interview-based research strategy. We interview stakeholders from the European institutions, the hyperscalers, and independent cybersecurity experts (total N = 17) to gather insights about European sovereignty policies in the cloud computing sector. We also analyse technical and policy documents by American hypers­ calers – with a focus on AWS, Microsoft, and Google Cloud – produced in the period 2021–2024. These documents shed light on the hyperscalers’ technology approach to sovereignty and detail the technical offerings underpinning it.

The paper contributes to various strands of literature, including the growing literature on digital sovereignty, weaponised interdependence, the governance of cross-border data flows, as well as cybersecurity more broadly. While the extant literature has conceptualised digital sovereignty as an attempt to gain more control over the digital,8 it has not delved into how Member States actually bargain for such control. By focusing on the infrastructure layer of the cloud, this study introduces the concept of the trust deficit to better understand how such bargain unfolds in practice. From an empirical perspective, the paper provides original qualitative data on the nature and impact of cloud sovereignty policies in the EU market. From a theoretical perspective, it brings together diverse bodies of research – political economy, information security, and legal scholarship, and more – to illuminate the political, legal, and economic dimension of the trust deficit in the cloud.

The trust deficit and sovereignty in the cloud Early studies on sovereignty and cloud computing have been mostly con­ cerned with the security implications of governments’ use of foreign cloud providers. These contributions mostly came from legal scholars and focused on the unresolved tensions between the cross-border nature of data flows on the one hand, and states’ attempts to impose their jurisdiction on such flows on the other.9 Data sovereignty policies, in this context, were not thoroughly conceptualised, but were nevertheless linked to the need to prevent foreign entities’ access to a nation’s data. As early as 2012, Irion noted that, since control over information is an essential component of state sovereignty, governments were likely to adopt data sovereignty strat­ egies when migrating their data to the cloud (Irion, 2012). Other scholars suggested that sovereignty rules are premised on a flawed understanding of the cloud’s transnational business model and are incompatible with the European commitments’ to free data flows. In this sense, sovereignty rules bely a protectionist approach bent on favouring the emergence of domestic cloud providers (Hon et al., 2016). Similarly, other contributions suggest that, while the European reliance on foreign cloud providers can pose issues to data confidentiality and availability, sovereignty policies like data localisation or nationality requirements on the use of cloud providers are a disproportion­ ate response to these concerns (Michels et al., 2023). Therefore, the legal debate on sovereignty and the cloud has been defined by two competing views, one that posits the ‘un-territoriality’ of data – which fundamentally challenges states’ ability to control information and communication flows (Daskal, 2016, 2018) – and an opposite view which contends that data flows can be reconciled with states’ legitimate claim to sovereignty over their borders through appropriate legal instruments (Woods, 2016, 2018).

With the emergence of the European agenda on strategic autonomy, the academic literature has linked digital sovereignty to the issue of control. Floridi, for example, defines digital sovereignty as governments’ struggle to increase control over critical data, software, hardware and infrastructure10 or, as Sheikh puts it, across the technology stack.11 Further, Fratini et al. have identified four models – the rights-based model, the market-oriented model, the centralisation model, and the state-based model – depending on how and why digital sovereignty is pursued (Fratini et al., 2024).

A special issue on the subject by Falkner et al. links digital sovereignty to the issue of control more systematically and attempts to gauge the extent to

which digital sovereignty initiatives lead to policy change in the EU.12 From a more markedly discursive perspective, and with a stronger focus on the cloud, Adler-Nissen and Eggeling point out that digital sovereignty has different meanings but broadly relates to issues of control (Adler-Nissen & Eggeling, 2024), while Baur credits the concept with bringing together secur­ ity concerns with broader European objectives of fostering innovation and triggering strategic investments in EU digital markets (Baur, 2023).

In this study, we build on the above definitions of digital sovereignty as control while filling two gaps in the literature. First, the extant literature on digital sovereignty and the cloud focuses mostly on discursive practices, without delving into how EU governments bargain with cloud hyperscalers. An exception in this regard is an in-depth study by Rone on cloud sover­ eignty which, however, focuses on a single policy tool (the European Cloud Certification Scheme) and on national preference formation around cloud sovereignty (Rone, 2024). Second, our study sheds light on the perspec­ tive of the private sector by analysing the technology solutions that hypers­ calers put forward to respond to the EU cloud sovereignty agenda. So far, such perspective has largely been missing, with the exception of Kemmerling and Trampusch, whose study on technology companies’ infrastructural power, however, limits its discussion of cloud computing to the case of Germany.13 To understand how states bargain in practice with the hyperscalers, we argue that it is necessary to integrate a social and technical understanding of the concept of trust into the analysis. Specifically, we argue that cloud sovereignty is the expression of a trust deficit between European govern­ ments and American hyperscalers. This is because states cannot reasonably achieve full sovereignty over a globally distributed digital infrastructure deliv­ ered by a handful of foreign providers, hence they necessarily need to trust – or relinquish control to – American hyperscalers.

The concept is developed inductively from our analysis of empirical data. Trust, like sovereignty, is an amorphous concept, which can assume different meanings depending on the context in which is used. We build on Gambetta’s seminal definition of trust as the assurance that one person or entity will perform according to a specific expectation (Gambetta, 1990). As such, trust denotes a condition of reliance on a third party14 and it is a key concern whenever there is interdependence between two entities (Mayer et al., 1995). Studies in the social sciences highlight how institutions shape trust (Farrell, 2009), while so-called ‘high-trust’ societies are associ­ ated to a nation’s economic growth and competitiveness (Fukuyama, 1996; Korczynski, 2000; Lorenz, 1993), increased social capital (Putnam, 2000), and technology adoption (Gefen et al., 2003). Trust is also a core tenet of information security, where it is understood as the confidence that a product or service will behave as expected.15 The emphasis here,

however, is less on the relational quality of trust and more on how technol­ ogy solutions enforce trust. For example, the mathematics behind strong encryption enhances trust because it protects data from unauthorised access.16 There have been attempts at doing away with trust through tech­ nological fixes.17 However, most experts agree that technology does not eliminate the need for trust, it simply shifts it from humans to algorithms and protocols (Doctorow, 2022; Schneier, 2019; Thompson, 1984). Thus, the issue of trust remains not only socially relevant, but politically salient, especially when governments are reliant on digital technologies for critical functions. Moreover, trust always entails information asymmetries and cannot be measured (Duenas-Cid and Calzati, 2023). Trust is ‘contextual’ and ‘asymmetric’, meaning that a party may trust another at a given time, but always takes a risk in trusting.18 As put by Burt, ‘trust is the most central aspect of any transaction and also the least quantifiable’, hence ‘we may trust, but we cannot verify’ (Burt, 2020). In the context of a technology service whose trustworthiness cannot be verified, trust could be ‘nothing more than a marketing campaign’.19 All this explains why, when it comes to cloud sover­ eignty, the tensions between European governments and American hypersca­ lers are essentially a matter of trust. Far from being a mere technical footnote in a government’s migration to the cloud, trust is linked to legitimate national security concerns. And yet, as Schneier notes, technology companies’ response to the issue is markedly different from how policymakers approach it. While tech­ nologists focus on technical controls that can assure a specific technology will behave as expected or ensure confidentiality, policymakers consider broader strategic objectives. Governments, for example, might be reassured by the confi­ dentiality assurances brought by technological and organisational controls, but still not trust a foreign provider with their sensitive data because that entails an element of dependence on a foreign entity that they are not willing to tolerate (Schneier, 2020).

In this sense, we posit that the dynamic of the trust deficit affects mostly power relations between states and hyperscalers, rather than those between private sector customers and hyperscalers. This is because states are more likely to consider their excessive dependence on foreign providers – or the lack of domestic alternatives – as strategically relevant. Private companies, particularly in regulated sectors, might also face concerns related to excessive dependencies. The financial sector, for instance, has been concerned for years with the excessive reliance of banks on a few cloud providers.20 Nevertheless, it can be expected that considerations about dependencies in the private sector will relate mostly to issues of resilience. Once concerns about continu­ ity of operations, system availability, and interoperability are addressed, private companies may be able to purchase cloud services in the pursuit of scalability and seamless integration while foregoing concerns about sover­ eign capabilities.

The trust deficit also highlights a crucial power and temporal dimension to the relation between the EU and the US. The act of EU governments trusting American hyperscalers with hosting their data and managing their digital infrastructures might leave them more dependent on American technology and result in a long-term loss of strategic capabilities, further deepening the present dependence on American technology providers. Such concerns are compounded by the acknowledgment that there is a link between the hyperscalers’ dominance of the European market and the US ability to project influence in the region.21 As shown in the empirical section of this study, the degree of trust that EU governments decide to place on American hyperscalers seems to be also a function of size and industrial capabilities: states that are capable of mobilising resources to develop their industrial base, like France and Germany, have shown to be more sensitive to the issue of trust. Conversely, smaller European states who cannot develop homegrown capabilities seem to be less receptive to the argument and willing to trust foreign providers.22 Moreover, the trust deficit maps into broader debates on the geoeconomic turn in the governance of digital technologies. As such, it can be conceptual­ ised as a consequence of European fears of being caught between the US– China competition for technological primacy and concerns about the US ‘weaponizing interdependence’ and harnessing its dominance of digital infra­ structures for strategic advantage.23 As the extant literature demonstrated, this predicament has resulted in attempts by the EU to gear its Digital Single Market towards the achievement of geopolitical goals under the banner of the Open Strategic Autonomy agenda.24 Such industrial policy shift has been variously identified as ‘selective fortification’ (Lavery, 2023), ‘geo-dirigiste’ turn (Seidl & Schmitz, 2023), ‘market activism’ (McNamara, 2023), and ‘market shaping’ (Di Carlo & Schmitz, 2023).

A full topology of trust is beyond the scope of this study.25 However, build­ ing from the above, in this study we refer to two dimensions of the trust deficit which can complement and enrich the literature on cloud sovereignty: trust as control and trust as access. Our conceptualisation draws from Michels et al.’s definition of EU cloud sovereignty as a set of policies aimed to shield the EU from threats to the confidentiality and availability of European data. Confidentiality refers to the US government potentially leveraging its extraterritorial laws (e.g., the Cloud Act or FISA section 702) to obtain data stored in the EU, thereby violating the legal sovereignty of Member States. Availability refers to the fact that European over-reliance on foreign technol­ ogy can expose Member States to supply-chain shocks.26 As noted by the authors, threats to confidentiality and availability mirror what Farrell and Newman label respectively the panopticon effect – the ability of a government to obtain information from strategic hubs in information flows like the cloud – and the chokepoint effect – the power of cutting off other states

We start with primary documents published by Amazon Web Services, Microsoft Azure, and Google Cloud that detail how these companies intend to comply with EU sovereignty rules (2021present). These include blog posts, white papers, and in-depth technical documentation that outline hyperscalers’ technology strategy. Technology solutions to political issues. Addressing the trust deficit and building sovereign clouds Microsoft Azure, Google Cloud, and Amazon Web Services have all developed tailored technical and organisational controls to address the trust deficit. Such solutions may vary depending on the specific technology strategy of each company, but they all involve a blend of data localisation, sophisticated access controls, and advanced encryption techniques. To complement their offerings and secure their operations in the European market, hyperscalers have also entered joint ventures with European companies – marketed as sovereign or trusted clouds. In these joint ventures, hyperscalers license their technology to a European company that operates the infrastructure.

An analysis of these offerings confirms that, at its core, the issue of sover­ eignty in the cloud boils down to trust, and that hyperscalers are trying to address the trust deficit mainly through technology.

Technology and organisational solutions Since 2021, hyperscalers have announced various commitments to allow for data localisation, that is the storage of data – including metadata29 – in a specific location defined by European customers, without moving them outside of the region.30 They have also unveiled new cryptographic solutions to ensure greater customer control over their data. To protect data at rest – the state of data when stored in the cloud and not actively in use – hypers­ calers offer client-side encryption, which allows to generate two encryption keys, the first stored by the hyperscaler, the second owned by the customer and placed outside the hyperscaler’s infrastructure, typically with an external key management vendor. Both keys are needed to access the data, hence these cannot be accessed by the cloud provider without the customer’s approval.31 To protect data in use – the state of data when someone is per­ forming computations on them – hyperscalers have been marketing confi­ dential computing (CC), an advanced form of cryptography which creates trusted execution environments within an hardware chip where customers can work on data without decrypting them.32 Both client-side encryption and confidential computing are marketed as technical controls that produce a sovereign outcome. Moreover, such controls seek to address the trust deficit by providing trust as control, ensuring that data cannot be accessed by the cloud provider. The net effect is that the vendor has less power over the data, while the customer gains greater control over it. Hypers­ calers have further tailored their cloud solutions to EU governments with pro­ ducts that aim to ensure not only trust as control, but also trust as access, that is, the assurance that governments can count on the continued availability of hyperscaler services in the face of potential disruptions. Offerings like Micro­ soft’s Cloud for Sovereignty,33 Google’s Assured Workloads,34 and AWS’ Euro­ pean Sovereign Cloud35 are all geared towards this objective. These specialised offerings for public sector customers allow to localise data, encrypt them at all stages, and restrict access solely to European personnel (trust as control). For customers whose data need to be physically close to their premises, hyperscalers allow to extend their services to customerowned data centres or to air gap (disconnect from the internet) highly sensi­ tive data (trust as access) to shield them from network disruptions.

By handing out to states greater control over their data, hyperscalers are seeking to bridge the trust deficit and make themselves compatible with the sovereignty that states seek to regain over cloud infrastructures. Yet the very fact that governments depend on technical solutions provided by

technology vendors to increase their control over data confirms the power asymmetry between those who own the technology (the hyperscalers) and the governments that bargain for more control over it. For example, as it has been noted, cryptography does not eliminate the need to trust the vendors, it only shifts it.36 In this sense, techniques like confidential comput­ ing might effectively increase data protection, but they still need to be implemented by the provider itself, leaving the issue of trust unresolved.

The interviews conducted for the purpose of this study suggest there is no agreement among stakeholders on whether the above-mentioned technical controls can effectively address the trust deficit. Some cybersecurity experts, for example, argue that the ultimate solution to the trust deficit resides in strong encryption, particularly encrypting data while storing keys with a different provider so as to make sure that the cloud provider never sees the content of the data.37 Others echo that technology can address the trust deficit by bypassing borders and national jurisdictions. Confidential computing, for example, can extend trust domains beyond the borders of a nation-state and onto far-flung machines, making their location irrelevant.38 Thus, while a government might not trust the company owning and operat­ ing the cloud infrastructure, it can trust that the technology will keep its data confidential because that operation is isolated from the cloud vendor. The same technology experts however, acknowledge that technology solutions are not without shortcomings. For example, while strong encryption allows to keep data confidential, managing encryption keys can increase complexity for the customers, especially when the keys are stored with an external vendor.39 Moreover, such solutions seem to work when data are first encrypted then uploaded to the cloud, but are not feasible in other cases, such as when working on a shared document in the cloud. With regards to confidential computing, results from our interviews suggest the technology is not commercially diffused as of yet, although participants have shown to be cautiously optimistic about its future implementation at scale.40 Some par­ ticipants highlight that confidential computing is still an evolving research area. As such, first generation systems might not be dependable enough against concerted cyberattacks.41 Others highlight that encrypting data in use is technically feasible, but the market is not mature enough for its implementation at scale.42 Finally, confidential computing applications seem to be subject to vulnerability exploits. For example, the hardware com­ ponents – the chips – that power confidential computing applications have been found to be potentially subject to side-channel attacks, which can allow malicious actors to extract sensitive information like cryptographic keys.43 Some public sector participants posit that such vulnerabilities are proof that technology solutions can increase trust as control and trust as access, but they cannot fully address the trust deficit in the cloud. Joint ventures and trusted clouds Another key part of the hyperscalers’ solutions are joint ventures with local European companies, also marketed as trusted clouds. Such ventures create a novel business entity where the hyperscaler is a minority stakeholder and European partners a majority investor. In all these partnerships, hyperscalers licenses the technology while the infrastructure is generally managed and operated by the local partner. While their contractual specifications may vary, these ventures generally rely on technical and organisational controls such as: building a segregated infrastructure operated independently by the European provider; granting access to vetted European personnel only; and deploying advanced cryptographic controls to ensure data confidentiality and integrity. Trusted clouds result from ‘bottom up’ market processes and represent an important part of hyperscalers’ attempts to comply with stringent data sovereignty rules in the EU.47 In other words, they are another response from the hyperscalers to European pressures for greater access and control over cloud infrastructures. Segregating the infrastruc­ ture, encrypting data at all stages, and granting access to vetted personnel only allows governments to have greater certainty about the confidentiality of their data (trust as control), while localising and storing data in specific data centres is meant to ensure availability (trust as access). Furthermore, trusted clouds are meant to ensure close collaboration between hypersca­ lers’ and European companies’ engineers to facilitate a partial transfer of technology know-how.48 In this sense, hyperscalers seem to acknowledge that technical guarantees alone are unlikely to fully address the trust deficit with European governments, and that a human element of trust is also needed.

The first joint venture dates back to 2021, when French companies Capge­ mini and Orange announced Bleu, a partnership with Microsoft to create a cloud provider compliant with France’s data sovereignty rules.49 Similar part­ nerships now exist between other hyperscalers and local European providers, with Google Cloud being particularly active on this front. As with the above-mentioned technology solutions, it remains unclear whether trusted clouds can fully address the trust deficit. Some EU policy­ makers concede that trusted clouds allow American hyperscalers to legally separate themselves from their parent company, thus avoiding exposure to extra-territorial laws and effectively eliminating the issue of trust.51 From the hyperscalers’ perspective, trusted clouds may render the application of extra-territorial legislations futile. If a European company runs the cloud infrastructure independently from the hyperscaler and has full control over its data, then this creates a ‘full sovereign cloud’ that is effec­ tively ‘impossible to crack’.52 Not everybody, however, seems to agree. Other stakeholders argue that strategic partnerships are a positive sign of the hyperscalers’ willingness to cater to Europe’s strategic concerns, but that they are insufficient to solve any judicial conundrum as segregating the infrastructure and allowing only European personnel to work in it does not overcome the obstacles of the Cloud act and FISA 702.53 Other companies point at the lasting software dependence that strategic partner­ ships may entail and are sceptical about whether sovereign environments can run independently from US technology. The argument is that hypersca­ ler clouds run on software that requires constant updates usually implemented within seconds. Creating a segregated cloud environment would make it difficult for hyperscalers to update their offerings as seam­ lessly as they do under normal circumstances, which could make such sovereign environments less secure.54 Hyperscalers too seem to recognise the limits of trusted clouds. Specifi­ cally, they highlight that these can be an optimal solution to address specific customer needs (e.g., ministries of defence and intelligence agencies), but they are unlikely to address the trust deficit in all possible scen­ arios since there is no single model for sovereignty among European states. Technology alone cannot address the economic dimension of the trust deficit Overall, EU policymakers recognise that the technical controls developed by hyperscalers to increase trust as access and trust as control should be wel­ comed as a positive development.67 However, both public and private sector stakeholders point out that technology alone is unlikely to replace trust. This is because the trust deficit has a both a security and economic dimension. The first relates to privacy concerns about the confidentiality of European data, especially public sector data. The second relates to the lack of competitive European alternatives to the hyperscalers. While techni­ cal solutions can effectively address the former, they do little to offset the latter. In this regard, EU policymakers argue that not even regulatory instru­ ments such as standards and certifications can fully replace trust. One par­ ticipant from an EU institution illustrated this point by drawing a parallel with Huawei and 5G networks. As part of its strategy to be perceived as a trustworthy vendor in the European market, Huawei has obtained many internationally recognised cybersecurity certifications.68 Yet the company still faces risks of being banned from the European market on the grounds of being a Chinese company (Espinoza, 2023). On the contrary, trust is the indispensable social and political pre-condition for bargaining with a cloud vendor. Stan­ dards and certifications can only enhance trust, they cannot precede it. Absent pre-existing trust between a government and a cloud vendor, there is only so much that a regulatory instrument like a certification can achieve. Impact of sovereignty mandates on the digital economy and cybersecurity A final insight on the relation between technology, trust, and sovereignty in the cloud regards the impact of data sovereignty rules on cybersecurity and the digital economy. Much of the academic literature posits that data sover­ eignty policies stemming from the trust deficit negatively impact the EU cybersecurity and digital economy. According to this view, hard localisation rules increase complexity for organisations, prevent access to best in class cybersecurity (which largely comes from American companies), and hinder information sharing for incident response.72 It is also suggested that data localisation hurts the digital economy by increasing costs for companies to store and process data and making them less ‘data intensive’.73 In other words, the consensus points to a zero-sum relationship between sovereignty on the one hand, and data security and economic prosperity on the other, where an advancement of the former leads to a retrenchment of the latter. While such arguments are notionally sensitive, empirical findings suggest they should not be taken at face value. On the contrary, they can be nuanced to better reflect the reality of the European policy debate on cloud sovereignty and the trust deficit. Criticism towards sovereignty

Conclusions This article has argued that understanding the political and economic dimen­ sions of trust is crucial to appreciate the sovereign turn in the governance of cloud computing. Scholars have mostly focused on the rise of digital sover­ eignty to analyse governments’ attempts to increase access and control over digital infrastructures like the cloud. Yet a mere focus on sovereignty does not explain how such attempts unfold in practice. In the age of transna­ tional digital technologies, governments must often rely on a handful of service providers whose technology they do not fully trust. This predicament begets profound strategic dilemmas. We have termed this the trust deficit. Focusing on the European Union, we have articulated the trust deficit in the cloud computing sector as essentially a matter of trust as control and trust as access. The former refers to guarantees regarding the confidentiality of European data, the latter to guarantees about the availability of hypersca­ ler computing in the face of economic and geopolitical disruptions.

Our study makes novel contributions on the political economy of cloud computing, as well as on the issue of trust in the governance of digital tech­ nologies and digital sovereignty more broadly. We have shown that, to address the trust deficit, hyperscalers have put forward their own version of sovereignty, one based on technology. This narrow understanding of sovereignty is different from the broader strategic considerations that gov­ ernments grapple with when bargaining for more access and control over cloud infrastructures. Such wedge in understanding entails that technology solutions cannot fully address the trust deficit as intended by governments. Specifically, while technology can address the technical portion of the trust deficit, it cannot address its economic dimension, which relates the uneven distribution of scalable computing power between the US and EU. Finally, contrary to what suggested by the academic and policy literature so far, we find that there is not enough evidence to suggest that data sovereignty rules born out of the trust deficit are incompatible with strong cybersecurity and the digital economy more broadly.

Ultimately, this paper has put forward the argument that sovereignty debates in the governance of digital technologies are, at their core, a
